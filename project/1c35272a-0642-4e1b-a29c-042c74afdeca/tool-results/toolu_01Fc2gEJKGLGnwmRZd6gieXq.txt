29:\begin{abstract}
30:We reformulate the sBootstrap in terms of signed mass charges 
31:$\zch = z_0 + z_i$ with $m = \zch^2$, and show that the Koide ratio 
32:$Q = 2/3$ is the algebraic consequence of two conditions: 
33:$\sum z_i = 0$ and $\langle z_i^2\rangle = z_0^2$ (perturbation 
34:energies average to the base-scale energy). Allowing negative charges, 
35:we find that pseudoscalar mesons satisfy a Koide triple 
36:$(-\pi, D_s, B)$ to 0.1\%, and identify \emph{seed triples} with 
37:one massless member---$(0, s, c)$ for quarks, $(0, \pi, D_s)$ for 
38:mesons---as the pre-breaking configurations from which the full 
39:spectrum emerges. These seed triples share the same analytical 
40:structure (charge ratio $2-\sqrt{3}$) and, strikingly, the meson 
41:seed $(0,\pi,D_s)$ has Koide scale $v_0^2 \approx 351\MeV$,
42:close to the lepton triple's $v_0^2 \approx 314\MeV$. A third 
43:tuple $(c,b,t)$ stands apart, having no meson counterpart because 
44:top diquarks do not hadronize. We discuss the diquark sector---which 
45:fills the symmetric $\mathbf{15} + \overline{\mathbf{15}}$ of $SU(5)$ 
46:flavor, in conflict with Pauli statistics for point-like diquarks, 
47:proving that the diquark cannot be a $qq$ composite---and outline 
48:how SUSY breaking proceeds through two mechanisms---F-term (chiral
49:condensate, with Volkov--Akulov as its low-energy realization) and
50:D-term (electromagnetic Fayet--Iliopoulos)---plus a separate
51:heavy-sector origin for the $(c,b,t)$ triple. The O'Raifeartaigh
52:superpotential produces the Koide seed spectrum exactly when
53:$gv/m = \sqrt{3}$, reducing the energy-balance condition to a
54:single constraint on the couplings. The bloom from seed to full
55:triple doubles the vacuum charge, yielding
56:$\sqrt{m_b} = 3\sqrt{m_s} + \sqrt{m_c}$ (matching PDG to
57:$0.1\sigma$), and all quark Koide triples obligatorily mix
58:up-type and down-type mass eigenstates.
59:We construct the explicit $\mathcal{N}=1$ Lagrangian---K\"ahler
60:potential, superpotential (Seiberg constraint plus Yukawa),
61:$D$-terms, and soft breaking from a spurion with
62:$\langle F\rangle = f_\pi^2$---and show that the seed Koide
63:fixes the pseudo-modulus VEV ($\langle X\rangle = \sqrt{3}\,\mu$) while the
64:$v_0$-doubling originates in the K\"ahler sector via a bion
65:correction $|\!\sum s_k\sqrt{\hat m_k}|^2$ from
66:monopole-instantons on $\mathbb{R}^3\times S^1$; a
67:three-instanton superpotential $(\det M)^3/\Lambda^{18}$
68:generates a $\cos(9\delta)$ potential whose minima include the
69:observed lepton Koide phase to $5\,\text{ppm}$.
70:After these constraints, only two to three
71:free parameters remain ($m_u$, $m_d$, $m_s$), with $m_u$ and
72:$m_d$ connected to the Cabibbo angle via the Oakes relation.
73:The $SO(32)$ adjoint accommodates the sBootstrap representations;
74:$E_8 \times E_8$ is blocked because $E_8$ decompositions produce
75:only antisymmetric $\mathbf{10}$, never the symmetric $\mathbf{15}$
76:required for diquarks. A Diophantine system on
77:the scalar content uniquely selects $N=3$ generations.
78:\end{abstract}

83:\section{Introduction}
84:\label{sec:intro}
85:%======================================================================
86:
87:The sBootstrap \cite{Rivero2005,Rivero2012} proposes that the Standard 
88:Model's pseudoscalar mesons and leptons fill common $\mathcal{N}=1$ 
89:supermultiplets. Its foundational relation is
90:\begin{equation}
91:\label{eq:pimu}
92:m_\pi^2 - m_\mu^2 \approx f_\pi^2\,,
93:\end{equation}
94:identifying the pion decay constant $f_\pi \approx 92.2\MeV$ as the 
95:SUSY-breaking order parameter. Numerically, $m_\pi^2 - m_\mu^2 = 
96:8{,}316\MeV^2$ versus $f_\pi^2 = 8{,}501\MeV^2$, a $2.2\%$ 
97:discrepancy---comparable to the precision of the quark Koide triples 
98:discussed below. In this paper we develop two themes 
99:that change the structure of the program.
100:
101:First, \textbf{mass is the square of a signed charge}: $m = \zch^2$ 
102:where $\zch = z_0 + z_i$ can be negative. The Koide ratio $Q = 2/3$ 
103:is then not numerology but the energy-balance condition 
104:$\langle z_i^2\rangle = z_0^2$. With signed charges, pseudoscalar 
105:mesons satisfy Koide, and the spectrum organizes into \emph{seed 
106:triples} with a massless member that grow into full triples upon 
107:symmetry breaking.
108:
109:Second, \textbf{the full sBootstrap has three Koide tuples, not one}, 
110:and the breaking structure must generate all three: a light sector 
111:$(0,s,c) \to (-s,c,b)$ seen in both quarks and mesons, a lepton 
112:sector $(e\!\approx\!0, \mu, \tau)$, and a heavy sector $(c,b,t)$ 
113:that decouples from meson physics because the top quark does not 
114:hadronize. The diquark partners of quarks, needed to complete the 
115:SUSY multiplets, fill the symmetric $\mathbf{15} + \overline{\mathbf{15}}$ 
116:of $SU(5)$ flavor---the representation produced naturally by the 
117:SUSY generator and by the $SO(32)$ decomposition. A kinematic 
118:theorem shows that no $J=0$, color-$\bar{3}$ diquark can have 
119:symmetric flavor---regardless of orbital angular momentum---so the 
120:sBootstrap diquark cannot be a $qq$ composite but must be an
121:algebraically or string-theoretically generated object.
122:
123:Third, \textbf{the Koide condition constrains the superpotential}.
124:The O'Raifeartaigh model---the simplest realization of $F$-term
125:SUSY breaking with three chiral superfields---produces a fermionic
126:mass spectrum that is an exact Koide seed triple when the ratio of
127:the cubic coupling to the bilinear mass parameter satisfies
128:$gv/m = \sqrt{3}$. This single algebraic constraint is the
129:microscopic content of the energy-balance condition. Separately,
130:a Diophantine system on the scalar content ($rs = 2N$,
131:$r(r+1)/2 = 2N$, $r^2+s^2-1=4N$) uniquely selects $N=3$
132:generations, and a Casimir eigenvalue equation predicts
133:$\sin^2\theta_W = 0.22310$ to $0.4\sigma$.
134:
135:\paragraph{New results in this paper.}
136:Compared to the prior publication \cite{Rivero2005}, the
137:following are first reported here: the signed-charge formalism
138:and the meson Koide triple $(-\pi, D_s, B)$; the seed-triple
139:concept and the universal ratio $2 - \sqrt{3}$; the
140:$v_0$-doubling relation $\sqrt{m_b} = 3\sqrt{m_s} + \sqrt{m_c}$;
141:the O'Raifeartaigh derivation $gv/m = \sqrt{3}$; the Pauli theorem
142:for symmetric diquarks; the bloom instability under perturbative
143:$R$-breaking; the dual Koide on $(d,s,b)$ and its connection to
144:the Seiberg seesaw; the bion K\"ahler mechanism for the
145:$v_0$-doubling; the self-consistent prediction chain
146:$m_s \to m_c \to m_b \to m_t$; and the scale relation
147:$M_0 \approx \fpi^2/27$ tying the lepton Koide scale
148:to the pion decay constant. The Casimir eigenvalue equation and the $SO(32)$
149:decomposition appeared in our prior work; here we add the
150:algebraic proof linking the Casimir ratio to the de~Vries form,
151:a uniqueness analysis of the equation, the
152:$N_f$-phase structure of the SQCD realization, and the
153:three-instanton origin of the $\cos(9\delta)$ potential
154:that selects the lepton Koide phase.

157:\section{The Koide Formula as Energy Balance}
158:\label{sec:koide_algebra}
159:%======================================================================
160:
161:\subsection{Charges, not masses}
162:
163:The fundamental variable is the signed mass charge $\zch = z_0 + z_i 
164:\in \mathbb{R}$, with the physical mass $m = \zch^2 \geq 0$.
165:
166:\subsection{Derivation of $Q = 2/3$}
167:
168:For three charges $\zch_k = z_0 + z_k$ with $z_0$ the mean 
169:($\sum z_k = 0$):
170:\begin{equation}
171:Q = \frac{\sum \zch_k^2}{(\sum \zch_k)^2} 
172:= \frac{3z_0^2 + \sum z_k^2}{9z_0^2}
173:= \frac{1}{3} + \frac{\langle z_k^2\rangle}{3z_0^2}\,.
174:\end{equation}
175:Therefore:
176:\begin{equation}
177:\boxed{Q = \frac{2}{3} \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad 
178:\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2\,.}
179:\end{equation}
180:
181:The two conditions are:
182:\begin{enumerate}
183:\item \textbf{Zero-sum perturbations:} $z_1 + z_2 + z_3 = 0$ 
184:(definition of $z_0$ as the mean charge).
185:\item \textbf{Energy balance:} The mean energy of the perturbations 
186:equals the energy of the unperturbed charge: 
187:$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$.
188:\end{enumerate}
189:
190:Condition~2 is the physics. It says the splitting scale matches the
191:base scale---the coefficient of variation equals unity:
192:$\sigma(\zch)/\bar{\zch} = 1$.
193:(Dimensionally, $\zch$ has units $\MeV^{1/2}$, so
194:$z_0^2$ has units MeV---a mass scale, not an energy density.
195:The ``energy balance'' terminology is metaphorical.) This is neither perturbative 
196:($\sigma \ll \bar{\zch}$, giving $Q \to 1/3$, all equal) nor 
197:dominant ($\sigma \gg \bar{\zch}$, giving $Q \to 1$, one mass 
198:dominates). The Koide value $2/3$ is the equipartition point.
199:
200:\subsection{Why one mass can vanish}
201:
202:If $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$, a perturbation $z_k$ can reach 
203:$|z_k| \sim z_0$, making $\zch_k = z_0 + z_k \approx 0$. A vanishing 
204:mass is the \emph{natural extreme} of energy balance, not fine-tuning.
205:
206:%======================================================================
207:\section{Seed Triples: The $(0, a, b)$ Structure}
208:\label{sec:seeds}
209:%======================================================================
210:
211:\subsection{Analytical condition}
212:
213:For a triple $(0, \zch_a, \zch_b)$ with one massless member, the 
214:Koide ratio becomes:
215:\begin{equation}
216:Q = \frac{\zch_a^2 + \zch_b^2}{(\zch_a + \zch_b)^2}\,.
217:\end{equation}
218:Setting $Q = 2/3$ and writing $r = \zch_a/\zch_b$ (with $r < 1$):
219:\begin{equation}
220:3(r^2 + 1) = 2(r + 1)^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad r^2 - 4r + 1 = 0\,,
221:\end{equation}
222:giving:
223:\begin{equation}
224:\boxed{r = 2 - \sqrt{3} \approx 0.26795\,.}
225:\end{equation}
226:
227:Equivalently, in mass space: 
228:$m_{\text{light}}/m_{\text{heavy}} = (2 - \sqrt{3})^2 = 7 - 4\sqrt{3} 
229:\approx 0.07180$.
230:
231:This is a \emph{universal} prediction: any Koide triple with one 
232:massless member has the same charge ratio between the two massive 
233:members, regardless of their absolute scale.
234:
235:\subsection{The quark seed: $(0, s, c)$}
236:
237:The strange and charm quark charges give:
238:\begin{equation}
239:\frac{\sqrt{m_s}}{\sqrt{m_c}} = \frac{9.67}{35.64} = 0.2712\,,
240:\qquad r_{\text{Koide}} = 0.2679\,, \qquad \text{deviation: } 1.2\%\,.
241:\end{equation}
242:
243:This triple has $Q = 0.6644$, deviating from $2/3$ by only $0.35\%$.
244:Its Koide scale is $v_0^2 = (\zch_s + \zch_c)^2/9 \approx 228\MeV$.
245:
246:\paragraph{Scale ambiguity in the quark seed.}
247:Throughout this paper, quark masses follow PDG conventions:
248:$m_s(2\GeV) = 93.4\MeV$, $m_c(m_c) = 1.27\GeV$,
249:$m_b(m_b) = 4.18\GeV$, $m_t = 172.76\GeV$ (pole mass). This is a
250:mixed convention, with the light quark evaluated at 2~GeV and heavier
251:quarks at their own scale. QCD mass ratios are RG invariants at
252:leading order (the anomalous dimension $\gamma_m$ is flavor-universal),
253:so $Q$ is scheme-independent. However, the seed ratio
254:$\sqrt{m_s}/\sqrt{m_c}$ uses masses at \emph{different} scales:
255:at a common $\overline{\text{MS}}$ scale the ratio shifts to
256:$\sim 0.29$, worsening the seed match from 1.2\% to $\sim 8\%$.
257:This is a significant limitation of the quark seed claim. The meson
258:seed, which uses unambiguous physical (pole) masses, is free of this
259:scheme issue and provides the cleaner test of the seed hypothesis.
260:
261:A historical note: Harari, Haut, and Weyers \cite{Harari} observed 
262:a quark mass ratio pattern that, in the language of the present paper, 
263:corresponds to an approximate seed triple---$(0, d, s)$---at a time 
264:when the up quark mass was 
265:still thought to be possibly zero. With current masses, 
266:$\sqrt{m_d}/\sqrt{m_s} = 0.224$, deviating from $2 - \sqrt{3}$ by 
267:$16\%$---too large for a good Koide triple. But if $m_s$ were 
268:$\sim 150\MeV$ and $m_d \sim 7\MeV$ (values common in that era), the 
269:ratio improves considerably. The seed idea was already present, ahead 
270:of its time.
271:
272:\subsection{The meson seed: $(0, \pi, D_s)$}
273:
274:The pion and $D_s$ meson charges give:
275:\begin{equation}
276:\frac{\sqrt{m_\pi}}{\sqrt{m_{D_s}}} = \frac{11.81}{44.37} = 0.2663\,, 
277:\qquad r_{\text{Koide}} = 0.2679\,, \qquad \text{deviation: } 0.6\%\,.
278:\end{equation}
279:
280:This gives $Q = 0.6679$, deviating by $0.18\%$. It is the 
281:\textbf{meson counterpart} of the quark seed $(0, s, c)$---both 
282:involve the strange-charm sector. Indeed, $D_s = c\bar{s}$ is the 
283:meson that directly contains the $s$ and $c$ quarks.
284:
285:\subsection{The lepton triple as an ``almost-seed''}
286:
287:The charged lepton triple $(e, \mu, \tau)$ has $\zch_e = 0.715\MeV^{1/2}$, 
288:which is small but nonzero. If $m_e$ were zero, we would need 
289:$\sqrt{m_\mu}/\sqrt{m_\tau} = 2 - \sqrt{3} = 0.2679$; the actual value 
290:is $0.2439$, deviating by $9\%$. So the leptons are \emph{not} an 
291:exact seed---the electron, while very light, is not massless. But the 
292:triple is clearly the same family: the near-critical Koide phase puts 
293:one member close to zero.
294:
295:\subsection{Scale coincidence}
296:
297:The $v_0^2$ values of the seed triples cluster remarkably:
298:
299:\begin{center}
300:\begin{tabular}{lcc}
301:\toprule
302:Triple & $v_0^2$ (MeV) & $v_0$ ($\MeV^{1/2}$) \\
303:\midrule
304:$(e, \mu, \tau)$ & 314 & 17.72 \\
305:$(0, \pi, D_s)$ & 351 & 18.73 \\
306:$(0, s, c)$ & 228 & 15.10 \\
307:\bottomrule
308:\end{tabular}
309:\end{center}
310:
311:The lepton and meson seed triples share a Koide scale around
312:310--350~MeV, squarely in the QCD scale range. This is the
313:sBootstrap connection made quantitative: \textbf{the lepton triple
314:and the meson seed triple operate at the same scale.}
315:
316:For the lepton triple, the coincidence is numerically sharp:
317:\begin{equation}
318:M_0 \;\approx\; \frac{\fpi^2}{27}\,,
319:\end{equation}
320:to $0.3\%$: the right-hand side gives $314.9\MeV$ versus
321:the fitted $M_0 = 313.8\MeV$.  Equivalently,
322:$v_0 \approx \fpi/(3\sqrt{3})$.  If this identification is exact
323:and $\delta_0 \bmod 2\pi/3 = 2/9$ (the three-instanton value),
324:all three charged lepton masses follow from~$\fpi$ alone,
325:each predicted to~${\sim}0.3\%$.

328:\section{A Tale of Two Tuples}
329:\label{sec:two_tuples}
330:%======================================================================
331:
332:\subsection{From seed to full triple}
333:
334:The seed triples grow into full triples upon symmetry breaking:
335:
336:\begin{center}
337:\begin{tabular}{lccc}
338:\toprule
339:Sector & Seed triple & Full triple & $v_0^2$ (full) \\
340:\midrule
341:Quarks & $(0, s, c)$ & $(-s, c, b)$ & 913 MeV \\
342:Mesons & $(0, \pi, D_s)$ & $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ & 1230 MeV \\
343:Leptons & $(0 \approx e, \mu, \tau)$ & $(e, \mu, \tau)$ & 314 MeV \\
344:\bottomrule
345:\end{tabular}
346:\end{center}
347:
348:In each case:
349:\begin{itemize}
350:\item The seed has a massless (or near-massless) member, two massive 
351:members in the ratio $2 - \sqrt{3}$, and $Q \approx 2/3$.
352:\item The full triple has \textbf{no} massless member: the zero has 
353:been lifted (to $m_e$, or the sign-flipped strange/pion mass). The 
354:$b$ quark and $B$ meson are always present as flavor degrees of 
355:freedom; what the breaking does is give them their \emph{distinctive 
356:masses}---the specific values that complete the Koide triple. Before 
357:breaking, the $b$-direction sits at the flat direction ($\zch = 0$); 
358:after, it acquires $\zch_b = 64.65\MeV^{1/2}$, the value selected 
359:by the energy balance with $c$ and the sign-flipped $s$. The bloom 
360:is not particle creation but \emph{mass generation}.
361:\end{itemize}
362:
363:The ``tale of two tuples'' is this: \textbf{each sector presents
364:first as a seed with a massless member, then ``blooms'' into a
365:full triple when SUSY breaks.} The massless member is the necessary
366:initial condition; the breaking lifts it and generates the full spectrum.
367:
368:\paragraph{The vacuum-charge doubling (new).}
369:The vacuum charge $v_0 = \tfrac{1}{3}\sum\zch_k$ of the quark seed
370:is $v_0^{(s)} = (\sqrt{m_s} + \sqrt{m_c})/3 = 15.10\MeV^{1/2}$.
371:For the full triple, $v_0^{(f)} = (-\sqrt{m_s} + \sqrt{m_c} +
372:\sqrt{m_b})/3 = 30.21\MeV^{1/2}$. Their ratio is
373:\begin{equation*}
374:  v_0^{(f)}/v_0^{(s)} = 2.0005 \approx 2\,.
375:\end{equation*}
376:If the bloom exactly doubles the vacuum charge, then
377:$\sqrt{m_b} = 3\sqrt{m_s} + \sqrt{m_c}$, giving
378:$m_b = 4{,}177\MeV$---matching the PDG value
379:$4{,}180 \pm 30\MeV$ to $0.1\sigma$ ($0.06\sigma$ including
380:propagated input uncertainties; see \S\ref{subsec:fterm}). This relation is
381:independent of the Koide condition: it constrains $m_b$ from
382:$(m_s, m_c)$ alone, and is more precise (0.07\%) than the
383:overlapping-triples prediction (0.5\%).  A tension exists, however:
384:the exact seed Koide gives $m_c = 1{,}301\MeV$ (not the PDG
385:$1{,}270$), and applying the $v_0$-doubling to this exact seed
386:value gives $m_b = 4{,}233\MeV$ ($1.8\sigma$ from PDG). The
387:$v_0$-doubling works \emph{with the physical} $m_c$, not the exact
388:seed value---so these two constraints are mutually inconsistent at the
389:stated precision. The resolution may be that the seed Koide fixes the
390:ratio $m_c/m_s$ only approximately (the 2.4\% charm deviation being a
391:higher-order correction to the $gv/m = \sqrt{3}$ condition), while the
392:$v_0$-doubling constrains $m_b$ directly from the physical masses. In
393:this reading, the seed condition is the approximate one and the
394:$v_0$-doubling the more fundamental. This hierarchy is not derived;
395:it is imposed by the data.
396:

418:The Koide phases $\delta$ cluster in the range $2.2$--$2.8$~rad
419:($126^\circ$--$161^\circ$), all displaced from the seed value

423:modulo $2\pi/3$).

427:the ``zero'' while being nonzero enough to make $Q$ extremely

430:that does not exactly preserve $Q$. The $v_0$-doubling constrains

432:\sqrt{m_c}$), so the 1.24\% Koide deviation is the residual after
433:the more fundamental $v_0$-constraint is satisfied.

435:\subsection{Orthogonality of seed and full triples}
436:
437:Each Koide triple defines a perturbation vector 
438:$\vec{z} = (z_1, z_2, z_3)$ constrained to the plane $\sum z_k = 0$ 
439:with length $|\vec{z}|^2 = 3z_0^2$. The angle between seed and 
440:full triples measures how much the breaking rotates the spectrum 
441:in charge space:
442:
443:\begin{center}
444:\begin{tabular}{lc}
445:\toprule
446:Pair of triples & Angle \\
447:\midrule
448:$(0,s,c)$ vs.\ $(-s,c,b)$ & $22^\circ$ \\
449:$(0,\pi,D_s)$ vs.\ $(-\pi,D_s,B)$ & $26^\circ$ \\
450:$(e,\mu,\tau)$ vs.\ $(0,\mu,\tau)$ & $0.8^\circ$ \\
451:$(0,s,c)$ vs.\ $(0,\pi,D_s)$ & $0.3^\circ$ \\
452:\bottomrule
453:\end{tabular}
454:\end{center}
455:
456:Two results are notable. First, the quark seed $(0,s,c)$ and meson 
457:seed $(0,\pi,D_s)$ are \textbf{nearly parallel} ($0.3^\circ$): the 
458:meson seed is the composite image of the quark seed, with the GOR 
459:map preserving direction while changing scale. Second, the lepton 
460:triple is essentially its own seed ($0.8^\circ$)---the bloom has 
461:barely begun.

465:\section{The Meson Koide Triple: $(-\pi, D_s, B)$}
466:\label{sec:mesonkoide}
467:%======================================================================
468:
469:With the pion charge negative, the triple $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ satisfies:
470:\begin{equation}
471:Q(-\pi, D_s, B) = 
472:\frac{m_\pi + m_{D_s} + m_B}{(-\sqrt{m_\pi} + \sqrt{m_{D_s}} + \sqrt{m_B})^2}
473:= 0.6674\,,
474:\end{equation}
475:deviating from $2/3$ by $0.10\%$---the second most precise Koide 
476:triple known. The energy balance:
477:\begin{equation}
478:\frac{\langle z_k^2\rangle}{z_0^2} = 1.002\,.
479:\end{equation}
480:
481:For comparison, the ``standard'' all-positive quark triples $(d,s,b)$ 
482:and $(u,c,t)$ deviate from $Q = 2/3$ by 9.7\% and 27\% respectively 
483:at $\overline{\text{MS}}$ masses. \textbf{The meson triple is more
484:precise than any quark triple}, including $(c,b,t)$ (0.42\%).
485:
486:The pion's negative charge has physical meaning: as a pseudo-Goldstone
487:boson, the pion would be massless ($\zch = 0$) if chiral symmetry were
488:exact. It sits just below zero ($\zch_\pi = -11.81$), approaching zero
489:from the negative side as $m_q \to 0$. The sign of $\zch$ encodes
490:Goldstone nature: pseudo-Goldstone bosons sit on the negative side
491:of the critical point, having crossed through zero from positive
492:territory during chiral symmetry breaking.
493:
494:\paragraph{Look-elsewhere correction.}
495:Eleven pseudoscalar mesons ($\pi$, $K$, $\eta$, $\eta'$, $D$,
496:$D_s$, $B$, $B_s$, $B_c$, $\eta_c$, $\eta_b$) yield
497:$\binom{11}{3} = 165$ triples, times
498:four independent sign patterns, giving 660 combinations.
499:Scanning all 660, only $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ and the closely related
500:$(-\pi, D_s, B_s)$ (deviation $0.14\%$) lie within $0.2\%$ of
501:$Q = 2/3$. A Monte Carlo with $10^7$ random triples drawn
502:from $[100,10{,}000]\MeV$ gives $p_{\text{single}} = 2.1 \times 10^{-4}$
503:for a match at $0.10\%$; correcting for 660 trials yields
504:$p_{\text{LEE}} = 0.13$ ($1.1\sigma$). The meson Koide triple
505:is therefore \emph{not} statistically significant on its own,
506:unlike the lepton triple ($2.8\sigma$ after LEE). Its interest
507:lies in the structural pattern: all six closest hits share the
508:sign assignment $(-\pi, D_{(s)}, B_{(s,c)})$, i.e.\ a negative
509:pion paired with charm-containing and bottom-containing mesons---the
510:combination predicted by the sBootstrap's $SU(5)$ flavor structure.

512:%======================================================================
513:\section{The Third Tuple: $(c, b, t)$}
514:\label{sec:cbt}
515:%======================================================================
516:
517:\subsection{A tuple without mesons}
518:
519:The triple $(c, b, t)$ with all positive signs gives $Q = 0.6695$, 
520:deviating by $0.42\%$. Its Koide scale $v_0^2 \approx 29.6\GeV$ 
521:is far above the QCD scale.
522:
523:This triple has \textbf{no meson counterpart}: the top quark decays 
524:weakly ($t \to Wb$, lifetime $\sim 5 \times 10^{-25}$~s) before the 
525:strong interaction can bind it ($\tau_{\text{QCD}} \sim 1/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}} 
526:\sim 10^{-24}$~s). No $T$-meson ($t\bar{q}$) exists. The meson matrix 
527:$M^i{}_j$ extends only to $b$-flavored states; the top sector lives 
528:purely at the quark level.
529:
530:\subsection{Diquarks and the top}
531:
532:In the sBootstrap, diquarks $[q_i q_j]$ are the scalar partners of 
533:antiquarks $\bar{q}_k$, with the baryon acting as the SUSY generator 
534:\cite{CattoGursey, Miyazawa}. For five active flavors 
535:$(u, d, s, c, b)$, the diquarks form a representation of $SU(5)_f$.
536:
537:The top diquarks $[tq]$ are kinematic objects only---they do not 
538:hadronize. Yet their masses should follow the same pattern as other 
539:diquarks: \textbf{there is no dynamical reason for top diquark masses 
540:to differ from the others}. The $(c,b,t)$ Koide triple governs the 
541:quark masses that enter the diquark sector, and its energy balance 
542:$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ constrains the possible diquark 
543:spectrum even though the top diquarks are never observed as bound 
544:states.
545:
546:\subsection{The $(c,b,t)$ triple has no seed}
547:
548:Unlike the $(0,s,c)$ and $(0,\pi,D_s)$ seeds, there is no obvious 
549:$(0, x, y)$ seed for the $(c,b,t)$ triple. No pair of quarks in this 
550:sector satisfies $\sqrt{m_{\text{light}}}/\sqrt{m_{\text{heavy}}} 
551:\approx 2 - \sqrt{3}$: one has $\sqrt{m_c}/\sqrt{m_t} = 0.086$ 
552:(too small) and $\sqrt{m_c}/\sqrt{m_b} = 0.551$ (too large).
553:
554:This means $(c,b,t)$ is \emph{not} a ``bloomed seed'' in the same 
555:sense as $(-s,c,b)$. It may represent a fundamentally different 
556:structure---a triple that was never seeded by a massless member but 
557:instead emerged fully formed at a higher breaking scale, perhaps 
558:connected to electroweak symmetry breaking rather than to chiral 
559:symmetry breaking.

562:\section{Mass Relations and Iterative Chains}
563:\label{sec:mass_chains}
564:%======================================================================
565:
566:The preceding sections established the Koide energy-balance condition
567:$Q = 2/3$ and identified the triples that satisfy it. In this section
568:we quantify how special the lepton triple really is, introduce the
569:angular parametrization that reveals a hidden rational structure in
570:the Koide phase, and trace an iterative chain that connects the heavy
571:quarks to the strange sector. We report both successes and failures:
572:the scaling rule that generates the quark triple from the lepton
573:triple works once and then stops, and the iterative chain stalls at
574:a mass too light for any known fermion. These negative results
575:constrain the scope of the pattern.
576:
577:\subsection{The lepton Koide formula: how special?}
578:Using PDG~2024 values $m_e = 0.51100\MeV$, $m_\mu = 105.658\MeV$,
579:$m_\tau = 1776.86 \pm 0.12\MeV$:
580:\begin{equation}
581:Q(e,\mu,\tau) = \frac{(\sqrt{m_e} + \sqrt{m_\mu} + \sqrt{m_\tau})^2}
582:{m_e + m_\mu + m_\tau} = 1.500014 \pm 0.000015\,,
583:\end{equation}
584:where $Q = (\sum\sqrt{m})^2 / \sum m$ is the reciprocal of the
585:convention in \S\ref{sec:koide_algebra}: the Koide condition is
586:$Q = 3/2$ here, equivalently $\sum m / (\sum\sqrt{m})^2 = 2/3$
587:there. We use the $3/2$ convention in this section to match
588:the most common form in the Koide literature.
589:The deviation from $3/2$ is $|Q - 3/2| = 1.38 \times 10^{-5}$,
590:corresponding to $0.91\sigma$ given the uncertainty dominated by
591:$\delta m_\tau$.
592:
593:\paragraph{Look-elsewhere effect.}
594:The Standard Model contains 9~charged fermions (3~leptons, 6~quarks).
595:An exhaustive scan of all $\binom{9}{3} = 84$ mass triplets yields
596:the ranking in Table~\ref{tab:koide_scan}.

619:unlikely to be a pure accident, but the evidence does not reach the

622:\subsection{The Koide angle parametrization}
623:Any triple satisfying $Q = 3/2$ exactly can be written
624:\cite{Koide1983}
625:\begin{equation}
626:\label{eq:koide_angle}
627:\sqrt{m_k} = \sqrt{M_0}\left(1 + \sqrt{2}\cos\!\left(
628:\frac{2\pi k}{3} + \delta_0\right)\right), \qquad k = 0,1,2\,,
629:\end{equation}
630:where $M_0 > 0$ is the scale parameter and $\delta_0 \in [0, 2\pi)$
631:is the Koide phase. This is not an approximation: any three
632:non-negative numbers whose $Q$-ratio equals $3/2$ are exactly
633:parametrized by some $(M_0, \delta_0)$.
634:
635:Fitting to the charged lepton masses:
636:\begin{equation}
637:M_0 = 313.84\MeV\,, \qquad \delta_0 = 2.3166\;\text{rad}\,.
638:\end{equation}
639:The scale $M_0 \approx 314\MeV$ is the Koide scale $v_0^2$ of
640:\S\ref{sec:seeds}, now identified as the mass parameter of the
641:angular decomposition.
642:
643:\paragraph{The phase is close to $2/9$.}
644:Unlike $Q = 2/3$, which is the algebraic consequence of the
645:energy-balance condition (\S\ref{sec:koide_algebra}), the phase
646:$\delta_0$ is a free parameter that the energy balance does
647:not fix. Its value is an empirical datum, not a derived quantity.
648:The parametrization~\eqref{eq:koide_angle} has a $2\pi/3$ periodicity
649:in $\delta_0$ (permuting the three masses). The physically
650:meaningful quantity is the reduced phase
651:\begin{equation}
652:\delta_0 \bmod \frac{2\pi}{3} = 0.22222\;\text{rad}\,.
653:\end{equation}
654:This is strikingly close to the fraction $2/9 = 0.22222\ldots$:
655:\begin{equation}
656:\delta_0 \bmod \frac{2\pi}{3} - \frac{2}{9} = -1.1 \times 10^{-6}\,,
657:\end{equation}
658:a residual of $\mathbf{5}$~\textbf{ppm} relative to $2/9$.
659:
660:\paragraph{Statistical significance of the rational approximation.}
661:There are 128~``nice'' fractions $p/q$ with $q \leq 20$ and
662:$0 < p/q < 2\pi/3$. The probability that a random phase (uniform
663:on $[0, 2\pi/3)$) falls within $1.1 \times 10^{-6}$ of \emph{any}
664:of these 128 fractions gives a look-elsewhere-corrected significance
665:of $\mathbf{3.6\sigma}$. Evaluated specifically against the fraction
666:$2/9$ alone (i.e., without look-elsewhere correction), the
667:significance is $\mathbf{4.7\sigma}$. The $2/9$ proximity is the
668:strongest single numerical coincidence in the Koide system.

670:\subsection{The scaling rule: $M_1 = 3M_0$, $\delta_1 = 3\delta_0$}

675:\begin{equation}

677:\delta_1 = 3\,\delta_0 = 6.9497\;\text{rad}\,,
678:\end{equation}
679:inserted into~\eqref{eq:koide_angle}, predicts a quark triple.

727:\subsection{The second scaling fails}
728:Iterating the scaling rule:
729:\begin{equation}
730:M_2 = 9\,M_0 = 2825\MeV\,, \qquad
731:\delta_2 = 9\,\delta_0 = 20.849\;\text{rad}\,,
732:\end{equation}
733:gives predicted masses $(478,\; 92.3,\; 16{,}377)\MeV$. Only the
734:middle value echoes $m_s$. The lightest ($478\MeV$) does not match
735:any known quark mass, and the heaviest ($16.4\GeV$) falls between
736:$m_b$ and $m_t$ with no candidate. No SM triplet accommodates these
737:values.
738:
739:\textbf{The scaling stops at one step.} This is a negative result
740:that constrains any attempt to build a recursive tower of Koide
741:triples: the multiplicative rule $M \to 3M$, $\delta \to 3\delta$
742:is not a general principle but a one-off relation between the lepton
743:and quark sectors. Whether this single success is accidental or
744:reflects a deeper connection (perhaps related to the three colors
745:of QCD, or to the three generations) remains open.

747:\subsection{Iterative chain: descending from $t$ and $b$}
748:\label{subsec:chain}
749:
750:An alternative approach to connecting the quark masses is purely
751:algebraic: given two known masses, solve the Koide equation
752:$Q = 3/2$ for the third. Starting from the top and bottom quarks:
753:
754:\paragraph{Step~1: $Q(m_b, m_?, m_t) = 3/2$.}
755:The Koide equation with $(m_b, m_t)$ known is quadratic in
756:$\sqrt{m_?}$, yielding four branches (two signs for $\sqrt{m_?}$,
757:two roots of each quadratic). The solutions are:
758:\begin{equation}
759:m_? = \{1357,\; 60{,}280,\; 1.34 \times 10^6,\;
760:3.55 \times 10^6\}\MeV\,.
761:\end{equation}
762:Only the first branch gives a physically viable mass:
763:$m_? = 1357\MeV$, which we identify with the charm quark
764:(PDG: $m_c(m_c) = 1270\MeV$, deviation $6.8\%$).
765:
766:\paragraph{Step~2: $Q(m_c, m_?, m_b) = 3/2$.}
767:With $m_c = 1357\MeV$ (the chain value, not the PDG value) and
768:$m_b = 4180\MeV$:
769:\begin{equation}
770:m_? = 92.17\MeV\,,
771:\end{equation}
772:which we identify with the strange quark. The negative square root
773:emerges naturally from the algebra---the solution selects the branch
774:with $\sqrt{m_?} < 0$ in the signed-charge framework, consistent
775:with the $(-s, c, b)$ structure of \S\ref{sec:two_tuples}.
776:
777:\paragraph{Step~3: the chain stalls.}
778:Continuing from $(m_s, m_c)$ with $m_s = 92.17\MeV$:
779:\begin{equation}
780:m_? = 0.035\MeV\,.
781:\end{equation}
782:This is far too light for either $u$ ($2.16\MeV$) or $d$
783:($4.67\MeV$). The chain does not reach the first-generation quarks;
784:it stalls at a mass two orders of magnitude below $m_u$.
785:
786:\paragraph{Step~4: cyclic echo.}
787:The stalled mass provides an algebraic consistency check.
788:Taking $m_{\text{stall}} = 0.035\MeV$ and $m_s = 92.17\MeV$, and
789:solving $Q(m_{\text{stall}}, m_?, m_s) = 3/2$:
790:\begin{equation}
791:m_? = 1357\MeV \quad \text{(to machine epsilon)}\,.
792:\end{equation}
793:The chain returns to its starting point: $t \to c \to s \to
794:\text{stall} \to c \to \cdots$. This is not a numerical coincidence
795:but an algebraic necessity: the Koide condition, combined with the
796:specific masses of $b$ and $t$, defines a closed orbit in
797:$(m_1, m_2, m_3)$ space. The cycle is:
798:\begin{multline*}
799:\cdots \;\to\; (b,t) \xrightarrow{Q=3/2} c
800:\;\to\; (c,b) \xrightarrow{Q=3/2} s \\
801:\;\to\; (s,c) \xrightarrow{Q=3/2} \text{stall}
802:\;\to\; (\text{stall}, s) \xrightarrow{Q=3/2} c
803:\;\to\; \cdots
804:\end{multline*}
805:
806:The chain captures $c$, $b$, $s$ but misses $u$, $d$, and the
807:leptons entirely. The first generation is outside the algebraic
808:orbit seeded by the third generation.

865:%======================================================================
866:\section{Composite Scalar Counting and Generation Uniqueness}
867:\label{sec:counting}
868:%======================================================================
869:
870:The sBootstrap rests on a single postulate: every scalar of the
871:Supersymmetric Standard Model is a composite of two quarks (diquark,
872:color triplet) or of a quark--antiquark pair (meson, color singlet),
873:with the electric charge given by the sum of the constituents' charges.
874:We now show that matching the number of such composites to the
875:fermionic degrees of freedom of $N$ generations uniquely forces
876:$N = 3$.
877:
878:\subsection{Setup and Diophantine system}
879:Let there be $N$ generations, each containing quarks of two types:
880:\emph{down} (charge $-1/3$) and \emph{up} (charge $+2/3$).  Not
881:every quark need participate as a string endpoint; we allow
882:$r$ light down-type quarks and $s$ light up-type quarks that form
883:composites.  The Standard Model with right-handed neutrinos has
884:$4N$ real (Weyl) fermionic degrees of freedom per charge sector in
885:each color channel.  Matching composites to fermions, charge sector
886:by charge sector, yields the following system.
887:
888:\begin{enumerate}
889:\item \textbf{Charge $+1/3$ composites} (one up $\times$ one down,
890:analogous to charged sleptons): $rs$ complex scalars must match
891:$2N$ real fermionic d.o.f., giving
892:\begin{equation}
893:\label{eq:rs}
894:rs = 2N\,.
895:\end{equation}
896:
897:\item \textbf{Charge $-2/3$ composites} (symmetric down--down pairs,
898:color sextet): $\binom{r+1}{2} = r(r+1)/2$ complex scalars must
899:match $2N$ real fermionic d.o.f., giving
900:\begin{equation}
901:\label{eq:rr}
902:\frac{r(r+1)}{2} = 2N\,.
903:\end{equation}
904:
905:\item \textbf{Neutral composites} (classified by $SU(r+s)$, minus
906:the overall singlet trace; includes the partner of the right-handed
907:neutrino): $(r+s)^2 - 1 = r^2 + s^2 + 2rs - 1$ states, of which
908:$2rs$ are charged and already counted.  The neutral count is therefore
909:$r^2 + s^2 - 1$, to be matched to $4N$ real d.o.f., giving
910:\begin{equation}
911:\label{eq:neutral}
912:r^2 + s^2 - 1 = 4N\,.
913:\end{equation}
914:\end{enumerate}
915:
916:\noindent
917:The alternative neutral equation $r^2 + s^2 - 1 = 2N$ (left-handed
918:neutrinos only) produces no positive-integer solution when combined
919:with \eqref{eq:rs}--\eqref{eq:rr}; thus the sBootstrap \emph{requires}
920:right-handed neutrinos.
921:
922:\subsection{The uniqueness theorem}
923:
924:\medskip
925:\noindent\textbf{Theorem 1} (Generation uniqueness).
926:\textit{The system \eqref{eq:rs}--\eqref{eq:neutral} has a unique
927:solution over the positive integers:}
928:\begin{equation}
929:\boxed{(N,\,r,\,s) = (3,\,3,\,2)\,.}
930:\end{equation}
931:
932:\medskip
933:\noindent\textit{Proof.}
934:Equating the right-hand sides of \eqref{eq:rs} and \eqref{eq:rr}
935:gives $rs = r(r+1)/2$, hence
936:\begin{equation}
937:\label{eq:s_from_r}
938:s = \frac{r+1}{2}\,,
939:\end{equation}
940:which requires $r$ odd.  Substituting \eqref{eq:rs} and
941:\eqref{eq:s_from_r} into \eqref{eq:neutral}:
942:\[
943:r^2 + \Bigl(\frac{r+1}{2}\Bigr)^{\!2} - 1 \;=\; 4\,\cdot\,\frac{r\,(r+1)/2}{2}
944:\;=\; r(r+1)\,.
945:\]
946:Expanding the left side,
947:\[
948:r^2 + \frac{r^2 + 2r + 1}{4} - 1 = r^2 + r\,,
949:\]
950:and clearing the denominator:
951:\[
952:4r^2 + r^2 + 2r + 1 - 4 = 4r^2 + 4r
953:\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
954:r^2 - 2r - 3 = 0\,,
955:\]
956:i.e.\ $(r-3)(r+1) = 0$.  Since $r > 0$, we obtain $r = 3$, whence
957:$s = 2$ and $N = rs/2 = 3$.
958:\hfill$\square$
959:
960:\medskip
961:The solution has $r + s = 5$ light quarks acting as string endpoints,
962:furnishing the fundamental representation of an $SU(5)$ flavor group.
963:The top quark is the unique ``elephant''---too heavy to serve as a
964:constituent of the scalar composites---in precise agreement with the
965:Standard Model, where $m_t \gg \Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$ prevents
966:top-flavored hadrons from forming.
967:
968:\subsection{Partial solutions and the hexagonal-number family}

1108:\subsection{Anomaly cancellation and asymptotic freedom}
1109:The representation $\mathbf{24} \oplus \mathbf{15} \oplus
1110:\overline{\mathbf{15}}$ must be anomaly-free if it is to appear
1111:in a consistent gauge theory.
1112:
1113:\medskip
1114:\noindent\textbf{Proposition 2} (Anomaly cancellation).
1115:\textit{The cubic $SU(5)$ anomaly of $\mathbf{24} \oplus
1116:\mathbf{15} \oplus \overline{\mathbf{15}}$ vanishes.}
1117:
1118:\medskip
1119:\noindent\textit{Proof.}
1120:The anomaly coefficient $A(\mathbf{R})$ for $SU(n)$ satisfies
1121:$A(\text{adjoint}) = 0$ (the adjoint is real),
1122:$A(\mathbf{15}) = n + 4 = 9$, and
1123:$A(\overline{\mathbf{15}}) = -(n+4) = -9$.  Therefore
1124:\[
1125:A(\mathbf{24}) + A(\mathbf{15}) + A(\overline{\mathbf{15}})
1126:= 0 + 9 + (-9) = 0\,.
1127:\]
1128:\hfill$\square$
1129:
1130:\medskip
1131:\noindent\textbf{Proposition 3} (Asymptotic freedom).
1132:\textit{An $SU(5)$ gauge theory with matter in the
1133:$\mathbf{24} \oplus \mathbf{15} \oplus \overline{\mathbf{15}}$
1134:is asymptotically free.}
1135:
1136:\medskip
1137:\noindent\textit{Proof.}
1138:The one-loop beta-function coefficient is
1139:\[
1140:b_0 = \frac{11}{3}\,C_2(G) - \frac{2}{3}\,\sum_{\text{Weyl}} T(\mathbf{R})\,,
1141:\]
1142:where $C_2(G) = n = 5$ for $SU(5)$, $T(\mathbf{adj}) = n = 5$,
1143:and $T(\mathbf{15}) = (n+2)/2 = 7/2$.  The Weyl fermion content
1144:consists of one adjoint and one $\mathbf{15} + \overline{\mathbf{15}}$
1145:(treating each as a Weyl fermion), giving
1146:$\sum T = 5 + 7/2 + 7/2 = 12$.  Therefore
1147:\[
1148:b_0 = \frac{11}{3} \times 5 - \frac{2}{3} \times 12
1149:= \frac{55 - 24}{3} = \frac{31}{3} > 0\,.
1150:\]
1151:\hfill$\square$
1152:
1153:\medskip
1154:Both properties are nontrivial: anomaly cancellation ensures
1155:the gauge symmetry is quantum-mechanically consistent, and
1156:$b_0 > 0$ ensures the theory is well-defined in the ultraviolet.
1157:Together they confirm that the representation forced by the
1158:sBootstrap counting is physically viable.

1160:\subsection{Summary of the counting argument}
1161:The logical chain is:
1162:\begin{enumerate}
1163:\item The sBootstrap postulate (composites $=$ scalars of the SSM)
1164:yields three Diophantine equations in $(N, r, s)$.
1165:\item The unique positive-integer solution is $(3, 3, 2)$:
1166:three generations, with the symmetric $\mathbf{15}$ forced over
1167:the antisymmetric $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$.
1168:\item The solution organizes into $SU(5)$ representations
1169:$\mathbf{24} \oplus \mathbf{15} \oplus \overline{\mathbf{15}}$,
1170:which are anomaly-free and asymptotically free.
1171:\item Right-handed neutrinos are required: without them, no
1172:integer solution exists.
1173:\item The top quark is excluded from the composite sector by
1174:the counting itself ($s = 2 < N = 3$), matching its
1175:kinematic decoupling ($m_t \gg \Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$).
1176:\end{enumerate}

1179:\section{The Diquark Sector and SU(5) Flavor}
1180:\label{sec:diquarks}
1181:%======================================================================
1182:
1183:\subsection{Counting partners}
1184:
1185:In the full sBootstrap \cite{Rivero2005}, the scalar partners of 
1186:the Standard Model fermions include both mesons ($q\bar{q}$) and 
1187:diquarks ($qq$). For five active flavors, the meson matrix gives 
1188:$5 \times 5 = 25$ scalars. The diquarks $[q_i q_j]$ form a 
1189:representation of $SU(5)_f$:
1190:\begin{equation}
1191:\mathbf{5} \otimes \mathbf{5} = \overline{\mathbf{10}}_A 
1192:\oplus \mathbf{15}_S\,.
1193:\end{equation}
1194:
1195:The symmetric $\mathbf{15}_S$ gives 15 diquarks including 
1196:same-flavor states $[uu]$, $[dd]$, \ldots, while the antisymmetric 
1197:$\overline{\mathbf{10}}_A$ gives 10 diquarks with $i \neq j$ only.
1198:
1199:\subsection{The symmetric representation}
1200:\label{subsec:sym15}
1201:
1202:The sBootstrap SUSY generator acts asymmetrically on the two
1203:fermion sectors. When it acts on a \emph{lepton}, it produces a
1204:meson: $\mathcal{Q} \cdot \ell \sim q\bar{q}$. Since the quark and antiquark 
1205:are distinguishable, the meson lies in the full 
1206:$\mathbf{5} \otimes \bar{\mathbf{5}} = \mathbf{24} + \mathbf{1}$. 
1207:There is no Pauli constraint, and the meson spectrum is perfectly 
1208:reproduced.
1209:
1210:When it acts on a \emph{quark}, it produces a diquark:
1211:$\mathcal{Q} \cdot q_i \sim q_i q_j$. The bosonic SUSY generator applied to 
1212:a single flavor index $i$ naturally produces the \textbf{symmetric} 
1213:product---the $\mathbf{15}_S$ representation, not the antisymmetric 
1214:$\overline{\mathbf{10}}$. This is the representation identified 
1215:in the $SO(32)$ decomposition \cite{Rivero2005}: the adjoint of 
1216:$SO(32)$ under $SU(5)_f$ decomposes to include the 
1217:$\mathbf{15} + \overline{\mathbf{15}}$ (diquarks) alongside the 
1218:$\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{24}$ (mesons).
1219:
1220:The 15 includes 5 same-flavor states ($[uu]$, $[dd]$, $[ss]$, 
1221:$[cc]$, $[bb]$) absent from the $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$. Among 
1222:these are exotic $+4/3$-charged diquarks such as $[uu]$. The cost: 
1223:an additional chiral $+4/3$ quark per generation, as identified 
1224:in \cite{Rivero2005}. Whether these exotic states appear in nature 
1225:as resonances, or whether they decouple at some high scale, is an 
1226:open question.
1227:
1228:\subsection{The Pauli theorem for scalar diquarks}
1229:\label{subsec:pauli}
1230:
1231:The requirement of the symmetric $\mathbf{15}$ conflicts with 
1232:Fermi statistics---not merely for $L=0$ diquarks, but for 
1233:\emph{all} scalar diquarks in the color $\bar{3}$ channel, 
1234:regardless of internal orbital structure.
1235:
1236:\medskip
1237:\noindent\textbf{Theorem.}
1238:\textit{For a spin-0 ($J=0$) diquark in the color $\bar{3}$ 
1239:(antisymmetric) representation, the flavor wave function is 
1240:antisymmetric for \textbf{all} values of the orbital angular 
1241:momentum $L$ and spin $S$.}
1242:
1243:\medskip
1244:\noindent\textit{Proof.}
1245:$J = 0$ requires $L = S$ (since $|L - S| \leq J \leq L + S$). 
1246:The exchange symmetry of the spin-orbital wave function is 
1247:$(-1)^{L+S+1} = (-1)^{2L+1} = -1$ for all $L$. Combined with 
1248:the color $\bar{3}$ factor $(-1)$, the non-flavor product is 
1249:$(-1)(-1) = +1$ (symmetric). Total antisymmetry then requires 
1250:flavor to be antisymmetric: $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$. \hfill$\square$
1251:
1252:\medskip
1253:This result is purely kinematic---independent of dynamics, 
1254:binding mechanisms, or spatial extent. No wave function, however 
1255:extended, can produce a symmetric-flavor scalar diquark in color 
1256:$\bar{3}$ while respecting Fermi statistics. The key cases:
1257:
1258:\begin{center}
1259:\begin{tabular}{ccccc}
1260:\toprule
1261:$L$ & $S$ & Spin-orbital $(-1)^{2L+1}$ & $\times$ Color & Flavor \\
1262:\midrule
1263:0 & 0 & $-1$ & $\times\,(-1) = +1$ & must be A \\
1264:1 & 1 & $-1$ & $\times\,(-1) = +1$ & must be A \\
1265:2 & 2 & $-1$ & $\times\,(-1) = +1$ & must be A \\
1266:\bottomrule
1267:\end{tabular}
1268:\end{center}
1269:
1270:The $L=1$, $S=1$ row closes the ``P-wave diquark'' escape route 
1271:that might otherwise seem available.
1272:
1273:The theorem has a sharp consequence: \textbf{the sBootstrap 
1274:diquark in the $\mathbf{15}$ cannot be a $qq$ composite.} It 
1275:must be either:
1276:\begin{enumerate}
1277:\item An algebraically generated object---the image of the 
1278:Miyazawa supercharge \cite{Miyazawa, CattoGursey} acting on 
1279:an antiquark, whose symmetry is determined by the superalgebra 
1280:rather than by the statistics of constituent quarks; or
1281:\item A fundamental string-theoretic object, as in the $SO(32)$ 
1282:decomposition \cite{Rivero2005}, where the $\mathbf{15}$ 
1283:arises from the adjoint representation of the gauge group and 
1284:the open-string diquark is not built from point-like quarks.
1285:\end{enumerate}
1286:
1287:The contrast with the meson sector is instructive. When the 
1288:SUSY generator acts on a lepton, it produces a meson 
1289:($q\bar{q}$): the quark and antiquark are distinguishable, 
1290:no Pauli constraint applies, and the meson spectrum is 
1291:reproduced without tension. When it acts on a quark, it 
1292:produces a diquark in the ``wrong'' representation---and the 
1293:theorem shows this cannot be fixed by any internal structure 
1294:of a $qq$ pair. The asymmetry between the two SUSY directions 
1295:is absolute: mesons work as composites; diquarks do not. 
1296:This is an unresolved obstruction: the sBootstrap requires
1297:the symmetric $\mathbf{15}$, but QCD composite diquarks cannot
1298:fill it. The proposed resolutions---Miyazawa supercharges and
1299:SO(32) string embeddings---are directions for future work, not
1300:established mechanisms. The diquark sector remains the weakest
1301:point of the program.

1409:\subsection{Physical content and the projection problem}
1410:\label{subsec:so32_open}

1412:Restricting to the adjoint sector $Q_A = Q_B = 0$ and dropping the
1413:horizontal $SU(3)$ factor, one reads off the $SU(5)$ content:
1414:\begin{equation}

1416:\end{equation}
1417:which is precisely the meson/slepton content of the sBootstrap.
1418:The matter sector at $|Q_A| = 2$ contains
1419:\begin{equation}
1420:\mathbf{15} \;\oplus\; \overline{\mathbf{15}}
1421:  \;\oplus\; \mathbf{10} \;\oplus\; \overline{\mathbf{10}}
1422:  \;\oplus\; \mathbf{5} \;\oplus\; \bar{\mathbf{5}}\,,
1423:\end{equation}
1424:among which the $\mathbf{15} + \overline{\mathbf{15}}$ are
1425:the symmetric diquarks demanded by the sBootstrap counting
1426:(\S\ref{subsec:sym15}).

1429:adjoint: the $\mathbf{24}$ in the neutral sector ($Q_A\!=\!Q_B\!=\!0$)
1430:and the $\mathbf{15} \oplus \overline{\mathbf{15}}$ in the matter
1431:sector ($|Q_A|\!=\!2$). However, the remaining $496 - 54 = 442$
1432:states (the $\mathbf{10}$, $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$,
1433:$\mathbf{5}$, $\bar{\mathbf{5}}$, the SU(3)-horizontal copies,
1434:and the neutral singlets) must be projected out or made massive.
1435:The mechanism for this projection---whether by an orbifold,
1436:a GSO-type condition, or a flux background---is an open problem.

1439:here is to provide a UV origin for the symmetric $\mathbf{15}$
1440:that the Pauli theorem (\S\ref{subsec:pauli}) forbids at the
1441:level of point-particle composites: in the string picture,
1442:diquarks are open-string states whose symmetry is determined by
1443:the gauge group, not by Fermi statistics of constituents.

1445:\paragraph{Why $SO(32)$ and not $E_8 \times E_8$.}
1446:Anomaly cancellation in ten dimensions selects two gauge groups:
1447:$SO(32)$ and $E_8 \times E_8$. The latter cannot accommodate the
1448:sBootstrap. Every decomposition of $E_8$ under $SU(5)$ produces
1449:the antisymmetric $\mathbf{10}$, never the symmetric $\mathbf{15}$:
1450:the maximal-subgroup chain $E_8 \supset SU(5) \times SU(5)$ gives
1451:$\mathbf{248} = (\mathbf{24},\mathbf{1}) + (\mathbf{1},\mathbf{24})
1452:+ (\mathbf{10},\bar{\mathbf{5}}) + (\overline{\mathbf{10}},\mathbf{5})
1453:+ (\mathbf{5},\mathbf{10}) + (\bar{\mathbf{5}},\overline{\mathbf{10}})$,
1454:with no $\mathbf{15}$ appearing anywhere.  The obstruction is
1455:irreducible: it reflects the fact that $E_8$'s simply-laced root
1456:lattice admits only antisymmetric tensor representations of
1457:$SU(5)$.  By contrast, $SO(32)$'s adjoint $\mathbf{496} =
1458:\wedge^2(\mathbf{32})$ naturally contains the symmetric
1459:$\mathbf{15}$ through the identity
1460:$\wedge^2(\mathbf{5} \otimes \mathbf{3}) \supset
1461:S^2(\mathbf{5}) \otimes \wedge^2(\mathbf{3})
1462:= (\mathbf{15}, \bar{\mathbf{3}})$.  The Type~I realization, where
1463:quark Chan-Paton factors directly generate $SO(32)$ from
1464:$5\!\times\!3 + 1 = 16$ endpoint labels, is the most natural home.
1465:
1466:The $E_8 \times E_8$ heterotic string is connected to Type~I $SO(32)$
1467:by a chain of dualities (S-duality to heterotic $SO(32)$, then
1468:T-duality on a non-simply-connected torus).  These dualities may
1469:allow the sBootstrap content to appear in the $E_8 \times E_8$ frame
1470:through a non-standard embedding, but the direct decomposition
1471:route is blocked by the $\mathbf{15}$ vs.\ $\mathbf{10}$
1472:obstruction.

1475:\subsection{Part B: The Casimir eigenvalue equation}
1476:\label{subsec:casimir_eq}
1477:%----------------------------------------------------------------------
1478:
1479:Consider the eigenvalue equation
1480:\begin{equation}
1481:\label{eq:casimir_quartic}
1482:x^2 \;+\; C_2\,x \;-\; C_2 = 0\,,
1483:\qquad C_2 = s(s+1)\,,
1484:\end{equation}
1485:where $C_2$ is the quadratic Casimir of $SU(2)$ at spin $s$ and
1486:$x = M^2/m^2$ for a mass scale $m$ to be determined.  Equivalently,
1487:$x^2/(1-x) = C_2$: the eigenvalue is a self-consistent solution
1488:where the ``squared coupling'' $x^2$ equals $C_2$ times the
1489:complement $(1-x)$.  The solutions are
1490:\begin{equation}
1491:\label{eq:casimir_roots}
1492:x_{\pm}(s) = \frac{-C_2 \pm \sqrt{C_2^2 + 4C_2}}{2}\,.
1493:\end{equation}
1494:
1495:\paragraph{Structural constraints (codimension analysis).}
1496:The most general two-parameter family of such equations is
1497:\begin{equation}
1498:x^2 + (f_0 + f_1 C_2)\,x + (g_0 + g_1 C_2) = 0\,,
1499:\end{equation}
1500:with four real coefficients $(f_0, f_1, g_0, g_1)$.  Three
1501:structural conditions pin the polynomial to \eqref{eq:casimir_quartic}:
1502:\begin{enumerate}
1503:\item $f_0 = 0$: no spin-independent mass term;
1504:\item $g_0 = 0$: a massless eigenstate exists at $s = 0$;
1505:\item $g_1 = -f_1$: coefficient antisymmetry
1506:  (the constant and linear terms in $C_2$ are related).
1507:\end{enumerate}
1508:Together with the normalization $f_1 = 1$, these exhaust the
1509:freedom.  A systematic scan of 582 polynomial alternatives
1510:(quadratics, cubics, quartics with Casimir-type functions and
1511:rational coefficients) confirms uniqueness: no other
1512:equation of this class reproduces $\sin^2\!\theta_W$ to
1513:sub-percent accuracy at $(s_1, s_2) = (1/2, 1)$.
1514:The ratio derived below is therefore a
1515:\emph{consequence} of these constraints, not a free parameter.
1516:
1517:\subsection{The electroweak ratio}
1518:Define the ratio
1519:\begin{equation}
1520:R \;\equiv\; 1 - \frac{x_+(1/2)}{x_+(1)}\,.
1521:\end{equation}
1522:Evaluating \eqref{eq:casimir_roots} at $s = 1/2$ ($C_2 = 3/4$)
1523:and $s = 1$ ($C_2 = 2$):
1524:\begin{align}
1525:x_+(1/2) &= \tfrac{1}{8}\bigl(-3 + \sqrt{57}\bigr)
1526:  \,,\quad\text{so } x_+(1/2)\,m^2 = M_W^2\,,
1527:\\[4pt]
1528:x_+(1) &= -1 + \sqrt{3}
1529:  \,,\quad\text{so } x_+(1)\,m^2 = M_Z^2\,.
1530:\end{align}
1531:The identification with $W$ and $Z$ is motivated by their
1532:$SU(2)$ quantum numbers ($s = 1/2$ maps to the doublet structure of $W$,
1533:$s = 1$ to the triplet).  Then
1534:\begin{equation}
1535:R = 1 - \frac{x_+(1/2)}{x_+(1)}
1536:  = \frac{(\sqrt{19} - 3)(\sqrt{19} - \sqrt{3}\,)}{16}
1537:  \;\approx\; 0.2231013\ldots
1538:\end{equation}
1539:The factored form follows from the discriminants of
1540:\eqref{eq:casimir_quartic}: $57 = 3 \times 19$ at $s = 1/2$ and
1541:$12 = 4 \times 3$ at $s = 1$.  Rationalizing the denominator
1542:$\sqrt{3} - 1$ yields an expression in $\sqrt{19}$ and $\sqrt{3}$
1543:alone, which coincides term-by-term with the form found
1544:empirically by de~Vries.
1545:
1546:This should be compared with the on-shell electroweak mixing
1547:parameter:
1548:\begin{equation}
1549:\label{eq:sin2_onshell}
1550:\sin^2\!\theta_W^{\text{os}}
1551:  \;\equiv\; 1 - \frac{M_W^2}{M_Z^2}
1552:  = 0.22320 \pm 0.00026\,,
1553:\end{equation}
1554:using $M_Z = 91.1876 \pm 0.0021\GeV$ and
1555:$M_W = 80.369 \pm 0.013\GeV$ (PDG~2024).
1556:
1557:Since $R = 1 - x_+(1/2)/x_+(1)$ and
1558:$\sin^2\!\theta_W = 1 - M_W^2/M_Z^2$, the two expressions are
1559:\emph{algebraically identical} once one identifies
1560:$x_+(s) = M^2(s)/m^2$.  The prediction $R \approx
1561:\sin^2\!\theta_W$ and the $W$-mass prediction derived below are
1562:therefore \textbf{one and the same observation}, not two
1563:independent tests.
1564:
1565:\paragraph{$W$-mass prediction.}
1566:From $M_W = M_Z\sqrt{1 - R}$:
1567:\begin{equation}
1568:M_W^{\text{pred}}
1569:  = 91.1876 \times \sqrt{1 - 0.22310}
1570:  = 80.374 \pm 0.002\GeV\,,
1571:\end{equation}
1572:which lies $0.39\sigma$ from the PDG average
1573:$M_W = 80.369 \pm 0.013\GeV$ and strongly disfavors the
1574:anomalous CDF-II measurement $M_W^{\text{CDF}} = 80.4335 \pm
1575:0.0094\GeV$ (6.2$\sigma$ tension with $R$).
1576:
1577:\paragraph{Scale parameter.}
1578:Setting $x_+(1)\,m^2 = M_Z^2$ gives
1579:\begin{equation}
1580:m = \frac{M_Z}{\sqrt{\sqrt{3} - 1}}
1581:  = 106.577\GeV
1582:  \approx m_\mu \times 10^3\,,
1583:\end{equation}
1584:a coincidence whose significance is unclear.

1586:\subsection{Open issues for the Casimir equation}
1587:\label{subsec:casimir_open}
1588:
1589:\paragraph{Renormalization scheme dependence.}
1590:The comparison \eqref{eq:sin2_onshell} uses the on-shell
1591:definition $\sin^2\!\theta_W = 1 - M_W^2/M_Z^2 = 0.22320$,
1592:\textbf{not} the $\overline{\text{MS}}$ value
1593:$\sin^2\!\theta_W(\overline{\text{MS}}) = 0.23122 \pm 0.00004$.
1594:The two differ by $\Delta \approx 0.008$, far larger than
1595:experimental errors.  The algebraic argument
1596:\eqref{eq:casimir_quartic} contains no dynamical mechanism to
1597:prefer one scheme over the other.  Until such a mechanism is
1598:identified, the on-shell agreement should be regarded as
1599:suggestive but scheme-dependent.

1601:\paragraph{The fourth eigenvalue.}
1602:At $s = 1/2$, the second root of \eqref{eq:casimir_quartic} gives
1603:\begin{equation}
1604:|M(1/2, -)|
1605:  = m\,\sqrt{|x_-(1/2)|}
1606:  = 122.4\GeV\,,
1607:\end{equation}
1608:which is 2.3\% below $M_H = 125.25 \pm 0.17\GeV$.  However,
1609:this eigenstate carries spin $1/2$, while the Higgs boson is
1610:spin $0$.  The near-degeneracy is noted as a numerical curiosity;
1611:we do not claim a connection.

1613:\paragraph{Origin.}
1614:This observation was first made by H.~de~Vries in a Physics
1615:Forums discussion (November 2004) and subsequently studied in

1646:\section{SUSY Breaking: From Seeds to Spectrum}
1647:\label{sec:breaking}
1648:%======================================================================
1649:
1650:The SUSY-breaking story begins with the foundational observation 
1651:\eqref{eq:pimu}: $m_\pi^2 - m_\mu^2 \approx f_\pi^2$. This is the 
1652:Volkov--Akulov mass formula for a pseudo-Goldstino---the muon---whose 
1653:superpartner---the pion---is a pseudo-Goldstone boson. The SUSY-breaking 
1654:scale is $f_\pi \approx 92\MeV$, and the breaking mechanism is chiral: 
1655:$\qqbar \neq 0$ breaks both chiral symmetry and the sBootstrap SUSY 
1656:simultaneously. The Volkov--Akulov realization is the \emph{physical 
1657:starting point}; what follows in this section is the SQCD machinery 
1658:that makes it precise.
1659:
1660:The dynamics must accomplish three things: generate the seed-to-bloom 
1661:transition for each tuple, preserve the energy-balance condition 
1662:$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ through the breaking, and produce 
1663:the correct splitting between meson and lepton masses at scale $f_\pi$.
1664:
1665:\subsection{The Seiberg framework}
1666:
1667:The natural arena is $\mathcal{N}=1$ SQCD with gauge group $SU(3)_c$ 
1668:and $N_f$ flavors of chiral superfields $Q^i$, $\bar{Q}_j$ in the 
1669:fundamental and antifundamental representations. For $N_f = N_c = 3$ 
1670:(the case relevant to light QCD), Seiberg's exact results 
1671:\cite{Seiberg1995} establish that the theory confines, with the 
1672:low-energy degrees of freedom being the composite meson superfield
1673:\begin{equation}
1674:M^i{}_j = Q^i \cdot \bar{Q}_j\,,
1675:\end{equation}
1676:a $3\times 3$ matrix of chiral superfields, together with baryons 
1677:$B = \epsilon_{ijk}Q^iQ^jQ^k$ and $\bar{B}$. These satisfy the 
1678:quantum-modified constraint
1679:\begin{equation}
1680:\label{eq:seiberg}
1681:\det M - B\bar{B} = \Lambda^{6}\,,
1682:\end{equation}
1683:where $\Lambda$ is the dynamical scale ($2N_c = 6$ for $N_c = 3$).
1684:
1685:Each entry $M^i{}_j$ is a chiral superfield containing a complex 
1686:scalar (the meson) and a Weyl fermion (the lepton, in the sBootstrap 
1687:identification). The scalar and fermion are degenerate in the SUSY 
1688:limit; SUSY breaking splits them.
1689:
1690:\subsection{The three chiral superfields of each seed}
1691:
1692:The seed triple $(0, \zch_a, \zch_b)$ maps naturally onto three 
1693:chiral superfields with the following mass structure before breaking:
1694:
1695:\begin{center}
1696:\begin{tabular}{lcl}
1697:\toprule
1698:Superfield & Mass & Role \\
1699:\midrule
1700:$\Phi_0$ & $m_0 = 0$ & Massless: flat direction \\
1701:$\Phi_a$ & $m_a = \zch_a^2$ & Light massive member \\
1702:$\Phi_b$ & $m_b = \zch_b^2$ & Heavy massive member \\
1703:\bottomrule
1704:\end{tabular}
1705:\end{center}
1706:
1707:The ratio $\zch_a/\zch_b = 2 - \sqrt{3}$ is enforced by the 
1708:Koide energy-balance condition applied to the seed.
1709:
1710:In the Seiberg description, these three superfields are entries of 
1711:the meson matrix $M^i{}_j$. For the $(0, s, c)$ seed, the relevant 
1712:entries involve the strange and charm quarks; $\Phi_0$ is the entry 
1713:whose quark-mass combination vanishes in the seed limit (i.e., the 
1714:direction in flavor space where $m_i + m_j \to 0$ through GOR).
1715:
1716:\subsection{F-term breaking: the O'Raifeartaigh mechanism}
1717:\label{subsec:fterm}
1718:
1719:The VA relation \eqref{eq:pimu} tells us \emph{that} SUSY is broken at 
1720:scale $f_\pi$. To understand \emph{how}, we need the microscopic 
1721:mechanism. The O'Raifeartaigh model \cite{ORaifeartaigh} is the simplest 
1722:realization of spontaneous $F$-term SUSY breaking. With three chiral 
1723:superfields, the superpotential
1724:\begin{equation}
1725:W = f\,\Phi_0 + m\,\Phi_1\Phi_2 + g\,\Phi_0\Phi_1^2
1726:\end{equation}
1727:gives $F$-terms:
1728:\begin{align}
1729:F_{\Phi_0}^* &= f + g\,\phi_1^2\,, \\
1730:F_{\Phi_1}^* &= m\,\phi_2 + 2g\,\phi_0\phi_1\,, \\
1731:F_{\Phi_2}^* &= m\,\phi_1\,.
1732:\end{align}
1733:If $f \neq 0$ and $m \neq 0$, then $F_{\Phi_2}^* = 0$ requires 
1734:$\phi_1 = 0$, but then $F_{\Phi_0}^* = f \neq 0$: SUSY is 
1735:\textbf{necessarily broken}. The key features are:
1736:\begin{enumerate}
1737:\item A massless field ($\Phi_0$) exists at tree level---it is the 
1738:pseudo-modulus.
1739:\item The breaking is triggered because $F_{\Phi_0}$ cannot vanish.
1740:\item After including loop corrections, $\Phi_0$ acquires a mass 
1741:(the Coleman--Weinberg potential lifts the flat direction).
1742:\end{enumerate}
1743:
1744:The structural parallel with the seed triples is:
1745:
1746:\begin{center}
1747:\begin{tabular}{llll}
1748:\toprule
1749:O'Raifeartaigh & Flavor & Seed & After breaking \\
1750:\midrule
1751:$\Phi_0$ (massless) & $b$ / $B$ & $\zch = 0$ (flat direction) & $\zch_b = 64.65$ (mass generated) \\
1752:$\Phi_1$ (light) & $s$ / $\pi$ & $\zch_s = 9.67$ & $\zch \to -9.67$ (sign-flipped) \\
1753:$\Phi_2$ (heavy) & $c$ / $D_s$ & $\zch_c = 35.64$ & $\zch_c \approx 35.64$ (shifted) \\
1754:\midrule
1755: & & $f = 0$ (SUSY) & $f = f_\pi$ (broken) \\
1756:\bottomrule
1757:\end{tabular}
1758:\end{center}
1759:
1760:This parallel is more than structural: the minimal O'Raifeartaigh
1761:model gives $\Phi_1$ and $\Phi_2$ a single mass parameter $m$, but the
1762:\emph{fermionic} mass matrix at the vacuum ($\phi_1 = 0$,
1763:$\phi_0 = v$) is
1764:\begin{equation*}
1765:  W_{ij} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2gv & m \\ 0 & m & 0 \end{pmatrix},
1766:\end{equation*}
1767:with eigenvalues $0$ (the goldstino) and $m_\pm = \sqrt{g^2v^2 + m^2} \pm gv$.
1768:The mass ratio $m_-/m_+ = (\sqrt{t^2+1} - t)^2$, where $t = gv/m$,
1769:is \emph{not} unity.  Setting $\sqrt{m_-}/\sqrt{m_+} = 2 - \sqrt{3}$
1770:(the Koide seed ratio) requires $\sqrt{t^2+1} - t = 2 - \sqrt{3}$,
1771:which gives
1772:\begin{equation*}
1773:  t = \frac{gv}{m} = \sqrt{3}\,,
1774:\end{equation*}
1775:the unique value at which $t^2 + 1 = 4$, so $m_\pm = (2 \pm \sqrt{3})\,m$.
1776:The Koide ratio for the triple $(0, m_-, m_+) = (0,\, (2-\sqrt{3})\,m,
1777:\, (2+\sqrt{3})\,m)$ evaluates to $Q = 4m/(\sqrt{2-\sqrt{3}} +
1778:\sqrt{2+\sqrt{3}})^2 = 4/6 = 2/3$ exactly, since
1779:$\sqrt{2-\sqrt{3}}\cdot\sqrt{2+\sqrt{3}} = 1$ and the squared sum
1780:telescopes to $6$.
1781:
1782:The Koide condition on the seed is therefore \emph{one algebraic
1783:constraint on the superpotential parameters}: $gv = \sqrt{3}\,m$.
1784:The coupling $g$, the pseudo-modulus VEV $v$, and the mass
1785:parameter $m$ are individually undetermined, but their ratio is fixed.
1786:This is the microscopic content of the energy-balance condition
1787:$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ applied to the seed.
1788:
1789:The $b$ quark and $B$ meson are
1790:always present as flavor degrees of freedom; the seed configuration
1791:$(0, s, c)$ means the $b$-direction sits at the flat direction before
1792:breaking. The bloom is not particle creation but mass generation:
1793:breaking lifts $\Phi_0$ from zero to $\zch_b \neq 0$, simultaneously
1794:flipping the sign of $\zch_s$, so that the same three flavors
1795:rearrange from $(0, s, c)$ into $(-s, c, b)$.
1796:
1797:\paragraph{The bloom as a phase rotation.}
1798:In the Koide parametrization~\eqref{eq:koide_angle}, the seed sits at
1799:$\delta = 3\pi/4$, where $\cos(3\pi/4) = -1/\sqrt{2}$ forces one
1800:mass to vanish: $\sqrt{m_0} = \sqrt{M_0}(1 + \sqrt{2}\cos\delta) = 0$.
1801:A Koide-preserving bloom ($Q = 2/3$ maintained) is a pure rotation
1802:$\delta: 3\pi/4 \to \delta_{\text{phys}}$ at fixed scale $v_0$
1803:and fixed amplitude $r = \sqrt{2}\,v_0$. The total mass
1804:$M = 6v_0^2$ is conserved---mass merely redistributes among the three
1805:eigenvalues. The sign flip is instantaneous: the seed sits exactly at
1806:the boundary $\zch_0 = 0$, and any infinitesimal rotation generates
1807:a nonzero charge with definite sign.
1808:
1809:Explicit R-symmetry breaking in the O'Raifeartaigh model (adding a
1810:term $\delta W \sim \varepsilon\,\Phi_0\Phi_2$) lifts the goldstino
1811:mass as $m_0 \sim \varepsilon^2/m$, but simultaneously pushes
1812:$Q$ away from $2/3$---no nonzero $\varepsilon$ preserves the Koide
1813:condition. The Koide seed is an \emph{unstable fixed point} of the
1814:R-breaking perturbation. In the dynamical (SQCD) realization, the
1815:bloom must therefore be a nonperturbative rearrangement---not a
1816:smooth deformation of the O'Raifeartaigh spectrum.
1817:
1818:A striking difference from standard O'Raifeartaigh: the 
1819:pseudo-modulus $\Phi_0$ normally acquires the \emph{smallest} mass 
1820:in the spectrum (a loop-suppressed Coleman--Weinberg mass, 
1821:parametrically $\sim g^2 f/16\pi^2 m$). In the sBootstrap bloom, 
1822:the $b$-direction acquires the \emph{largest} mass 
1823:($m_b \gg m_s$ and $m_b > m_c$). The bloom is far more dramatic than a 
1824:perturbative CW lifting---it is a nonperturbative, strong-dynamics 
1825:effect in which confinement generates a mass of order $\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}^2/m_q$, 
1826:not $g^2/(16\pi^2)$. Numerically, the one-loop CW mass for the
1827:pseudo-modulus at the Seiberg vacuum is $m_{\text{CW}} \approx 13\MeV$,
1828:more than two orders of magnitude below the physical $m_b = 4{,}180\MeV$.
1829:This inversion is a signature of the dynamical (SQCD) realization
1830:versus the perturbative (superpotential) one.
1831:
1832:\paragraph{A candidate mechanism: fractional instantons.}
1833:On $\mathbb{R}^3 \times S^1$, SU($N_c$) instantons fractionalize into
1834:$N_c$ monopole-instantons \cite{Unsal2008}. Each monopole-instanton
1835:carries a single fermionic zero mode per flavor; when the zero mode is
1836:lifted by the quark mass $m_i$, the amplitude acquires a factor
1837:$\sqrt{m_i}$ (not $m_i$). Since the $N_c = 3$ monopole contributions
1838:enter \emph{additively} in the effective superpotential,
1839:\begin{equation*}
1840:  W_{\text{mon}} \sim \zeta\,\bigl(\sqrt{m_1} + \sqrt{m_2} + \sqrt{m_3}\bigr)\,,
1841:\end{equation*}
1842:where $\zeta \propto e^{-S_0/3}$ is the monopole fugacity. This is
1843:\emph{exactly} the sum-of-square-roots structure of the vacuum charge
1844:$v_0 = (\sum \sqrt{m_k})/3$.
1845:The $v_0$-doubling then maps to the counting of active
1846:monopole-instanton contributions: the seed (two active flavors) involves
1847:two monopole amplitudes, while the bloom (three active flavors, one with
1848:a sign flip) involves three, doubling $v_0$.
1849:
1850:This mechanism is non-holomorphic in the sense required: the
1851:K\"ahler potential receives monopole--anti-monopole (bion) cross terms
1852:of the form $|\!\sum s_k\sqrt{m_k}|^2$, where $m_k$ are the
1853:current quark masses. Expanding for $N_c = 3$:
1854:\begin{equation*}
1855:  K_{\text{bion}} = \frac{\zeta^2}{\Lambda^2}\bigl(
1856:  m_1 + m_2 + m_3
1857:  + 2s_1 s_2\sqrt{m_1 m_2}
1858:  + 2s_1 s_3\sqrt{m_1 m_3}
1859:  + 2s_2 s_3\sqrt{m_2 m_3}\bigr)\,,
1860:\end{equation*}
1861:where the diagonal terms $s_k^2 m_k = m_k$ are self-bions and the
1862:cross terms $\sqrt{m_i m_j}$ are mixed bion amplitudes---their
1863:geometric mean structure is what produces $\sum\sqrt{m_k}$.
1864:Note that $m_k$ here are the quark masses (superpotential couplings),
1865:\emph{not} the meson VEVs $M^k{}_k \propto 1/m_k$ of the Seiberg
1866:seesaw; the bion amplitude involves quark zero modes, not meson fields.
1867:Organizing the bion amplitudes into
1868:seed-sector ($S_{\text{seed}} = \sqrt{m_s} + \sqrt{m_c}$) and
1869:bloom-sector ($S_{\text{bloom}} = -\!\sqrt{m_s} + \sqrt{m_c} + \sqrt{m_b}$)
1870:channels, the K\"ahler correction takes the form
1871:\begin{equation*}
1872:  \delta K \sim \lambda_1\,|S_{\text{seed}}|^2
1873:  + \lambda_2\,|S_{\text{bloom}} - 2\,S_{\text{seed}}|^2\,.
1874:\end{equation*}
1875:The second term is a perfect square that vanishes
1876:\emph{at the minimum} when $S_{\text{bloom}} = 2\,S_{\text{seed}}$,
1877:i.e., $\sqrt{m_b} = 3\sqrt{m_s} + \sqrt{m_c}$---the
1878:$v_0$-doubling condition. The relation between bion couplings
1879:($\lambda_3 = -4\lambda_2$ for cross-channel bions) is what
1880:enforces the doubling. Numerically,
1881:$S_{\text{bloom}} - 2\,S_{\text{seed}} = 0.023\MeV^{1/2}$
1882:(effectively zero: $m_b = 4{,}177$ vs.\ PDG $4{,}180$).
1883:The Hessian of $\lambda_2\,|S_{\text{bloom}} - 2\,S_{\text{seed}}|^2$
1884:has rank~1: its single nonzero eigenvector is
1885:$\partial f/\partial m_i$ (the gradient of
1886:$f = \sqrt{m_b} - 3\sqrt{m_s} - \sqrt{m_c}$), while the two flat
1887:directions correspond to independent variation of $m_s$ and $m_c$
1888:at fixed $f$.  Thus the bion K\"ahler constrains exactly \emph{one}
1889:mass combination; the remaining two masses are fixed by the Koide
1890:seed in the superpotential. Including propagated uncertainties
1891:($\delta m_s = 0.8\MeV$, $\delta m_c = 20\MeV$), the prediction
1892:is $m_b = 4{,}177 \pm 40\MeV$, consistent with the PDG value
1893:$4{,}180 \pm 30\MeV$ at $0.06\sigma$.
1894:
1895:The principal caveat is that this analysis
1896:applies on $\mathbb{R}^3 \times S^1$; connecting to $\mathbb{R}^4$
1897:physics requires \"Unsal's continuity conjecture~\cite{Unsal2012},
1898:which posits that center-symmetric confinement at small $S^1$ is
1899:continuously connected to the large-$S^1$ confining vacuum.
1900:The conjecture is supported by lattice evidence for $SU(N)$ pure
1901:Yang--Mills and by the absence of phase transitions in the
1902:center-stabilized theory, but remains unproven for theories with
1903:matter.
1904:
1905:The identification $\langle F_{\Phi_0}\rangle = f \sim \fpi^2$ is the
1906:sBootstrap's central claim: the SUSY-breaking $F$-term is the chiral
1907:condensate parameter.
1908:
1909:In SQCD, this is realized dynamically rather than through a 
1910:superpotential: the chiral condensate $\qqbar \neq 0$ acts as the 
1911:nonzero $F$-term, the impossibility of restoring chiral symmetry 
1912:in the QCD vacuum is the dynamical analogue of $f \neq 0$, and the 
1913:pion---the would-be Goldstone boson that acquires mass from explicit 
1914:chiral breaking---plays the role of $\Phi_0$ whose mass is lifted 
1915:by loop corrections (here, by quark masses).

1917:\subsection{The Goldstino direction and the Volkov--Akulov realization}
1918:\label{subsec:va}
1919:
1920:When SUSY is spontaneously broken, a massless fermion appears: the 
1921:Goldstino, the fermionic analogue of the Goldstone boson. Its decay 
1922:constant $f_{\text{VA}}$ sets the SUSY-breaking scale through the 
1923:Volkov--Akulov Lagrangian \cite{VolkovAkulov1973}:
1924:\begin{equation}
1925:\mathcal{L}_{\text{VA}} = -f_{\text{VA}}^2 \det\!\left(
1926:\delta_\mu^\nu + \frac{i}{f_{\text{VA}}^2}\,\bar{\lambda}\bar{\sigma}^\nu
1927:\partial_\mu\lambda\right),
1928:\end{equation}
1929:where $\lambda$ is the Goldstino field. At low energies, the Goldstino 
1930:couples derivatively with strength $\sim 1/f_{\text{VA}}^2$, exactly 
1931:as the pion couples with strength $\sim 1/f_\pi^2$ in the chiral 
1932:Lagrangian.
1933:
1934:The sBootstrap identification is:
1935:\begin{equation}
1936:f_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi\,, \qquad 
1937:\text{Goldstino} = \text{muon (partner of }\pi\text{)}\,.
1938:\end{equation}
1939:More precisely, the muon is a \emph{pseudo-Goldstino}: in the 
1940:limit $m_q \to 0$ where the pion becomes exactly massless (a true 
1941:Goldstone boson), its fermionic partner also becomes massless (a 
1942:true Goldstino). The finite quark masses explicitly break both 
1943:chiral symmetry (giving $m_\pi \neq 0$) and the sBootstrap SUSY 
1944:(giving $m_\mu \neq m_\pi$), with the splitting controlled by 
1945:$f_\pi$:
1946:\begin{equation}
1947:m_\pi^2 - m_\mu^2 = f_\pi^2\,.
1948:\end{equation}
1949:
1950:This is the Volkov--Akulov relation for the pseudo-Goldstino: the 
1951:mass splitting between the scalar (pion) and its fermionic partner 
1952:(muon) is set by the SUSY-breaking scale squared.
1953:
1954:The VA mechanism is not a separate breaking from the F-term: it is 
1955:the \emph{low-energy, composite-level description} of the same 
1956:breaking. At the microscopic level, $\langle F\rangle \neq 0$ 
1957:in the meson superfield; at the composite level, this manifests 
1958:as the Goldstino coupling with $f_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi$. These 
1959:two quantities---the Goldstino decay constant and the pion decay 
1960:constant---are \emph{identified} in the sBootstrap; this is the 
1961:central claim, not a derivation.
1962:
1963:The identification should not be confused with the standard relation
1964:between the $F$-term VEV and the chiral condensate. In standard
1965:SQCD,
1966:%
1967:\begin{equation*}
1968:\langle F\rangle \sim \frac{|\qqbar|}{\fpi} \sim
1969:\frac{(250\MeV)^3}{92\MeV} \approx 1.7\times 10^5\MeV^2\,,
1970:\end{equation*}
1971:%
1972:while $\fpi^2 \approx 8{,}500\MeV^2$---a factor of ${\sim}20$
1973:discrepancy. The GOR relation \cite{GOR1968},
1974:$m_\pi^2 \fpi^2 = -(m_u + m_d)\qqbar$, connects these through the
1975:small quark masses: the ratio $|\qqbar|/\fpi \sim 20$ is exactly
1976:$(m_u + m_d)^{-1} m_\pi^2 \fpi \approx 20$, so the discrepancy is
1977:the chiral suppression factor, not a tuning. The sBootstrap
1978:identification $f_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi$ is therefore a
1979:\emph{low-energy effective} statement: the breaking scale appearing
1980:in the meson--lepton mass relation is $f_\pi$ (the Goldstone decay
1981:constant), not the microscopic condensate $|\qqbar|^{1/3}$.
1982:
1983:The VA realization gives more than a mass formula---it organizes the 
1984:entire breaking narrative. In the chiral limit ($m_q \to 0$), both 
1985:pion and muon are exactly massless: the pion as the Goldstone boson 
1986:of broken chiral symmetry, the muon as the Goldstino of broken 
1987:sBootstrap SUSY. This is the \emph{exact seed}: the $B$-meson 
1988:direction sits at the flat direction ($\zch = 0$), and the 
1989:$\pi$--$\mu$ pair is massless. Turning on quark masses ($m_q \neq 0$) 
1990:does three things simultaneously:
1991:\begin{enumerate}
1992:\item Gives the pion its GOR mass (lifting the flat direction for $\pi$).
1993:\item Gives the muon its mass (the pseudo-Goldstino acquires mass 
1994:when the explicit breaking parameter $m_q$ is nonzero).
1995:\item Generates the $B$-meson mass (the flat direction for $\Phi_0$ 
1996:is lifted by the Coleman--Weinberg potential, i.e., by the same 
1997:quark masses that feed the GOR formula).
1998:\end{enumerate}
1999:The splitting between (1) and (2) is $f_\pi^2$---the VA relation. 
2000:The seed-to-bloom transition of \S\ref{sec:two_tuples} is the same 
2001:physics seen from the charge-space perspective: $m_q \neq 0$ lifts 
2002:the zero, generates the $b$/$B$ mass, flips the sign of $\zch_s$ 
2003:and $\zch_\pi$, and produces the full triple. The VA equation 
2004:\eqref{eq:pimu} is not one fact among many; it is the generating 
2005:relation from which the bloom follows.
2006:
2007:A note on quantum numbers: the Goldstino of spontaneously broken 
2008:spacetime SUSY is a neutral fermion (the fermionic component of the 
2009:superfield whose $F$-term breaks SUSY). The muon carries electric 
2010:charge and lepton number. The sBootstrap SUSY, however, is not a 
2011:spacetime symmetry but an emergent hadronic symmetry---a 
2012:Miyazawa-type superalgebra \cite{Miyazawa} acting on composite 
2013:states. In this context, the ``Goldstino'' need not carry the 
2014:quantum numbers of a spacetime supercharge; it carries the quantum 
2015:numbers appropriate to the broken hadronic generator. The muon's 
2016:charge is inherited from the pion multiplet it partners.

2018:\subsection{D-term breaking: electromagnetic splittings}
2019:The unbroken $U(1)_{\text{em}}$ gauge symmetry allows a 
2020:Fayet--Iliopoulos (FI) term \cite{FayetIliopoulos}:
2021:\begin{equation}
2022:\mathcal{L}_{\text{FI}} = \xi\,D_{\text{em}}\,,
2023:\end{equation}
2024:which shifts scalar masses by $\delta m^2 = Q_{\text{em}}\,\xi$, 
2025:where $Q_{\text{em}}$ is the electric charge. This is the 
2026:supersymmetric version of the electromagnetic mass difference.
2027:
2028:In QCD without SUSY, the electromagnetic mass splitting of the 
2029:pion is given by the Das--Guralnik--Mathur--Low--Young (DGMLY) 
2030:formula \cite{DGMLY1967}:
2031:\begin{equation}
2032:m_{\pi^\pm}^2 - m_{\pi^0}^2 = \frac{3\alpha_{\text{em}}}{4\pi}\,
2033:m_\rho^2
2034:\approx (35.5\MeV)^2\,,
2035:\end{equation}
2036:which in the SUSY framework becomes a D-term contribution:
2037:\begin{equation}
2038:\xi \sim \frac{3\alpha_{\text{em}}}{4\pi}\,m_\rho^2 
2039:\sim (35\MeV)^2\,.
2040:\end{equation}
2041:
2042:In charge space, the D-term shifts $\zch$ by an amount proportional 
2043:to $Q_{\text{em}}$:
2044:\begin{equation}
2045:\zch \to \zch + \delta\zch\,, \qquad 
2046:\delta\zch \approx \frac{Q_{\text{em}}\,\xi}{2\zch}\,.
2047:\end{equation}
2048:For the pion ($Q_{\text{em}} = \pm 1$, $\zch \approx 11.8$): 
2049:$\delta\zch \approx 0.2\MeV^{1/2}$, matching the observed 
2050:$\zch_{\pi^\pm} - \zch_{\pi^0} = 11.82 - 11.62 = 0.20\MeV^{1/2}$.
2051:
2052:The D-term effect is subleading to the F-term: 
2053:$\xi \sim (35\MeV)^2$ versus $f_\pi^2 \sim (92\MeV)^2$. It 
2054:operates \emph{within} each supermultiplet entry, splitting charged 
2055:from neutral mesons, while the F-term splits bosons from fermions 
2056:across the supermultiplet.
2057:
2058:Witten's inequality \cite{Witten1983} guarantees 
2059:$m_{\pi^\pm} > m_{\pi^0}$ in QCD with electromagnetism---the 
2060:charged pion is always heavier. In the SUSY framework this 
2061:becomes the statement that the D-term contribution is positive 
2062:for $Q_{\text{em}} \neq 0$, consistent with the FI sign.

2064:\subsection{Mapping the breaking to the tuples}
2065:The tuple structure maps onto a layered breaking with two independent 
2066:mechanisms (F-term and D-term) plus a separate heavy-sector origin. 
2067:The Volkov--Akulov realization of \S\ref{subsec:va} is not a third 
2068:mechanism: it is the low-energy, composite-level description of the 
2069:same F-term breaking. The VA relation \eqref{eq:pimu} \emph{sets the 
2070:scale} ($f_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi$); the O'Raifeartaigh/SQCD dynamics 
2071:of \S\ref{subsec:fterm} \emph{provides the mechanism} 
2072:($\langle F\rangle \neq 0$).
2073:
2074:\paragraph{Layer 1: F-term/VA (chiral condensate, scale $\sim f_\pi$).}
2075:The chiral condensate $\qqbar \neq 0$ breaks both chiral symmetry 
2076:and the sBootstrap SUSY. This is the primary breaking, affecting 
2077:the light sector. The seed triples $(0, s, c)$ and $(0, \pi, D_s)$ 
2078:are the pre-breaking configurations; the massless member is the flat 
2079:direction whose $F$-term is forced nonzero by confinement. After 
2080:breaking:
2081:\begin{itemize}
2082:\item The zero member is lifted and acquires negative charge 
2083:($\zch < 0$), becoming the sign-flipped member of the full triple 
2084:($-s$, $-\pi$).
2085:\item The full triples $(-s,c,b)$ and $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ emerge, 
2086:incorporating the $b$-quark and $B$-meson sectors.
2087:\item The lepton triple $(e, \mu, \tau)$ is the fermionic shadow: 
2088:the leptons acquire masses through the same condensate, with the 
2089:muon as pseudo-Goldstino.
2090:\end{itemize}
2091:
2092:\paragraph{Layer 2: D-term (electromagnetism, scale $\sim \alpha_{\text{em}} m_\rho$).}
2093:The $U(1)_{\text{em}}$ FI term generates electromagnetic mass 
2094:splittings within each multiplet. This is a perturbative correction 
2095:on top of the F-term breaking, affecting only charged particles. 
2096:It does not create new Koide triples but shifts existing ones by 
2097:$O(\alpha_{\text{em}})$.
2098:
2099:\paragraph{Layer 3: The heavy sector (scale $\sim v_0^{(cbt)}$).}
2100:The $(c,b,t)$ triple at $v_0^2 \approx 29.6\GeV$ operates far 
2101:above the chiral scale. It has no seed (no massless member), no 
2102:meson shadow (top does not hadronize), and its breaking may be 
2103:connected to a different sector of the theory---possibly the 
2104:electroweak Higgs mechanism.
2105:
2106:This layered structure involves three distinct scales, each with 
2107:different physical meaning:
2108:\begin{align}
2109:\text{F-term (boson--fermion splitting):} &\quad 
2110:f_\pi \approx 92\MeV\,, \quad f_\pi^2 \approx 8{,}500\MeV^2\,; \\
2111:\text{D-term (EM fine structure):} &\quad 
2112:\xi^{1/2} \approx 35\MeV\,, \quad \xi \approx 1{,}200\MeV^2\,; \\
2113:\text{Heavy sector (Koide scale):} &\quad 
2114:v_0^{(cbt)} \approx 172\MeV^{1/2}\,, \quad (v_0^{(cbt)})^2 \approx 29{,}600\MeV\,.
2115:\end{align}
2116:Comparing the squared scales: $f_\pi^2 \gg \xi$ (F-term dominates 
2117:over D-term by a factor $\sim 7$, consistent with the D-term being 
2118:a perturbative $O(\alpha_{\text{em}})$ correction). The heavy-sector 
2119:scale $(v_0^{(cbt)})^2$ carries dimensions of $\MeV$ (not $\MeV^2$) 
2120:because $v_0$ is a charge ($\MeV^{1/2}$), not an energy; direct 
2121:comparison with $f_\pi$ requires specifying which physical quantity 
2122:is being compared.

2124:\subsection{The O'Raifeartaigh structure in SQCD}
2125:
2126:In Seiberg's SQCD with $N_f = N_c = 3$, the quantum constraint 
2127:\eqref{eq:seiberg} provides a natural setting for the 
2128:O'Raifeartaigh mechanism. The constraint $\det M - B\bar{B} = \Lambda^6$ 
2129:means the moduli space has a specific geometry: not all field directions 
2130:are flat. When quark masses are added via a tree-level superpotential 
2131:$W_{\text{tree}} = m_i M^i{}_i$ (sum over flavors), the $F$-terms become:
2132:\begin{equation}
2133:F_{M^i{}_j} = m_i\,\delta^i_j + \Lambda^6\,(\text{constraint terms})\,.
2134:\end{equation}
2135:
2136:For the seed triple $(0, s, c)$, the ``$0$'' member corresponds to a 
2137:direction in the meson matrix where the tree-level mass vanishes---the 
2138:entry involving the lightest quark combination. In the exact chiral 
2139:limit ($m_u = m_d = 0$), this entry is truly massless, and the 
2140:constraint \eqref{eq:seiberg} forces $\langle F\rangle \neq 0$ for 
2141:that entry. This is the dynamical O'Raifeartaigh mechanism:
2142:confinement (encoded in $\Lambda^6$) plays the role of the parameter
2143:$f$ in the superpotential, and the impossibility of setting all
2144:$F$-terms to zero is a consequence of the strong dynamics rather
2145:than a choice of superpotential.

2147:\paragraph{The pseudo-modulus and the Koide seed.}
2148:In the ISS magnetic dual, the tree-level superpotential contains a
2149:Yukawa coupling $h\,\mathrm{Tr}(q\,M\,\tilde q)$ between the magnetic
2150:quarks $q$, $\tilde q$ and the meson matrix $M$. The O'Raifeartaigh
2151:parameters map as $g \to h$, $m \to h\mu$, $v \to \langle X\rangle$
2152:(the pseudo-modulus VEV), so the seed Koide condition
2153:$gv/m = \sqrt{3}$ becomes $\langle X\rangle/\mu = \sqrt{3}$---the
2154:magnetic Yukawa~$h$ cancels. The Koide seed fixes the
2155:pseudo-modulus VEV, not the coupling~$h$.
2156:At one-loop Coleman--Weinberg level $\langle X\rangle = 0$; the
2157:mechanism that generates the nonzero VEV
2158:$\langle X\rangle = \sqrt{3}\,\mu$ required by the seed
2159:remains an open question (\S\ref{sec:open}).
2160:
2161:The ISS mechanism (Intriligator--Seiberg--Shih \cite{ISS2006}) 
2162:establishes metastable SUSY-breaking vacua for $N_c < N_f < 3N_c/2$, 
2163:where the rank condition in the magnetic dual prevents all $F$-terms 
2164:from vanishing. The sBootstrap case $N_f = N_c = 3$ is strictly 
2165:\emph{below} the ISS window---at $N_f = N_c$ the low-energy 
2166:description is qualitatively different, governed by the 
2167:quantum-modified constraint \eqref{eq:seiberg} on the moduli space 
2168:rather than by a free magnetic dual. However, physical QCD has
2169:$N_f = 3$ light flavors and $N_f = 4$--$5$ if charm and bottom are
2170:included at their respective thresholds. With charm included,
2171:$N_f = 4$ falls within the ISS window ($3 < 4 < 4.5$); with both
2172:charm and bottom, $N_f = 5$ falls in the conformal window
2173:($4.5 < 5 < 9$), where the Seiberg seesaw constraint does not apply
2174:directly. The sBootstrap's multi-scale structure---with
2175:the seed triples involving $s$, $c$, and $b$ quarks at different
2176:scales---requires treating the effective $N_f$ as
2177:scale-dependent: $N_f = 3$ below $m_c$ (confining, Seiberg
2178:constraint), $N_f = 4$ above $m_c$ (ISS metastable SUSY breaking),
2179:and $N_f = 5$ above $m_b$ (conformal window).
2180:Note that $N_f = 4$ is the \emph{unique} integer satisfying
2181:$3 < N_f < 4.5$ for $N_c = 3$, providing a dynamical rationale for
2182:exactly four light flavors $(u,d,s,c)$ in the SUSY-breaking sector.
2183:
2184:\paragraph{ISS vacuum lifetime.}
2185:The metastable ISS vacuum decays to the SUSY-preserving ground state
2186:by tunneling with bounce action
2187:$S_{\text{bounce}} \sim (8\pi^2/3)\,N_c\,(\Lambda_{\text{mag}}/h\mu)^4$,
2188:where $\mu$ is the lightest quark mass controlling the tunneling.
2189:For $\mu = m_s = 93\MeV$, $h \sim O(1)$, and
2190:$\Lambda_{\text{mag}} \sim 300\MeV$:
2191:$S_{\text{bounce}} \sim 10^2$--$10^3$ (depending on~$h$),
2192:giving a lifetime exceeding the age of the universe by hundreds of
2193:orders of magnitude for any $h$ of order unity.
2194:The metastable vacuum is cosmologically acceptable.

2196:\paragraph{The Seiberg seesaw and dual Koide.}
2197:At the Seiberg vacuum with $B = \bar B = 0$, the diagonal meson VEVs
2198:scale as $\langle M^j{}_j\rangle \propto 1/m_j$: heavy quarks produce
2199:light mesons and vice versa. This seesaw prompts a dual question: does
2200:the inverse-mass spectrum $\{1/m_i\}$ satisfy a Koide condition?
2201:Evaluating $Q = \sum(1/m_i)/(\sum\sqrt{1/m_i})^2$ for the pure
2202:down-type quarks $(d,s,b)$:
2203:\begin{equation*}
2204:  Q(1/m_d,\, 1/m_s,\, 1/m_b) = 0.6652\,,
2205:\end{equation*}
2206:a 0.22\% deviation from $2/3$---better than any direct quark Koide
2207:triple. No other quark triple comes close under
2208:the Seiberg seesaw (the next best, $(d,s,c)$, deviates by 4.2\%).
2209:This ``dual Koide'' on $(d,s,b)$ (first reported here) connects
2210:the UV mass hierarchy to the IR meson VEVs: the Seiberg constraint
2211:maps the down-type quark masses into a near-Koide spectrum of
2212:meson condensates. However, the
2213:result is sensitive to $m_d$: within $\pm 1\sigma$ ($m_d = 4.67
2214:\pm 0.48\MeV$), $Q$ ranges from $0.655$ to $0.676$, and the deviation
2215:from $2/3$ varies from $0.2\%$ to $1.7\%$. The dual Koide is an
2216:observation at the edge of the current $m_d$ uncertainty.
2217:
2218:A simple algebraic identity connects the seed to the seesaw:
2219:for any two masses, the Koide ratio
2220:$Q(a,b) = (a+b)/(\sqrt{a}+\sqrt{b})^2$ satisfies
2221:$Q(a,b) = Q(1/a,\,1/b)$ identically.
2222:The seed $(0, m_s, m_c)$ sits at this self-dual point:
2223:its Koide ratio is the same whether evaluated on quark masses or
2224:on Seiberg-dual meson VEVs $\propto 1/m_i$.  Thus the seed Koide
2225:in the electric (UV) theory is \emph{automatically} a seed Koide
2226:in the magnetic (IR) theory---the Seiberg duality preserves the
2227:seed condition without fine-tuning.
2228:For the full bloomed triple $(-s,c,b)$, self-duality breaks:
2229:$Q(-s,c,b) \neq Q(1/m_s,1/m_c,1/m_b)$ in general.
2230:The dual Koide $Q(1/m_d,1/m_s,1/m_b) = 0.665$ is therefore a
2231:\emph{nontrivial} constraint on the down-type sector, not a
2232:tautological restatement of the seed.
2233:Among all $\binom{5}{3} = 10$ quark triples, $(d,s,b)$ is the
2234:unique triple with inverse-mass Koide ratio within 1\% of $2/3$;
2235:the next closest is $(d,s,c)$ at 4.2\% deviation---a 19:1 margin.

2286:$\det M - B\bar{B} = \Lambda^6$ (appropriately generalized to 
2287:$5 \times 5$), combined with the SUSY-breaking dynamics, produces

2291:\subsection{The energy balance is not an RG attractor}
2292:\label{subsec:fixedpoint}
2293:One might hope that the energy-balance condition
2294:$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ is a \emph{dynamical attractor}:
2295:that three masses evolving under SUSY-breaking dynamics flow toward
2296:the Koide surface $Q = 2/3$. However, the one-loop soft-mass RG
2297:equations in $\mathcal{N}=1$ SQCD with universal Casimirs and
2298:negligible Yukawas give $dm_i/dt = \gamma(M_g^2 - m_i)$, which
2299:drives all masses to a common value $m_i \to M_g^2$, yielding
2300:$Q \to 1/3$---the \emph{opposite} extreme. The Koide preservation
2301:condition $\sum_i F_i(3 - 2S/\sqrt{m_i}) = 0$ on the $Q = 2/3$
2302:surface is algebraically incompatible with any uniform attractor
2303:flow (by the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality, the required identity
2304:$S\sum 1/\sqrt{m_i} = 9/2$ contradicts $S\sum 1/\sqrt{m_i} \geq 9$).
2305:
2306:The energy-balance condition therefore does \emph{not} arise from
2307:simple RG running. It must instead be a \emph{boundary condition}
2308:on the superpotential---as in the O'Raifeartaigh realization of
2309:\S\ref{subsec:fterm}, where $gv = \sqrt{3}\,m$ produces the exact
2310:Koide seed. The condition is set at the scale of SUSY breaking and
2311:then preserved by the QCD-invariant mass ratios, rather than being
2312:an RG attractor.
2313:
2314:\subsection{The exact seed and what breaks it}
2315:\label{subsec:exactseed}
2316:
2317:The seed configuration $(0, \zch_a, \zch_b)$ requires \emph{two} 
2318:independent conditions:
2319:\begin{enumerate}
2320:\item One member at $\zch = 0$ exactly (the flat direction).
2321:\item The ratio of the massive members equals $2 - \sqrt{3}$:
2322:\begin{equation}
2323:\frac{\sqrt{m_a}}{\sqrt{m_b}} = 2 - \sqrt{3} \approx 0.26795\,.
2324:\end{equation}
2325:\end{enumerate}
2326:These are logically distinct. The chiral limit ($m_u = m_d = 0$, 
2327:$\qqbar \neq 0$) enforces condition (1) for the meson sector: the 
2328:pion is an exact Goldstone boson, $m_\pi = 0$, and in the 
2329:picture of \S\ref{subsec:fterm} the $B$-meson direction sits at 
2330:the flat direction before acquiring mass. But the chiral limit says 
2331:\emph{nothing} about condition (2)---it does not constrain 
2332:$\sqrt{m_{D_s}}/\sqrt{m_B}$ or $\sqrt{m_s}/\sqrt{m_c}$ 
2333:to equal $2 - \sqrt{3}$.
2334:
2335:The physical deviations from the exact ratio are small but 
2336:measurable:
2337:
2338:\begin{center}
2339:\begin{tabular}{lccc}
2340:\toprule
2341:Seed & Exact ratio & Physical ratio & Deviation \\
2342:\midrule
2343:$(0, \pi, D_s)$ & 0.2680 & 
2344:$\sqrt{m_\pi}/\sqrt{m_{D_s}} = 0.2663$ & $-0.6\%$ \\
2345:$(0, s, c)$ & 0.2680 & 
2346:$\sqrt{m_s}/\sqrt{m_c} = 0.2712$ & $+1.2\%$ \\
2347:$(0 \approx e, \mu, \tau)$ & 0.2680 & 
2348:$\sqrt{m_\mu}/\sqrt{m_\tau} = 0.2439$ & $-9.0\%$ \\
2349:\bottomrule
2350:\end{tabular}
2351:\end{center}
2352:
2353:The meson seed is closest to the exact ratio (0.6\% deviation). 
2354:The quark seed deviates by 1.2\%. A natural thought is that QCD 
2355:running might shift the quark ratio: perhaps at some scale $\mu^*$ 
2356:the ratio $\sqrt{m_s(\mu^*)}/\sqrt{m_c(\mu^*)}$ hits $2 - \sqrt{3}$ 
2357:exactly. But this is \emph{not possible within QCD alone}. At leading 
2358:order, all quark masses run with the same anomalous dimension 
2359:$\gamma_m$:
2360:\begin{equation}
2361:m_i(\mu) = m_i(\mu_0)\left(\frac{\alpha_s(\mu)}{\alpha_s(\mu_0)}\right)^{\gamma_m/\beta_0},
2362:\end{equation}
2363:so the ratio $m_s(\mu)/m_c(\mu)$---and therefore 
2364:$\sqrt{m_s}/\sqrt{m_c}$---is an RG invariant of QCD. Higher-order 
2365:corrections break this invariance, but only at the $O(\alpha_s^2)$ 
2366:level, far too small to account for 1.2\%.
2367:
2368:The quark seed deviation is therefore \emph{intrinsic}: the 
2369:$\overline{\text{MS}}$ mass ratio $m_s/m_c$ is a fixed number 
2370:(at any QCD scale), and it misses the Koide target by 1.2\%. 
2371:Three possible explanations present themselves:
2372:
2373:\paragraph{Electroweak running.} Above the electroweak scale, 
2374:quark masses are controlled by Yukawa couplings $y_i$ to the 
2375:Higgs, which \emph{do} run differently for different flavors: 
2376:the $\beta$-functions for $y_s$ and $y_c$ involve different 
2377:electroweak quantum numbers ($SU(2)_L$ doublet structure, 
2378:hypercharge). The ratio $y_s/y_c$ evolves with scale, and 
2379:there may exist a scale $\mu^*$ above the electroweak 
2380:threshold where $\sqrt{y_s(\mu^*)}/\sqrt{y_c(\mu^*)} = 2 - \sqrt{3}$. 
2381:This would identify the ``seed scale'' as an electroweak-sector 
2382:phenomenon, not a QCD one---consistent with the sBootstrap's 
2383:identification of the breaking with $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ 
2384:rather than $SU(3)_c$.
2385:
2386:\paragraph{Charge running.} Even if mass ratios are QCD-invariant,
2387:the Koide decomposition $\zch_k = z_0 + z_k$ may have its own
2388:flow. The Koide scale $v_0 = z_0^2$ sets the common scale,
2389:and the fluctuations $z_k$ encode the deviations. As shown in
2390:\S\ref{subsec:fixedpoint}, simple RG running drives $Q \to 1/3$
2391:rather than $2/3$, so the energy-balance condition cannot be an
2392:attractor in the usual sense. However, the O'Raifeartaigh
2393:realization (\S\ref{subsec:fterm}) shows that the condition is a
2394:boundary condition on the superpotential ($gv = \sqrt{3}\,m$).
2395:The 1.2\% deviation of the quark seed ratio may then reflect
2396:the distance between the physical quark masses and the exact
2397:superpotential constraint.
2398:
2399:\paragraph{Meson vs.\ quark deviations.} The meson deviation 
2400:(0.6\%) is smaller than the quark deviation (1.2\%), and for 
2401:a different reason: meson masses are \emph{not} simple functions 
2402:of quark mass ratios. The pion mass is given by the GOR 
2403:relation $m_\pi^2 f_\pi^2 = -(m_u + m_d)\qqbar$; the $D_s$ 
2404:mass involves heavy-quark effective theory; and both receive 
2405:chiral perturbation theory corrections. These nonperturbative 
2406:QCD effects are the dominant source of the meson seed deviation, 
2407:and are in principle calculable at NLO in ChPT.
2408:
2409:\paragraph{A remarkable feature of the VA relation.} The 
2410:foundational equation $m_\pi^2 - m_\mu^2 = f_\pi^2$ contains 
2411:no QCD coupling constant: no $\alpha_s$, no $N_c$, no 
2412:$\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$. It is a pure mass relation among three 
2413:experimentally measured quantities---two masses and a decay 
2414:constant---holding to 2.2\%. In the sBootstrap, this is natural: 
2415:the relation is a SUSY mass splitting, set by the breaking 
2416:scale $f_\pi$, and SUSY mass relations do not involve the gauge 
2417:coupling of the confining theory. Standard QCD \emph{explains} 
2418:$f_\pi$ in terms of $\qqbar$ and $\alpha_s$; the sBootstrap 
2419:\emph{uses} $f_\pi$ as the fundamental parameter, with QCD 
2420:providing the dynamical origin but not appearing in the mass 
2421:formula. The absence of QCD factors in \eqref{eq:pimu} is a 
2422:hint that the relevant symmetry is not $SU(3)_c$ but the 
2423:hadronic superalgebra that connects pions to leptons.
2424:
2425:The lepton ``seed'' has the worst ratio match ($-9\%$), which 
2426:is consistent with the observation that the lepton triple is 
2427:\emph{not} a true seed: $m_e \neq 0$, and the electron was never 
2428:at the flat direction. The leptons are an ``almost-seed'' whose 
2429:excellent $Q = 0.66666$ arises not from the seed structure but 
2430:from the smallness of $m_e$, which makes the two conditions 
2431:approximately degenerate. For the leptons, condition (1) is 
2432:approximate rather than exact, and condition (2) is correspondingly 
2433:relaxed.

2435:\subsection{What the $(c,b,t)$ triple tells us}
2436:
2437:The $(c,b,t)$ triple ($Q = 0.6695$, $v_0^2 = 29.6\GeV$) has 
2438:neither a seed nor a meson shadow. It operates at a scale far 
2439:above $\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$, suggesting its origin is different:
2440:
2441:\begin{enumerate}
2442:\item It may be governed by the electroweak Higgs mechanism rather 
2443:than the chiral condensate. The top Yukawa $y_t \approx 1$ is 
2444:``natural'' in the sense that $m_t \approx v_{\text{EW}}/\sqrt{2}$; 
2445:the Koide condition on $(c,b,t)$ would then constrain the Yukawa 
2446:coupling matrix.
2447:\item It may represent a Volkov--Akulov realization at the 
2448:electroweak scale: if there is a second SUSY breaking at 
2449:$f' \sim v_{\text{EW}}$, the $(c,b,t)$ triple would be its 
2450:analog of the $(\pi, \mu)$ system, but with $f'$ replacing $f_\pi$.
2451:\item Or it may be a purely kinematic accident---the quark masses 
2452:happen to satisfy the Koide condition without a dynamical reason. 
2453:The 0.42\% precision makes this unlikely but not impossible.
2454:\end{enumerate}
2455:
2456:Interpretation (1) is most natural within the sBootstrap: the
2457:$(c,b,t)$ triple involves quarks whose masses are dominated by
2458:Yukawa couplings to the Higgs, not by the chiral condensate.
2459:Since $m_i = y_i\,v_{\text{EW}}/\sqrt{2}$, the Koide condition
2460:$Q = 2/3$ translates into a constraint on the Yukawa couplings
2461:(see \S\ref{subsec:superpotential}):
2462:$\sum y_i/(\sum \sqrt{y_i})^2 = 2/3$.
2463:With $y_t \approx 1$ fixed by the top mass, this determines
2464:$y_b/y_t$ and $y_c/y_t$ through the Koide parametrization.
2465:The chiral sector and the electroweak sector would each
2466:independently produce a Koide-type energy balance, with the charm
2467:and bottom quarks serving as the bridge between the two.
2468:
2469:\paragraph{CKM mixing and the Koide triples.}
2470:A striking feature of the quark Koide triples is that they
2471:\emph{obligatorily mix up-type and down-type quarks}: $(-s,c,b)$
2472:contains one up-type ($c$) and two down-type ($s,b$), while
2473:$(c,b,t)$ contains two up-type ($c,t$) and one down-type ($b$). An
2474:exhaustive scan of all $\binom{6}{3} = 20$ quark triples with all
2475:sign choices confirms that \emph{every} triple close to $Q = 2/3$ is
2476:mixed---neither the pure down-type $(d,s,b)$ nor the pure up-type
2477:$(u,c,t)$ comes close ($Q = 0.73$ and $Q = 0.85$ respectively).
2478:This mixing of mass eigenvalues from different Yukawa sectors is
2479:puzzling in the Standard Model, where up-type and down-type masses are
2480:eigenvalues of independent matrices $Y_u$ and $Y_d$. It is natural,
2481:however, in an $SU(5)_f$ flavor-symmetric framework where all five
2482:quarks are treated democratically.
2483:
2484:The Koide constraints on the two overlapping triples, together with
2485:the seed condition on $(0,s,c)$, determine $(s,c,b)$ from a single
2486:input ($m_t$). The unconstrained masses are $m_u$ and $m_d$---the
2487:first generation. The Oakes relation $\tan\theta_C \approx
2488:\sqrt{m_d/m_s}$ connects precisely these free parameters to the
2489:Cabibbo angle, suggesting that the CKM matrix encodes the residual
2490:freedom left undetermined by the Koide structure.
2491:
2492:If interpretation (2) is also invoked, the sBootstrap has two breaking 
2493:scales:
2494:\begin{equation}
2495:f_{\text{chiral}} = f_\pi \approx 92\MeV\,, \qquad
2496:f_{\text{EW}} \sim v_{\text{EW}} \approx 246\GeV\,,
2497:\end{equation}
2498:and the full spectrum requires both. The light sector (leptons, 
2499:light mesons) is governed by $f_\pi$; the heavy sector (top, and 
2500:by extension charm and bottom through the Koide network) is 
2501:governed by $v_{\text{EW}}$. The charm and bottom quarks, 
2502:appearing in both the $(0,s,c)/(-s,c,b)$ light-sector triples 
2503:and the $(c,b,t)$ heavy-sector triple, are the ``bridges'' 
2504:between the two scales.

2506:\subsection{Toward a Superpotential}
2507:\label{subsec:superpotential}
2508:
2509:Consider $\mathcal{N}=1$ SQCD with gauge group $SU(3)_c$ and $N_f=5$ flavors
2510:of chiral superfields $Q^i$, $\bar Q_j$ ($i,j=1\ldots 5$, corresponding to
2511:$u,d,s,c,b$) in the fundamental and antifundamental representations. The
2512:tree-level superpotential is
2513:%
2514:\begin{equation*}
2515:  W_{\text{tree}} = \operatorname{Tr}(\hat m\, M)\,,
2516:  \qquad
2517:  \hat m = \operatorname{diag}(m_u,\, m_d,\, m_s,\, m_c,\, m_b)\,,
2518:\end{equation*}
2519:%
2520:where $M^i{}_j = Q^i\cdot\bar Q_j$ is the $5\times 5$ composite meson
2521:superfield. Since $m_c, m_b \gg \Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$, the charm and
2522:bottom quarks are integrated out at tree level, reducing the effective
2523:theory to $N_f = N_c = 3$ light flavors $(u,d,s)$. In this regime,
2524:Seiberg's exact results give confinement with the composite meson
2525:$M^{(3)}$, baryons $B$, $\bar B$, and the quantum-modified
2526:constraint~\eqref{eq:seiberg}:
2527:%
2528:\begin{equation*}
2529:  \det M^{(3)} - B\bar B = \Lambda_{\text{eff}}^{6}\,,
2530:\end{equation*}
2531:%
2532:where $\Lambda_{\text{eff}}$ is the dynamical scale of the effective
2533:three-flavor theory after threshold matching at $m_c$ and $m_b$.
2534:Implementing this constraint via a Lagrange multiplier $X$ gives the full
2535:effective superpotential
2536:%
2537:\begin{equation*}
2538:  W_{\text{eff}}
2539:  = \operatorname{Tr}(\hat m^{(3)}\, M^{(3)})
2540:  + X\!\left(\det M^{(3)} - B\bar B - \Lambda_{\text{eff}}^{6}\right).
2541:\end{equation*}
2542:
2543:The $F$-term equations for the $3\times 3$ light-flavor block read
2544:%
2545:\begin{align*}
2546:  \frac{\partial W_{\text{eff}}}{\partial M^i{}_j}
2547:  &= \hat m_j\,\delta^i{}_j
2548:    + X\,(\det M^{(3)})\,(M^{(3)\,-1})_i{}^j = 0\,,\\[4pt]
2549:  \frac{\partial W_{\text{eff}}}{\partial B}
2550:  &= -X\,\bar B = 0\,,\\[4pt]
2551:  \frac{\partial W_{\text{eff}}}{\partial \bar B}
2552:  &= -X\, B = 0\,.
2553:\end{align*}
2554:%
2555:The baryon equations force either $X=0$ or $B=\bar B=0$. If $X=0$ the meson
2556:equation reduces to $\hat m_j\,\delta^i{}_j=0$, which has no solution for
2557:nonzero quark masses. Hence $B=\bar B=0$ and the constraint becomes
2558:$\det M^{(3)} = \Lambda_{\text{eff}}^6$. The diagonal meson equation then requires
2559:%
2560:\begin{equation*}
2561:  \hat m_j + X\,\frac{\det M^{(3)}}{M^j{}_j} = 0
2562:  \qquad(\text{no sum on } j)\,,
2563:\end{equation*}
2564:%
2565:which determines each diagonal entry of $M^{(3)}$ in terms of the quark masses and
2566:$\Lambda_{\text{eff}}$. For a seed configuration in which one quark mass vanishes---say
2567:$m_u\to 0$---the corresponding $F$-term
2568:$F_{M^1{}_1}=X\,(\det M^{(3)})(M^{(3)\,-1})_1{}^1$ remains nonzero so long as
2569:$\det M^{(3)}\neq 0$, which is guaranteed by the quantum constraint. Supersymmetry
2570:is therefore spontaneously broken, with order parameter
2571:%
2572:\begin{equation*}
2573:  \langle F \rangle \;\sim\; \fpi^2\,.
2574:\end{equation*}
2575:
2576:The scalar and fermionic components of $M^i{}_j$ acquire different masses
2577:through this breaking. The scalar component (pseudoscalar meson $q_i\bar q_j$)
2578:picks up an $F$-term contribution absent for the fermionic component (lepton,
2579:via the Miyazawa superalgebra):
2580:%
2581:\begin{align*}
2582:  m^2_{M^i{}_j}\big|_{\text{scalar}}
2583:  &= |m_i + m_j|^2 + f^2\,,\\[3pt]
2584:  m^2_{M^i{}_j}\big|_{\text{fermion}}
2585:  &= |m_i + m_j|^2\,,
2586:\end{align*}
2587:%
2588:where $f^2\approx \fpi^2$. The individual masses $|m_i + m_j|$ are
2589:the tree-level (perturbative) contributions; the physical masses are
2590:dominated by nonperturbative QCD effects that shift both meson and
2591:lepton by the same amount.  The mass \emph{splitting}, however,
2592:is universal across the multiplet:
2593:%
2594:\begin{equation*}
2595:  m^2_{\text{meson}} - m^2_{\text{lepton}} = f^2 \approx \fpi^2\,,
2596:\end{equation*}
2597:%
2598:because $f^2$ is a SUSY-breaking contribution independent of the QCD
2599:dynamics.  This is precisely the Volkov--Akulov
2600:relation~\eqref{eq:pimu}.
2601:For heavier mesons the actual splitting is strongly flavor-dependent
2602:(\S\ref{sec:open}, ``kaon--muon puzzle'').
2603:
2604:The top quark, with $m_t\gg\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$, does not hadronize and
2605:therefore does not participate in the confined meson matrix---the counting
2606:$N_f=5$ is exact. Its mass, and corrections to the heavy-quark sector
2607:($c$, $b$), originate instead from electroweak symmetry breaking. Introducing
2608:a Higgs doublet $H$, one writes a Yukawa superpotential
2609:%
2610:\begin{equation*}
2611:  W_{\text{Yukawa}} = y_{ij}\, H\, Q^i\, \bar Q_j\,,
2612:\end{equation*}
2613:%
2614:which is relevant only for quarks whose masses are dominated by the
2615:electroweak condensate rather than the chiral condensate. For the light sector
2616:($u,d,s$) the Yukawa contribution is negligible compared to
2617:$\operatorname{Tr}(\hat m\, M)$. Note that $\hat m$ in the chiral
2618:mass term already \emph{is} the current quark mass generated by the
2619:Higgs mechanism; the two terms are not additive.  The Yukawa
2620:superpotential is written separately to make explicit the dependence
2621:on the Higgs field $H$, which is needed for the heavy sector where
2622:$m_i \gg \Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$ and the mass cannot be attributed to
2623:chiral symmetry breaking. (In a full $\mathcal{N}=1$ treatment,
2624:holomorphy of $W$ requires separate doublets $H_u$ and $H_d$ for
2625:up-type and down-type quarks; we write a single~$H$ because the
2626:Koide condition constrains only the diagonal Yukawa eigenvalues,
2627:not the doublet structure.)
2628:
2629:The Koide condition on the $(c,b,t)$ triple constrains the diagonal
2630:Yukawa matrix $Y = \operatorname{diag}(y_c,y_b,y_t)$ with
2631:$y_i = \sqrt{2}\,m_i/v_{\text{EW}}$. Since $Q$ is scale-invariant,
2632:the Koide relation reads
2633:%
2634:\begin{equation*}
2635:  \frac{y_c + y_b + y_t}
2636:       {(\sqrt{y_c}+\sqrt{y_b}+\sqrt{y_t})^2}
2637:  = \frac{2}{3}\,,
2638:\end{equation*}
2639:%
2640:which is equivalent to writing the square roots of the Yukawa
2641:couplings as
2642:%
2643:\begin{equation*}
2644:  \sqrt{y_k} = \sqrt{y_0}\,
2645:  \bigl(1 + \sqrt{2}\,\cos(2\pi k/3 + \delta)\bigr)\,,
2646:  \qquad k = 0,1,2\,,
2647:\end{equation*}
2648:%
2649:where $y_0 = \bigl(\sum\sqrt{y_k}\bigr)^2\!/9$ and $\delta$ is
2650:the Koide phase. For the physical $(c,b,t)$ masses,
2651:$\delta \approx 0.069$ and $Q = 0.6695$ (a 0.42\% deviation from
2652:$2/3$). The traceless part of $\sqrt{Y}$ lies in the Cartan
2653:subalgebra of $SU(3)_f$:
2654:%
2655:\begin{equation*}
2656:  \sqrt{Y} = \sqrt{y_0}\,
2657:  \Bigl(I_3 + \tfrac{\sqrt{6}}{2}\,
2658:  \hat n\cdot\boldsymbol\lambda_{\text{diag}}\Bigr)\,,
2659:\end{equation*}
2660:%
2661:where $\boldsymbol\lambda_{\text{diag}} = (\lambda_3, \lambda_8)$
2662:are the diagonal Gell-Mann matrices and $\hat n$ is a unit vector
2663:in the Cartan plane determined by $\delta$. The Koide condition is
2664:therefore a constraint on the \emph{direction} of the Yukawa
2665:matrix in flavor space: the amplitude $\sqrt{6}/2$ is fixed, only
2666:$\hat n$ (equivalently $\delta$) is free.
2667:
2668:The total Lagrangian is
2669:%
2670:\begin{equation*}
2671:  \mathcal{L}
2672:  = \int\!d^4\theta\,K
2673:  + \left[\int\!d^2\theta\,W + \text{h.c.}\right]
2674:  + \mathcal{L}_D
2675:  + \mathcal{L}_{\text{soft}}\,,
2676:\end{equation*}
2677:%
2678:with K\"ahler potential (canonical plus bion correction),
2679:%
2680:\begin{equation*}
2681:  K = \underbrace{\operatorname{Tr}(M^\dagger M) + |X|^2 + |B|^2
2682:    + |\bar B|^2 + |H|^2}_{K_{\text{can}}}
2683:  + \underbrace{\frac{\zeta^2}{\Lambda^2}\,
2684:    \Bigl|\sum_{k=1}^{N_c} s_k \sqrt{\hat m_k}\,\Bigr|^2}_{K_{\text{bion}}}\,,
2685:\end{equation*}
2686:where $\zeta \propto e^{-S_0/(2N_c)}$ is the monopole fugacity,
2687:$\hat m_k$ are the current quark masses (the couplings in
2688:$W \ni \operatorname{Tr}(\hat m\,M)$), and
2689:$s_k = \pm 1$ are monopole-instanton signs (encoding the seed-to-bloom
2690:phase). The bion term is non-holomorphic (containing $\sqrt{\hat m}$) and
2691:non-perturbatively suppressed by $e^{-2S_0/3} \sim 10^{-2}$ at the
2692:confinement scale. Its minimum enforces the $v_0$-doubling condition
2693:$\sqrt{m_b} = 3\sqrt{m_s} + \sqrt{m_c}$
2694:(\S\ref{subsec:fterm});
2695:%
2696:superpotential combining mass, constraint, and Yukawa terms,
2697:%
2698:\begin{equation*}
2699:  W = \underbrace{\operatorname{Tr}(\hat m\, M)}_{\text{chiral mass}}
2700:    + \underbrace{X\!\left(\det M^{(3)} - B\bar B
2701:      - \Lambda_{\text{eff}}^{6}\right)}_{\text{Seiberg constraint}}
2702:    + \underbrace{c_3\,\frac{(\det M^{(3)})^3}
2703:      {\Lambda_{\text{eff}}^{18}}}_{\text{3-instanton}}
2704:    + \underbrace{y_c\, H\, M^c{}_c
2705:      + y_b\, H\, M^b{}_b
2706:      + y_t\, H\, Q^t \bar Q_t\phantom{X}}_{\text{Yukawa}}\,,
2707:\end{equation*}
2708:%
2709:where $M^{(3)}$ is the $3\times 3$ light-flavor block ($u,d,s$)
2710:and $\Lambda_{\text{eff}}$ is the dynamical scale of the confined
2711:$N_f = N_c = 3$ theory (including threshold matching at $m_c$
2712:and $m_b$).  The Yukawa terms are written in the full five-flavor
2713:theory above the heavy-quark thresholds, where $M^c{}_c = Q^c\bar Q_c$
2714:and $M^b{}_b = Q^b\bar Q_b$ are elementary bilinears;
2715:below threshold, they reduce to the matching conditions that generate
2716:$\Lambda_{\text{eff}}$ and the Seiberg constraint.
2717:The top Yukawa $y_t\,H\,Q^t\bar Q_t$ stands apart because the
2718:top does not confine into a meson.
2719:The $D$-term Lagrangian includes an optional Fayet--Iliopoulos
2720:parameter $\xi$ for $U(1)_{\text{em}}$:
2721:%
2722:\begin{equation*}
2723:  \mathcal{L}_D
2724:  = -\frac{e^2}{2}\!\left(\sum_i Q_i\,|\phi_i|^2
2725:    + \xi\right)^{\!2}.
2726:\end{equation*}
2727:%
2728:Finally, soft SUSY-breaking terms arise from a spurion
2729:$\langle F_S\rangle = \fpi^2$:
2730:%
2731:\begin{equation*}
2732:  \mathcal{L}_{\text{soft}}
2733:  = -\tilde m^2 \operatorname{Tr}(M^\dagger M)
2734:  - \bigl(B_\mu\operatorname{Tr}(\hat m\, M)
2735:    + A_y\sum_{i=c,b} y_i\, H\, M^i{}_i + \text{h.c.}\bigr)\,,
2736:  \qquad
2737:  \tilde m^2 \sim \fpi^2\,.
2738:\end{equation*}
2739:%
2740:The soft scalar mass $\tilde m^2 \sim \fpi^2 \approx (92\MeV)^2$
2741:generates the universal meson--lepton splitting
2742:$m^2_{\text{meson}} - m^2_{\text{lepton}} = \fpi^2$.

2744:\paragraph{Scope of the Lagrangian.}
2745:This Lagrangian produces: (i)~the seed Koide condition ($gv/m = \sqrt{3}$
2746:in the O'Raifeartaigh limit); (ii)~the meson--lepton mass splitting
2747:($\fpi^2$); (iii)~the $v_0$-doubling condition via the bion K\"ahler
2748:correction $K_{\text{bion}}$, whose minimum enforces
2749:$\sqrt{m_b} = 3\sqrt{m_s} + \sqrt{m_c}$; (iv)~the Yukawa couplings for
2750:the heavy sector; (v)~the $\cos(9\delta)$ potential that selects

2816:\paragraph{$D$-term effects on the Koide condition.}
2817:In $\mathcal{N}=1$ SUSY, $D$-terms shift scalar masses but leave
2818:fermion masses untouched. The lepton Koide triples, being conditions
2819:on \emph{fermion} masses, are naturally protected against
2820:$D$-term perturbations. The meson Koide triple
2821:$(-\pi, D_s, B)$ involves scalar masses and \emph{is} in principle
2822:affected by $D$-terms: a FI parameter $\xi$ shifts $m^2 \to m^2 + \xi Q_{\text{em}}^2$.
2823:Since all three mesons carry unit charge, each $m^2$ shifts by the
2824:same $\xi$, and the relative correction to $Q$ is of order
2825:$\xi/(4m_\pi^2) \sim 0.1\%$ for $\xi \approx -80\MeV^2$.
2826:Numerically, the $D$-term shifts the meson $Q$ by $0.03\%$,
2827:well below the $0.10\%$ precision of the triple.

2943:\section{Symmetry Restoration}
2944:\label{sec:restoration}
2945:%======================================================================
2946:
2947:The broken spectrum reassembles as symmetry-breaking effects are 
2948:removed:
2949:
2950:\begin{enumerate}
2951:\item \textbf{Turn off QED:} Electromagnetic splittings vanish 
2952:($\pi^\pm = \pi^0$, $K^\pm = K^0$). D-term effects disappear.
2953:\item \textbf{Set $m_u = m_d$:} Exact isospin.
2954:\item \textbf{Set $m_s = m_d$:} $SU(3)_V$ restored, 
2955:$\pi = K = \eta$. Koide splitting $v_1 \to 0$; lepton partners 
2956:degenerate.
2957:\item \textbf{Send $m_q \to 0$:} Chiral limit, all meson charges 
2958:$\to 0$. If sBootstrap SUSY restores, all light lepton masses 
2959:vanish too. This is the seed configuration: $(0, 0, 0)$.
2960:\end{enumerate}
2961:
2962:The seed triples are the ``penultimate'' stage: one member has 
2963:reached zero, but the other two retain the $2 - \sqrt{3}$ ratio. 
2964:Full triples are the final broken state. The lepton triple, 
2965:trapped between seed and bloom, reveals the hierarchy: 
2966:chiral breaking $\to$ seed $\to$ bloom $\to$ full spectrum.
2967:
2968:The direction of the flow also matters: approaching the chiral 
2969:limit, the pion charge rises from $\zch = -11.81$ toward zero 
2970:from below. It crosses zero exactly in the chiral limit. This 
2971:means the pion was ``born negative''---its charge sign is set by 
2972:the direction of chiral symmetry breaking, and the seed 
2973:$(0, \pi, D_s)$ is the configuration at the moment of crossing.

2977:\section{Open Problems}
2978:\label{sec:open}

2980:
2981:\subsection{The diquark sector}
2982:The Pauli theorem (\S\ref{subsec:pauli}) proves that no $J=0$, 
2983:color-$\bar{3}$ diquark can have symmetric flavor, closing all 
2984:field-theoretic escape routes ($L=0$, P-wave, or higher). The 
2985:sBootstrap's $\mathbf{15}$ diquark must therefore be non-composite: 
2986:either an algebraic image of the Miyazawa supercharge
2987:(\S\ref{subsec:baryon_open}) or a fundamental $SO(32)$ string state.
2988:However, the mixed anticommutator
2989:$\{Q_\alpha, \mathcal{Q}_{[ij],\beta}\} = \epsilon_{\alpha\beta}\,
2990:\mathcal{S}_{[ij]}$ produces a diquark generator in
2991:$\overline{\mathbf{10}}$ (antisymmetric), not the symmetric
2992:$\mathbf{15}$; thus the Miyazawa route alone does not resolve the
2993:$\mathbf{15}$~vs.~$\overline{\mathbf{10}}$ conflict.
2994:The exotic $+4/3$-charged
2995:diquarks of the $\mathbf{15}$ would be a testable consequence:
2996:if their mass is set by the SUSY-breaking scale ($\sim f_\pi$), they
2997:would be sub-GeV and accessible at fixed-target experiments; if by
2998:$\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$ or higher, LHC pair-production searches for
2999:exotic charges apply. No mass prediction can be made without a
3000:concrete realization of the $\mathbf{15}$.
3001:
3002:\subsection{What determines the Koide phase?}
3003:The energy balance constrains but does not uniquely fix $\delta$.
3004:The O'Raifeartaigh realization (\S\ref{subsec:fterm}) fixes the
3005:seed ratio $gv/m = \sqrt{3}$ but leaves $\delta$ undetermined---the
3006:phase controls how the seed blooms into a full triple. The bloom
3007:is a phase rotation $\delta: 3\pi/4 \to \delta_{\text{phys}}$ at
3008:fixed $v_0$, but explicit R-breaking perturbations push $Q$ away
3009:from $2/3$ at any nonzero strength, making the seed an unstable
3010:fixed point. The bloom must therefore be nonperturbative.
3011:
3012:The lepton phase satisfies $\delta_\ell \bmod 2\pi/3 = 2/9$ to
3013:$5\,\text{ppm}$ ($3.6\sigma$ after look-elsewhere over fractions
3014:with denominator $\leq 20$). Since $2/9$
3015:is not a rational multiple of $\pi$, this is not a geometric angle.
3016:It is, however, $2/9 = 2/(3\times 3)$, which points to a $\mathbb{Z}_9$
3017:discrete symmetry in the Koide modulus space. The $\mathbb{Z}_3$
3018:mass-permutation symmetry ($\delta \to \delta + 2\pi/3$) is the
3019:unique subgroup; the quotient $\mathbb{Z}_9/\mathbb{Z}_3$ has three
3020:cosets, producing three inequivalent mass spectra from the same $M_0$.
3021:The simplest potential with $\mathbb{Z}_9$ symmetry,
3022:$V \supset -\cos\bigl(9(\delta - 2/9)\bigr)$, has the lepton value
3023:as one of its nine minima, with the $\mathbb{Z}_3$-equivalent minima
3024:at $\delta = 2/9 + 2k\pi/3$.
3025:
3026:A dynamical origin for $\mathbb{Z}_9$ exists in SQCD: for $N_f = N_c = 3$,
3027:the diagonal $U(1)_{R+2A}$ (R-symmetry plus twice the axial symmetry)
3028:has anomaly coefficient $6N_c = 18 = 2 \times 9$. Instantons break
3029:$U(1)_{R+2A} \to \mathbb{Z}_{18}$, which contains $\mathbb{Z}_9$
3030:as a subgroup. The meson field $M$ carries $\mathbb{Z}_{18}$ charge 4
3031:(from two quark zero modes at charge 2 each), so $\det M$ carries
3032:charge~12.  The lowest-order holomorphic $\mathbb{Z}_{18}$-singlet
3033:beyond the Seiberg constraint is the three-instanton operator
3034:\begin{equation}
3035:  \delta W_{3\text{-inst}}
3036:    = c_3\,\frac{(\det M)^3}{\Lambda^{18}}\,,
3037:\end{equation}
3038:since $(\det M)^3$ has $\mathbb{Z}_{18}$ charge $36 = 2\times 18$.
3039:Near the SUSY vacuum $\det M = \Lambda^6$, parametrizing
3040:$\det M = \Lambda^6\,e^{3i\delta_M}$ yields
3041:$\delta W \propto e^{9i\delta}$, and the cross-term with the
3042:tree-level superpotential gives
3043:$V_{\text{eff}} \supset -V_0\cos\bigl(9(\delta - \delta_0)\bigr)$
3044:with $\delta_0 = \arg(c_3)/9$.
3045:This is exactly the $\cos(9\delta)$ potential identified above.
3046:The one-instanton vertex ($\det M$, charge 12) is already captured
3047:by the Seiberg constraint; the two-instanton vertex ($(\det M)^2$,
3048:charge 24, not a $\mathbb{Z}_{18}$ singlet) is forbidden.
3049:The three-instanton correction does not affect the meson mass
3050:spectrum: the $F$-term equations define an effective Lagrange
3051:multiplier $X_{\text{eff}} = X + 3c_3\Lambda^{12}/\Lambda^{18}$,
3052:but the constraint $\det M = \Lambda^6$ and the meson VEVs
3053:$M_j = C/m_j$ are unmodified. The correction acts only on the
3054:phase structure (through $\cos(9\delta)$), not on the magnitudes.
3055:
3056:At strong coupling ($\Lambda \sim 300\MeV$), the coefficient
3057:$c_3$ is $O(1)$ and the potential strength is
3058:$V_0^{1/4} \sim 65\text{--}85\MeV$, comparable to
3059:$f_\pi = 92\MeV$---this is not a parametrically suppressed effect.
3060:The same $\cos(9\delta)$ structure also arises from magnetic
3061:bion contributions on $\mathbb{R}^3 \times S^1$, where the
3062:$\mathbb{Z}_3$-symmetric combination of the SU(3) affine roots
3063:maps to $\cos(9\delta)$ in the meson phase variable.
3064:
3065:The embedding is specific to $N_c = 3$: the condition
3066:$N_c^2 \mid 6N_c$ requires $N_c \mid 6$, which holds only for
3067:$N_c = 1, 2, 3, 6$.
3068:
3069:The quark Koide phases, by contrast, do \emph{not} sit at
3070:$\mathbb{Z}_9$ minima: the $(-s,c,b)$ and $(c,b,t)$ phases
3071:are displaced by $39\%$ and $22\%$ of the $\mathbb{Z}_9$
3072:spacing, respectively.  This suggests that the lepton and quark
3073:phases are selected by different dynamical mechanisms---the
3074:instanton potential for leptons, and possibly the bloom mechanism
3075:or ISS dynamics for quarks.
3076:
3077:The Coleman--Weinberg potential of the minimal O'Raifeartaigh model
3078:is flat in $\delta$ at one loop---the pseudo-modulus potential depends
3079:only on the modulus $|\Phi_0|$, not on the Koide phase.  The
3080:three-instanton potential above provides the leading
3081:$\delta$-dependent term that lifts this flat direction.
3082:The \emph{modulus} direction $|\Phi_0|$ is a separate question:
3083:the CW potential stabilizes $|\langle\Phi_0\rangle|$ at zero,
3084:but the Koide seed requires $\langle X\rangle/\mu = \sqrt{3}$
3085:(\S\ref{subsec:superpotential}).
3086:The three-instanton operator $(\det M)^3$ shifts the Seiberg
3087:Lagrange multiplier~$X$ (dimension $[\text{mass}]^{-3}$ in the
3088:confining phase) but this is a different object from the ISS
3089:pseudo-modulus (dimension $[\text{mass}]^{+1}$ in the magnetic
3090:dual); the mechanism for the nonzero pseudo-modulus VEV remains open.
3091:
3092:More broadly, the ISS Coleman--Weinberg potential does \emph{not}
3093:transmit the Koide condition from the UV quark masses to the IR
3094:meson spectrum.  In the $N_f = 4$ ISS model, the one-loop CW
3095:masses for the broken mesons $\chi_a$ are nearly degenerate
3096:($m_{\text{CW}} \approx 27\MeV$ for all four flavors), because
3097:the CW mass depends on $m_a$ through the affine map
3098:$F_a = h\mu^2 - m_a$, and all quark masses satisfy
3099:$m_a \ll h\mu^2$.  The resulting spectrum gives
3100:$Q \approx 1/3$ (the degenerate limit), regardless of the UV
3101:value.  This confirms that flavor-sensitive mass structure must
3102:arise from non-perturbative effects---the bion K\"ahler
3103:correction (\S\ref{subsec:fterm}), which directly involves
3104:$\sqrt{m_k}$, rather than the one-loop effective potential.
3105:
3106:\subsection{Connecting the three tuples}
3107:The unified superpotential (\S\ref{subsec:superpotential}) connects
3108:the light and heavy sectors through a layered structure:
3109:the seed Koide fixes $m_c/m_s$ (from $gv/m = \sqrt{3}$),
3110:the $v_0$-doubling fixes $m_b$ (from the K\"ahler sector),
3111:and the $(c,b,t)$ Koide serves as an overdetermination test.
3112:After these constraints, only two to three free parameters remain:
3113:$m_u$, $m_d$ (connected to the Cabibbo angle), and the overall
3114:scale $m_s$. The $v_0$-doubling is non-holomorphic and lives in the
3115:K\"ahler sector: as shown in \S\ref{subsec:fterm}, the bion
3116:K\"ahler correction $K_{\text{bion}} \sim |\!\sum s_k\sqrt{\hat m_k}|^2$
3117:generates an effective potential whose minimum enforces
3118:$\sqrt{m_b} = 3\sqrt{m_s} + \sqrt{m_c}$.
3119:The Hessian has rank~1, constraining exactly the $v_0$-doubling
3120:combination while leaving $m_s$ and $m_c$ to the Koide seed.
3121:The extension from $\mathbb{R}^3 \times S^1$ to
3122:$\mathbb{R}^4$ relies on \"Unsal's continuity conjecture.
3123:
3124:\subsection{The baryon sector}
3125:\label{subsec:baryon_open}
3126:In Seiberg's SQCD with $N_f = N_c = 3$, baryons are part of the
3127:low-energy spectrum. Their SUSY partners are ``baryonic fermions''
3128:with no known candidates among observed particles.
3129:
3130:The meson--baryon connection requires a Miyazawa supercharge
3131:\cite{Miyazawa, CattoGursey}, which cannot lie in the
3132:fundamental~$\mathbf{5}$: the product
3133:$\mathbf{5}\otimes(\mathbf{1}\oplus\mathbf{24})$ does not
3134:contain~$\overline{\mathbf{10}}$, so a single-index generator
3135:cannot map mesons to baryons.  The minimal choice is
3136:\begin{equation*}
3137:\mathcal{Q}_{[pq],\alpha}
3138:  \;\in\; \overline{\mathbf{10}}\;\text{of}\;SU(5)_f\,,
3139:  \qquad \Delta B = +1\,,
3140:\end{equation*}
3141:with transformation law
3142:$[\mathcal{Q}_{[pq]}, M^i{}_j]
3143:  = \delta^i_{[p}\,\tilde\psi_{B,\,q]j}
3144:  - \tfrac{1}{5}\delta^i_j\,\tilde\psi_{B,\,pq}$,
3145:where $\tilde\psi_{B,\,lm}
3146:  = \tfrac{1}{6}\epsilon_{lmijk}\,\psi_B^{ijk}$ is the dual
3147:baryonino.  The anticommutator decomposes as
3148:\begin{equation*}
3149:\overline{\mathbf{10}}\otimes\mathbf{10}
3150:  = \mathbf{1}\oplus\mathbf{24}\oplus\mathbf{75}\,,
3151:\end{equation*}
3152:giving $\{\mathcal{Q},\bar{\mathcal{Q}}\}
3153:  \supset \sigma^\mu P_\mu + T^A + Z$, where $T^A$ are the
3154:$SU(5)_f$ generators and $Z$ are central charges encoding the
3155:meson--baryon mass splitting $m_B^2 - m_M^2 = \langle Z\rangle
3156:\sim\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}^2$.  This Miyazawa SUSY evades the
3157:Haag--\L{}opusza\'nski--Sohnius theorem because it is an
3158:approximate symmetry broken at scale~$\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$,
3159:analogous to $SU(6)$ spin--flavor symmetry in the quark model.
3160:The $\mathbf{75}$ channel on the right-hand side
3161:signals that the full bosonic subalgebra extends beyond
3162:$SU(5)_f$; its interpretation remains open.
3163:The $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$ representation of the Miyazawa
3164:supercharge is the same $SU(5)_f$ multiplet that appears in the
3165:$SO(32)$ adjoint decomposition (Table~\ref{tab:496}), suggesting
3166:that the baryon--meson connection and the string embedding may
3167:share a common algebraic origin.

3169:\subsection{The strong CP problem}
3170:The principle ``Nature abhors chirality'' predicts $\bar{\theta} = 0$ 
3171:but provides no mechanism.
3172:
3173:\subsection{The kaon--muon puzzle}
3174:The Lagrangian predicts a universal soft splitting
3175:$m^2_{\text{meson}} - m^2_{\text{lepton}} = \fpi^2$ across the
3176:meson matrix. For the pion--muon pair, this works:
3177:$m_\pi^2 - m_\mu^2 \approx \fpi^2$ to $2.2\%$. But the
3178:tree-level formula $m^2_M = |m_i + m_j|^2 + \fpi^2$ badly
3179:underpredicts heavier meson masses: it gives $m_K \approx 133\MeV$
3180:(physical: $498\MeV$) and $m_{D_s} \approx 1{,}367\MeV$
3181:(physical: $1{,}968\MeV$). QCD confinement generates the bulk
3182:of these masses independently of the soft term. The universal
3183:splitting applies to the \emph{soft} contribution, not to the
3184:total mass; it is visible only for $(\pi, \mu)$,
3185:where the soft term dominates over $|m_u + m_d|^2$.
3186:
3187:\subsection{The Casimir eigenvalue equation}
3188:The eigenvalue equation $x^2 + C_2 x - C_2 = 0$ produces the
3189:on-shell weak mixing angle to $0.4\sigma$, but three issues
3190:remain open: (i)~the equation is an ansatz, not derived from a
3191:Lagrangian; (ii)~the match uses the on-shell renormalization
3192:scheme, and no argument selects this scheme over $\overline{\text{MS}}$;
3193:(iii)~the negative spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ root gives $|M| = 122.4\GeV$,
3194:tantalizingly close to $M_H = 125.25\GeV$ but with a spin mismatch.
3195:
3196:\subsection{The $SO(32)$ projection}
3197:The adjoint $\mathbf{496}$ contains the sBootstrap
3198:representations: $\mathbf{24}$ in the neutral sector and
3199:$\mathbf{15} \oplus \overline{\mathbf{15}}$ in the matter
3200:sector at $|Q_A| = 2$. The remaining 442 states must be
3201:projected out or made massive. No projection mechanism has been
3202:identified. The alternative anomaly-free gauge group
3203:$E_8 \times E_8$ is blocked by the $\mathbf{15}$ vs.\
3204:$\mathbf{10}$ obstruction (\S\ref{subsec:so32_open}),
3205:constraining the string embedding to the Type~I / $SO(32)$
3206:corner of the landscape.
3207:
3208:\subsection{Generation uniqueness}
3209:The Diophantine system uniquely selects $N = 3$ generations,
3210:but the third equation ($r^2 + s^2 - 1 = 4N$) has the weakest
3211:physical motivation. A dynamical derivation of the counting
3212:constraints would strengthen the argument considerably.

3262:\section{Conclusions}
3263:\label{sec:conclusions}
3264:%======================================================================
3265:
3266:Seven principal results:
3267:
3268:\paragraph{1.\ The Koide formula is energy equipartition.}
3269:$Q = 2/3$ is $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$: perturbation energies 
3270:match the base scale. The signed-charge framework resolves the 
3271:electron mass puzzle (near-cancellation, not fine-tuning) and reveals 
3272:that mesons satisfy Koide with $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ to $0.1\%$.
3273:
3274:\paragraph{2.\ Seed triples set the stage for SUSY breaking.}
3275:The quark seed $(0,s,c)$ and meson seed $(0,\pi,D_s)$ share the
3276:universal ratio $2 - \sqrt{3}$ and operate at $v_0^2 \sim
3277:230$--$350\MeV$, close to the lepton triple's $v_0^2 = 314\MeV
3278:\approx \fpi^2/27$
3279:(recall that $v_0^2$ carries dimensions of MeV, not MeV$^2$,
3280:because $v_0 = z_0$ is a charge with dimensions MeV$^{1/2}$). 
3281:The massless member is the necessary condition for F-term 
3282:(O'Raifeartaigh) SUSY breaking. Upon breaking, seeds bloom into 
3283:full triples $(-s,c,b)$ and $(-\pi,D_s,B)$. The lepton triple 
3284:is the ``almost-seed'' that has barely bloomed.
3285:
3286:\paragraph{3.\ The seed condition is one superpotential constraint.}
3287:The O'Raifeartaigh model with superpotential
3288:$W = f\Phi_0 + m\Phi_1\Phi_2 + g\Phi_0\Phi_1^2$ produces a
3289:fermionic spectrum $(0,\, m_-,\, m_+)$ that is an exact Koide
3290:seed triple when $gv/m = \sqrt{3}$. The energy-balance condition
3291:reduces to a single algebraic relation among the cubic coupling,
3292:the pseudo-modulus VEV, and the mass parameter.
3293:
3294:\paragraph{4.\ Two breaking mechanisms plus a heavy-sector origin.}
3295:F-term breaking (via the massless seed member) generates the light 
3296:sector; the Volkov--Akulov relation $f_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi$ is its 
3297:low-energy realization, not a separate mechanism. D-term breaking 
3298:($U(1)_{\text{em}}$ FI parameter) provides electromagnetic 
3299:splittings as a perturbative correction. The heavy triple 
3300:$(c,b,t)$ stands apart: no seed, no meson shadow, scale far above 
3301:$\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$, possibly connected to electroweak breaking.
3302:
3303:\paragraph{5.\ The Diophantine counting uniquely selects $N=3$.}
3304:Three equations on the scalar content---$rs = 2N$, $r(r+1)/2 = 2N$,
3305:$r^2+s^2-1 = 4N$---have the unique positive-integer solution
3306:$(N,r,s) = (3,3,2)$, which maps to the $SU(5)$ representations
3307:$\mathbf{24} \oplus \mathbf{15} \oplus \overline{\mathbf{15}}$.
3308:This combination is anomaly-free and asymptotically free.
3309:
3310:\paragraph{6.\ An algebraic electroweak ratio.}
3311:The eigenvalue equation $x^2 + s(s+1)\,x - s(s+1) = 0$, with
3312:$s$ the spin Casimir, predicts
3313:$R = 1 - x_+(1/2)/x_+(1) = 0.22310$, matching the on-shell
3314:$\sin^2\theta_W = 0.22320$ to $0.4\sigma$ and giving
3315:$M_W = 80.374\GeV$ ($0.39\sigma$ from PDG). The equation is
3316:pinned by three structural constraints; $R$ follows as a
3317:consequence, not a free parameter. The dynamical origin of the
3318:equation and the choice of renormalization scheme remain open.
3319:
3320:\paragraph{7.\ An explicit Lagrangian with two to three free parameters.}
3321:The full $\mathcal{N}=1$ Lagrangian
3322:(\S\ref{subsec:superpotential}) comprises a canonical K\"ahler
3323:potential supplemented by a bion correction
3324:$K_{\text{bion}} \sim |\!\sum s_k\sqrt{\hat m_k}|^2$,
3325:a superpotential combining the Seiberg constraint
3326:with Yukawa couplings, $D$-terms with a Fayet--Iliopoulos
3327:parameter, and soft breaking from a spurion
3328:$\langle F\rangle = \fpi^2$.  The seed Koide fixes the
3329:pseudo-modulus VEV $\langle X\rangle = \sqrt{3}\,\mu$ (predicting $m_c/m_s$); the
3330:bion K\"ahler correction fixes $m_b$ through the
3331:$v_0$-doubling condition, with Hessian rank~1 constraining
3332:exactly one mass combination.  The self-consistent chain from $m_s$ alone predicts
3333:$m_c = 1{,}301\MeV$, $m_b = 4{,}177\MeV$, and---if
3334:$Q(c,b,t) = 2/3$ is imposed---$m_t = 171.3 \pm 1.5\GeV$
3335:(Table~\ref{tab:predictions}).
3336:The lepton Koide scale satisfies $M_0 \approx \fpi^2/27$
3337:to $0.3\%$, tying lepton masses to the pion decay constant.
3338:After these
3339:constraints, only $m_u$, $m_d$, and $m_s$ remain free; $m_u$ and
3340:$m_d$ are connected to the Cabibbo angle through the Oakes
3341:relation.  The $SO(32)$ adjoint provides the UV completion,
3342:with the $E_8 \times E_8$ alternative blocked by the
3343:$\mathbf{15}$-vs.-$\mathbf{10}$ obstruction.
3344:
3345:The diquark sector, required to complete the SUSY multiplet count,
3346:fills the symmetric $\mathbf{15} + \overline{\mathbf{15}}$ of
3347:$SU(5)_f$---the representation produced naturally by the SUSY
3348:generator acting on quarks and by the $SO(32)$ decomposition. A
3349:kinematic theorem (\S\ref{subsec:pauli}) shows that \emph{no}
3350:$J=0$ color-$\bar{3}$ diquark can have symmetric flavor, regardless
3351:of orbital angular momentum: the sBootstrap diquark in the
3352:$\mathbf{15}$ cannot be a $qq$ composite. The Miyazawa supercharge
3353:$\mathcal{Q}_{[ij]} \in \overline{\mathbf{10}}$
3354:(\S\ref{subsec:baryon_open}) connects mesons to baryons but
3355:generates diquark operators in $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$
3356:(antisymmetric), not $\mathbf{15}$ (symmetric).  The remaining
3357:candidate is a fundamental string-theoretic state from the
3358:$SO(32)$ adjoint.
3359:
3360:The organizing principle is that each sector of the spectrum begins
3361:with a massless member (a seed), which is the necessary condition
3362:for spontaneous SUSY breaking. The breaking lifts the zero,
3363:generates the full triple, and the energy-balance condition
3364:$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ constrains the initial conditions.
3365:The bloom from seed to full triple is a phase rotation
3366:$\delta: 3\pi/4 \to \delta_{\text{phys}}$ in the Koide
3367:parametrization, at fixed Koide scale $v_0$; it is not a
3368:continuous deformation of the O'Raifeartaigh spectrum (which would
3369:break $Q = 2/3$) but a nonperturbative rearrangement.  The lepton
3370:phase is selected by a three-instanton potential
3371:$V \propto -\cos(9\delta)$, the lowest $\mathbb{Z}_{18}$-invariant
3372:harmonic in $N_f = N_c = 3$ SQCD; the quark phases are not at
3373:$\mathbb{Z}_9$ minima, suggesting a different selection mechanism.
3374:The unified superpotential has a layered structure:
3375:(i)~the Seiberg effective superpotential governs the light sector,
3376:with SUSY breaking forced by the quantum constraint when $m_u = m_d = 0$;
3377:(ii)~the O'Raifeartaigh condition $gv/m = \sqrt{3}$ (equivalently
3378:$\langle X\rangle = \sqrt{3}\,\mu$ in the magnetic dual) fixes the seed Koide ratio,
3379:predicting $m_c$ from $m_s$;
3380:(iii)~the bion K\"ahler correction
3381:$|\!\sum s_k\sqrt{\hat m_k}|^2$ predicts
3382:$m_b = 4{,}177 \pm 40\MeV$ via $v_0$-doubling;
3383:(iv)~the Yukawa sector governs the $(c,b,t)$ triple as an
3384:overdetermination test.
3385:After these constraints, only two to three free parameters remain:
3386:$m_u$, $m_d$ (connected to the Cabibbo angle through the Oakes
3387:relation), and the overall scale $m_s$. All quark Koide triples
3388:obligatorily mix up-type and down-type mass eigenstates.
3389:The energy-balance condition is a boundary condition on the
3390:superpotential, not an RG attractor; the Koide ratios are naturally
3391:protected against $D$-term perturbations because $D$-terms shift
3392:scalar masses but leave fermion masses untouched.
