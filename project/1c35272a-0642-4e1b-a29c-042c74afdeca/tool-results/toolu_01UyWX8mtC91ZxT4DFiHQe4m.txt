    29‚Üí\begin{abstract}
    30‚ÜíWe reformulate the sBootstrap in terms of signed mass charges 
    31‚Üí$\zch = z_0 + z_i$ with $m = \zch^2$, and show that the Koide ratio 
    32‚Üí$Q = 2/3$ is the algebraic consequence of two conditions: 
    33‚Üí$\sum z_i = 0$ and $\langle z_i^2\rangle = z_0^2$ (perturbation 
    34‚Üíenergies average to the vacuum energy). Allowing negative charges, 
    35‚Üíwe find that pseudoscalar mesons satisfy a Koide triple 
    36‚Üí$(-\pi, D_s, B)$ to 0.1\%, and identify \emph{seed triples} with 
    37‚Üíone massless member---$(0, s, c)$ for quarks, $(0, \pi, D_s)$ for 
    38‚Üímesons---as the pre-breaking configurations from which the full 
    39‚Üíspectrum emerges. These seed triples share the same analytical 
    40‚Üístructure (charge ratio $2-\sqrt{3}$) and, strikingly, the meson 
    41‚Üíseed $(0,\pi,D_s)$ has vacuum energy $v_0^2 \approx 351\MeV$, 
    42‚Üíclose to the lepton triple's $v_0^2 \approx 314\MeV$. A third 
    43‚Üítuple $(c,b,t)$ stands apart, having no meson counterpart because 
    44‚Üítop diquarks do not hadronize. We discuss the diquark sector---which 
    45‚Üímust fill the antisymmetric representation of $SU(5)$ flavor rather 
    46‚Üíthan the symmetric, requiring additional dynamical input---and outline 
    47‚Üíhow the three types of SUSY breaking (F-term, D-term, nonlinear 
    48‚ÜíVolkov--Akulov) may map onto the three-tuple structure.
    49‚Üí\end{abstract}

    53‚Üí%======================================================================
    54‚Üí\section{Introduction}
    55‚Üí\label{sec:intro}
    56‚Üí%======================================================================
    57‚Üí
    58‚ÜíThe sBootstrap \cite{Rivero2005,Rivero2012} proposes that the Standard 
    59‚ÜíModel's pseudoscalar mesons and leptons fill common $\mathcal{N}=1$ 
    60‚Üísupermultiplets. Its foundational relation is
    61‚Üí\begin{equation}
    62‚Üí\label{eq:pimu}
    63‚Üím_\pi^2 - m_\mu^2 \approx f_\pi^2\,,
    64‚Üí\end{equation}
    65‚Üíidentifying the pion decay constant $f_\pi \approx 92.2\MeV$ as the 
    66‚ÜíSUSY-breaking order parameter. Numerically, $m_\pi^2 - m_\mu^2 = 
    67‚Üí8{,}316\MeV^2$ versus $f_\pi^2 = 8{,}501\MeV^2$, a $2.2\%$ 
    68‚Üídiscrepancy---comparable to the precision of the quark Koide triples 
    69‚Üídiscussed below. In this paper we develop two themes 
    70‚Üíthat change the structure of the program.
    71‚Üí
    72‚ÜíFirst, \textbf{mass is the square of a signed charge}: $m = \zch^2$ 
    73‚Üíwhere $\zch = z_0 + z_i$ can be negative. The Koide ratio $Q = 2/3$ 
    74‚Üíis then not numerology but the energy-balance condition 
    75‚Üí$\langle z_i^2\rangle = z_0^2$. With signed charges, pseudoscalar 
    76‚Üímesons satisfy Koide, and the spectrum organizes into \emph{seed 
    77‚Üítriples} with a massless member that grow into full triples upon 
    78‚Üísymmetry breaking.
    79‚Üí
    80‚ÜíSecond, \textbf{the full sBootstrap has three Koide tuples, not one}, 
    81‚Üíand the breaking structure must generate all three: a light sector 
    82‚Üí$(0,s,c) \to (-s,c,b)$ seen in both quarks and mesons, a lepton 
    83‚Üísector $(e\!\approx\!0, \mu, \tau)$, and a heavy sector $(c,b,t)$ 
    84‚Üíthat decouples from meson physics because the top quark does not 
    85‚Üíhadronize. The diquark partners of quarks, needed to complete the 
    86‚ÜíSUSY multiplets, must fill the antisymmetric $\overline{10}$ of 
    87‚Üí$SU(5)$ flavor rather than the symmetric 15, requiring extra 
    88‚Üídynamical input from the breaking sector.
    89‚Üí
    90‚Üí%======================================================================
    91‚Üí\section{The Koide Formula as Energy Balance}
    92‚Üí\label{sec:koide_algebra}
    93‚Üí%======================================================================
    94‚Üí
    95‚Üí\subsection{Charges, not masses}
    96‚Üí
    97‚ÜíThe fundamental variable is the signed mass charge $\zch = z_0 + z_i 
    98‚Üí\in \mathbb{R}$, with the physical mass $m = \zch^2 \geq 0$.
    99‚Üí
   100‚Üí\subsection{Derivation of $Q = 2/3$}
   101‚Üí
   102‚ÜíFor three charges $\zch_k = z_0 + z_k$ with $z_0$ the mean 
   103‚Üí($\sum z_k = 0$):
   104‚Üí\begin{equation}
   105‚ÜíQ = \frac{\sum \zch_k^2}{(\sum \zch_k)^2} 
   106‚Üí= \frac{3z_0^2 + \sum z_k^2}{9z_0^2}
   107‚Üí= \frac{1}{3} + \frac{\langle z_k^2\rangle}{3z_0^2}\,.
   108‚Üí\end{equation}
   109‚ÜíTherefore:
   110‚Üí\begin{equation}
   111‚Üí\boxed{Q = \frac{2}{3} \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad 
   112‚Üí\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2\,.}
   113‚Üí\end{equation}
   114‚Üí
   115‚ÜíThe two conditions are:
   116‚Üí\begin{enumerate}
   117‚Üí\item \textbf{Zero-sum perturbations:} $z_1 + z_2 + z_3 = 0$ 
   118‚Üí(definition of $z_0$ as the mean charge).
   119‚Üí\item \textbf{Energy balance:} The mean energy of the perturbations 
   120‚Üíequals the energy of the unperturbed charge: 
   121‚Üí$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$.
   122‚Üí\end{enumerate}
   123‚Üí
   124‚ÜíCondition~2 is the physics. It says the splitting scale matches the 
   125‚Üíbase scale---the coefficient of variation equals unity: 
   126‚Üí$\sigma(\zch)/\bar{\zch} = 1$. This is neither perturbative 
   127‚Üí($\sigma \ll \bar{\zch}$, giving $Q \to 1/3$, all equal) nor 
   128‚Üídominant ($\sigma \gg \bar{\zch}$, giving $Q \to 1$, one mass 
   129‚Üídominates). The Koide value $2/3$ is the equipartition point.
   130‚Üí
   131‚Üí\subsection{Why one mass can vanish}
   132‚Üí
   133‚ÜíIf $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$, a perturbation $z_k$ can reach 
   134‚Üí$|z_k| \sim z_0$, making $\zch_k = z_0 + z_k \approx 0$. A vanishing 
   135‚Üímass is the \emph{natural extreme} of energy balance, not fine-tuning.
   136‚Üí
   137‚Üí%======================================================================
   138‚Üí\section{Seed Triples: The $(0, a, b)$ Structure}
   139‚Üí\label{sec:seeds}
   140‚Üí%======================================================================
   141‚Üí
   142‚Üí\subsection{Analytical condition}
   143‚Üí
   144‚ÜíFor a triple $(0, \zch_a, \zch_b)$ with one massless member, the 
   145‚ÜíKoide ratio becomes:
   146‚Üí\begin{equation}
   147‚ÜíQ = \frac{\zch_a^2 + \zch_b^2}{(\zch_a + \zch_b)^2}\,.
   148‚Üí\end{equation}
   149‚ÜíSetting $Q = 2/3$ and writing $r = \zch_a/\zch_b$ (with $r < 1$):
   150‚Üí\begin{equation}
   151‚Üí3(r^2 + 1) = 2(r + 1)^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad r^2 - 4r + 1 = 0\,,
   152‚Üí\end{equation}
   153‚Üígiving:
   154‚Üí\begin{equation}
   155‚Üí\boxed{r = 2 - \sqrt{3} \approx 0.26795\,.}
   156‚Üí\end{equation}
   157‚Üí
   158‚ÜíEquivalently, in mass space: 
   159‚Üí$m_{\text{light}}/m_{\text{heavy}} = (2 - \sqrt{3})^2 = 7 - 4\sqrt{3} 
   160‚Üí\approx 0.07180$.
   161‚Üí
   162‚ÜíThis is a \emph{universal} prediction: any Koide triple with one 
   163‚Üímassless member has the same charge ratio between the two massive 
   164‚Üímembers, regardless of their absolute scale.
   165‚Üí
   166‚Üí\subsection{The quark seed: $(0, s, c)$}
   167‚Üí
   168‚ÜíThe strange and charm quark charges give:
   169‚Üí\begin{equation}
   170‚Üí\frac{\sqrt{m_s}}{\sqrt{m_c}} = \frac{9.67}{35.64} = 0.2712\,, 
   171‚Üí\qquad r_{\text{Koide}} = 0.2679\,, \qquad \text{deviation: } 1.2\%\,.
   172‚Üí\end{equation}
   173‚Üí
   174‚ÜíThis triple has $Q = 0.6644$, deviating from $2/3$ by only $0.35\%$. 
   175‚ÜíIts vacuum energy is $v_0^2 = (\zch_s + \zch_c)^2/9 \approx 228\MeV$.
   176‚Üí
   177‚ÜíA historical note: Harari and a collaborator observed that $(0, d, s)$ 
   178‚Üíwas approximately a Koide triple, at a time when the up quark mass was 
   179‚Üístill thought to be possibly zero \cite{Harari}. With current masses, 
   180‚Üí$\sqrt{m_d}/\sqrt{m_s} = 0.224$, deviating from $2 - \sqrt{3}$ by 
   181‚Üí$16\%$---too large for a good Koide triple. But if $m_s$ were 
   182‚Üí$\sim 150\MeV$ and $m_d \sim 7\MeV$ (values common in that era), the 
   183‚Üíratio improves considerably. The seed idea was already present, ahead 
   184‚Üíof its time.
   185‚Üí
   186‚Üí\subsection{The meson seed: $(0, \pi, D_s)$}
   187‚Üí
   188‚ÜíThe pion and $D_s$ meson charges give:
   189‚Üí\begin{equation}
   190‚Üí\frac{\sqrt{m_\pi}}{\sqrt{m_{D_s}}} = \frac{11.81}{44.37} = 0.2663\,, 
   191‚Üí\qquad r_{\text{Koide}} = 0.2679\,, \qquad \text{deviation: } 0.6\%\,.
   192‚Üí\end{equation}
   193‚Üí
   194‚ÜíThis gives $Q = 0.6679$, deviating by $0.18\%$. It is the 
   195‚Üí\textbf{meson counterpart} of the quark seed $(0, s, c)$---both 
   196‚Üíinvolve the strange-charm sector. Indeed, $D_s = c\bar{s}$ is the 
   197‚Üímeson that directly contains the $s$ and $c$ quarks.
   198‚Üí
   199‚Üí\subsection{The lepton triple as an ``almost-seed''}
   200‚Üí
   201‚ÜíThe charged lepton triple $(e, \mu, \tau)$ has $\zch_e = 0.715\MeV^{1/2}$, 
   202‚Üíwhich is small but nonzero. If $m_e$ were zero, we would need 
   203‚Üí$\sqrt{m_\mu}/\sqrt{m_\tau} = 2 - \sqrt{3} = 0.2679$; the actual value 
   204‚Üíis $0.2439$, deviating by $9\%$. So the leptons are \emph{not} an 
   205‚Üíexact seed---the electron, while very light, is not massless. But the 
   206‚Üítriple is clearly the same family: the near-critical Koide phase puts 
   207‚Üíone member close to zero.
   208‚Üí
   209‚Üí\subsection{Vacuum energy coincidence}
   210‚Üí
   211‚ÜíThe $v_0^2$ values of the seed triples cluster remarkably:
   212‚Üí
   213‚Üí\begin{center}
   214‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lcc}
   215‚Üí\toprule
   216‚ÜíTriple & $v_0^2$ (MeV) & $v_0$ ($\MeV^{1/2}$) \\
   217‚Üí\midrule
   218‚Üí$(e, \mu, \tau)$ & 314 & 17.72 \\
   219‚Üí$(0, \pi, D_s)$ & 351 & 18.73 \\
   220‚Üí$(0, s, c)$ & 228 & 15.10 \\
   221‚Üí\bottomrule
   222‚Üí\end{tabular}
   223‚Üí\end{center}
   224‚Üí
   225‚ÜíThe lepton and meson seed triples share a vacuum energy around 
   226‚Üí310--350~MeV, squarely in the QCD scale range. This is the 
   227‚ÜísBootstrap connection made quantitative: \textbf{the lepton triple 
   228‚Üíand the meson seed triple operate at the same scale.}
   229‚Üí
   230‚Üí%======================================================================
   231‚Üí\section{A Tale of Two Tuples}
   232‚Üí\label{sec:two_tuples}
   233‚Üí%======================================================================
   234‚Üí
   235‚Üí\subsection{From seed to full triple}
   236‚Üí
   237‚ÜíThe seed triples grow into full triples upon symmetry breaking:
   238‚Üí
   239‚Üí\begin{center}
   240‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lccc}
   241‚Üí\toprule
   242‚ÜíSector & Seed triple & Full triple & $v_0^2$ (full) \\
   243‚Üí\midrule
   244‚ÜíQuarks & $(0, s, c)$ & $(-s, c, b)$ & 913 MeV \\
   245‚ÜíMesons & $(0, \pi, D_s)$ & $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ & 1230 MeV \\
   246‚ÜíLeptons & $(0 \approx e, \mu, \tau)$ & $(e, \mu, \tau)$ & 314 MeV \\
   247‚Üí\bottomrule
   248‚Üí\end{tabular}
   249‚Üí\end{center}
   250‚Üí
   251‚ÜíIn each case:
   252‚Üí\begin{itemize}
   253‚Üí\item The seed has a massless (or near-massless) member, two massive 
   254‚Üímembers in the ratio $2 - \sqrt{3}$, and $Q \approx 2/3$.
   255‚Üí\item The full triple has \textbf{no} massless member: the zero has 
   256‚Üíbeen lifted (to $m_e$, or the sign-flipped strange/pion mass), and a 
   257‚Üínew heavy member ($b$ quark, $B$ meson) has appeared. The full triple 
   258‚Üíalso satisfies $Q \approx 2/3$ but at a larger scale $v_0$.
   259‚Üí\end{itemize}
   260‚Üí
   261‚ÜíThe ``tale of two tuples'' is this: \textbf{each sector presents 
   262‚Üífirst as a seed with a massless member, then ``blooms'' into a 
   263‚Üífull triple when SUSY breaks.} The massless member is the necessary 
   264‚Üíinitial condition; the breaking lifts it and generates the full spectrum.
   265‚Üí
   266‚Üí\subsection{Precision summary}
   267‚Üí
   268‚Üí\begin{center}
   269‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lccc}
   270‚Üí\toprule
   271‚ÜíTriple & $Q$ & Dev.\ from $2/3$ & $v_0^2$ (MeV) \\
   272‚Üí\midrule
   273‚Üí\multicolumn{4}{c}{\textit{Seed triples}} \\
   274‚Üí$(0, \pi, D_s)$ & 0.6679 & 0.18\% & 351 \\
   275‚Üí$(0, s, c)$ & 0.6644 & 0.35\% & 228 \\
   276‚Üí$(0 \approx e, \mu, \tau)$ & 0.6667 & 0.001\% & 314 \\
   277‚Üí\midrule
   278‚Üí\multicolumn{4}{c}{\textit{Full triples}} \\
   279‚Üí$(e, \mu, \tau)$ & 0.66666 & 0.001\% & 314 \\
   280‚Üí$(-\pi, D_s, B)$ & 0.6674 & 0.10\% & 1230 \\
   281‚Üí$(-s, c, b)$ & 0.6750 & 1.24\% & 913 \\
   282‚Üí$(c, b, t)$ & 0.6695 & 0.42\% & 22\,770 \\
   283‚Üí\bottomrule
   284‚Üí\end{tabular}
   285‚Üí\end{center}
   286‚Üí
   287‚ÜíThe lepton triple is unique: seed and full triple are nearly 
   288‚Üíidentical, because the electron mass is small enough to serve as 
   289‚Üíthe ``zero'' while being nonzero enough to make $Q$ extremely 
   290‚Üíprecise.
   291‚Üí
   292‚Üí\subsection{Orthogonality of seed and full triples}
   293‚Üí
   294‚ÜíEach Koide triple defines a perturbation vector 
   295‚Üí$\vec{z} = (z_1, z_2, z_3)$ constrained to the plane $\sum z_k = 0$ 
   296‚Üíwith length $|\vec{z}|^2 = 3z_0^2$. The angle between seed and 
   297‚Üífull triples measures how much the breaking rotates the spectrum 
   298‚Üíin charge space:
   299‚Üí
   300‚Üí\begin{center}
   301‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lc}
   302‚Üí\toprule
   303‚ÜíPair of triples & Angle \\
   304‚Üí\midrule
   305‚Üí$(0,s,c)$ vs.\ $(-s,c,b)$ & $22^\circ$ \\
   306‚Üí$(0,\pi,D_s)$ vs.\ $(-\pi,D_s,B)$ & $26^\circ$ \\
   307‚Üí$(e,\mu,\tau)$ vs.\ $(0,\mu,\tau)$ & $0.8^\circ$ \\
   308‚Üí$(0,s,c)$ vs.\ $(0,\pi,D_s)$ & $0.3^\circ$ \\
   309‚Üí\bottomrule
   310‚Üí\end{tabular}
   311‚Üí\end{center}
   312‚Üí
   313‚ÜíTwo results are notable. First, the quark seed $(0,s,c)$ and meson 
   314‚Üíseed $(0,\pi,D_s)$ are \textbf{nearly parallel} ($0.3^\circ$): the 
   315‚Üímeson seed is the composite image of the quark seed, with the GOR 
   316‚Üímap preserving direction while changing scale. Second, the lepton 
   317‚Üítriple is essentially its own seed ($0.8^\circ$)---the bloom has 
   318‚Üíbarely begun.

   321‚Üí%======================================================================
   322‚Üí\section{The Meson Koide Triple: $(-\pi, D_s, B)$}
   323‚Üí\label{sec:mesonkoide}
   324‚Üí%======================================================================
   325‚Üí
   326‚ÜíWith the pion charge negative, the triple $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ satisfies:
   327‚Üí\begin{equation}
   328‚ÜíQ(-\pi, D_s, B) = 
   329‚Üí\frac{m_\pi + m_{D_s} + m_B}{(-\sqrt{m_\pi} + \sqrt{m_{D_s}} + \sqrt{m_B})^2}
   330‚Üí= 0.6674\,,
   331‚Üí\end{equation}
   332‚Üídeviating from $2/3$ by $0.10\%$---the second most precise Koide 
   333‚Üítriple known. The energy balance:
   334‚Üí\begin{equation}
   335‚Üí\frac{\langle z_k^2\rangle}{z_0^2} = 1.002\,.
   336‚Üí\end{equation}
   337‚Üí
   338‚ÜíFor comparison, the ``standard'' all-positive quark triples $(d,s,b)$ 
   339‚Üíand $(u,c,t)$ deviate from $Q = 2/3$ by 9.7\% and 27\% respectively 
   340‚Üíat $\overline{\text{MS}}$ masses. \textbf{The meson triple is more 
   341‚Üíprecise than any quark triple} except $(c,b,t)$.
   342‚Üí
   343‚ÜíThe pion's negative charge has physical meaning: as a pseudo-Goldstone 
   344‚Üíboson, the pion would be massless ($\zch = 0$) if chiral symmetry were 
   345‚Üíexact. It sits just below zero ($\zch_\pi = -11.81$), approaching zero 
   346‚Üífrom the negative side as $m_q \to 0$. The sign of $\zch$ encodes 
   347‚ÜíGoldstone nature: pseudo-Goldstone bosons sit on the negative side 
   348‚Üíof the critical point, having crossed through zero from positive 
   349‚Üíterritory during chiral symmetry breaking.
   350‚Üí
   351‚Üí%======================================================================
   352‚Üí\section{The Third Tuple: $(c, b, t)$}
   353‚Üí\label{sec:cbt}
   354‚Üí%======================================================================
   355‚Üí
   356‚Üí\subsection{A tuple without mesons}
   357‚Üí
   358‚ÜíThe triple $(c, b, t)$ with all positive signs gives $Q = 0.6695$, 
   359‚Üídeviating by $0.42\%$. Its vacuum energy $v_0^2 \approx 22.8\GeV$ 
   360‚Üíis far above the QCD scale.
   361‚Üí
   362‚ÜíThis triple has \textbf{no meson counterpart}: the top quark decays 
   363‚Üíweakly ($t \to Wb$, lifetime $\sim 5 \times 10^{-25}$~s) before the 
   364‚Üístrong interaction can bind it ($\tau_{\text{QCD}} \sim 1/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}} 
   365‚Üí\sim 10^{-24}$~s). No $T$-meson ($t\bar{q}$) exists. The meson matrix 
   366‚Üí$M^i{}_j$ extends only to $b$-flavored states; the top sector lives 
   367‚Üípurely at the quark level.
   368‚Üí
   369‚Üí\subsection{Diquarks and the top}
   370‚Üí
   371‚ÜíIn the sBootstrap, diquarks $[q_i q_j]$ are the scalar partners of 
   372‚Üíantiquarks $\bar{q}_k$, with the baryon acting as the SUSY generator 
   373‚Üí\cite{CattoGursey, Miyazawa}. For five active flavors 
   374‚Üí$(u, d, s, c, b)$, the diquarks form a representation of $SU(5)_f$.
   375‚Üí
   376‚ÜíThe top diquarks $[tq]$ are kinematic objects only---they do not 
   377‚Üíhadronize. Yet their masses should follow the same pattern as other 
   378‚Üídiquarks: \textbf{there is no dynamical reason for top diquark masses 
   379‚Üíto differ from the others}. The $(c,b,t)$ Koide triple governs the 
   380‚Üíquark masses that enter the diquark sector, and its energy balance 
   381‚Üí$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ constrains the possible diquark 
   382‚Üíspectrum even though the top diquarks are never observed as bound 
   383‚Üístates.
   384‚Üí
   385‚Üí\subsection{The $(c,b,t)$ triple has no seed}
   386‚Üí
   387‚ÜíUnlike the $(0,s,c)$ and $(0,\pi,D_s)$ seeds, there is no obvious 
   388‚Üí$(0, x, y)$ seed for the $(c,b,t)$ triple. No pair of quarks in this 
   389‚Üísector satisfies $\sqrt{m_{\text{light}}}/\sqrt{m_{\text{heavy}}} 
   390‚Üí\approx 2 - \sqrt{3}$: one has $\sqrt{m_c}/\sqrt{m_t} = 0.086$ 
   391‚Üí(too small) and $\sqrt{m_c}/\sqrt{m_b} = 0.551$ (too large).
   392‚Üí
   393‚ÜíThis means $(c,b,t)$ is \emph{not} a ``bloomed seed'' in the same 
   394‚Üísense as $(-s,c,b)$. It may represent a fundamentally different 
   395‚Üístructure---a triple that was never seeded by a massless member but 
   396‚Üíinstead emerged fully formed at a higher breaking scale, perhaps 
   397‚Üíconnected to electroweak symmetry breaking rather than to chiral 
   398‚Üísymmetry breaking.
   399‚Üí
   400‚Üí%======================================================================
   401‚Üí\section{The Diquark Sector and SU(5) Flavor}
   402‚Üí\label{sec:diquarks}
   403‚Üí%======================================================================
   404‚Üí
   405‚Üí\subsection{Counting partners}
   406‚Üí
   407‚ÜíIn the full sBootstrap \cite{Rivero2005}, the scalar partners of 
   408‚Üíthe Standard Model fermions include both mesons ($q\bar{q}$) and 
   409‚Üídiquarks ($qq$). For five active flavors, the meson matrix gives 
   410‚Üí$5 \times 5 = 25$ scalars. The diquarks $[q_i q_j]$ form a 
   411‚Üírepresentation of $SU(5)_f$:
   412‚Üí\begin{equation}
   413‚Üí\mathbf{5} \otimes \mathbf{5} = \overline{\mathbf{10}}_A 
   414‚Üí\oplus \mathbf{15}_S\,.
   415‚Üí\end{equation}
   416‚Üí
   417‚ÜíThe symmetric $\mathbf{15}_S$ gives 15 diquarks including 
   418‚Üísame-flavor states $[uu]$, $[dd]$, \ldots, while the antisymmetric 
   419‚Üí$\overline{\mathbf{10}}_A$ gives 10 diquarks with $i \neq j$ only.
   420‚Üí
   421‚Üí\subsection{Why antisymmetric?}
   422‚Üí
   423‚ÜíFor the sBootstrap to produce the exact count of scalar partners 
   424‚Üímatching the Standard Model fermion content, the diquarks must 
   425‚Üítransform in the \textbf{antisymmetric} $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$ 
   426‚Üí\cite{Rivero2005}. The argument:
   427‚Üí
   428‚ÜíThe 25 mesons account for the slepton and ``internal'' squark 
   429‚Üísectors. The remaining squarks must come from diquarks. The 
   430‚Üí$\overline{\mathbf{10}}$ provides exactly 10 diquarks: $[ud]$, 
   431‚Üí$[us]$, $[uc]$, $[ub]$, $[ds]$, $[dc]$, $[db]$, $[sc]$, $[sb]$, 
   432‚Üí$[cb]$. With their antiparticles, this gives 20 scalar states. The 
   433‚Üítotal $25 + 10 = 35$ (or $25 + 20 = 45$ counting antiparticles 
   434‚Üíseparately) matches the scalar content of the Supersymmetric 
   435‚ÜíStandard Model.
   436‚Üí
   437‚ÜíThe symmetric $\mathbf{15}$ would give 15 diquarks, overcounting 
   438‚Üíby 5 (the same-flavor states). Moreover, the exotic $+4/3$ charged 
   439‚Üídiquarks $[uu]$, $[uc]$, $[cc]$ have no lepton partner among known 
   440‚Üíparticles---a fatal obstacle.
   441‚Üí
   442‚Üí\subsection{The Pauli problem}
   443‚Üí
   444‚ÜíThe antisymmetric flavor requirement conflicts with a naive 
   445‚Üíapplication of Fermi statistics. For spin-0, $L=0$ diquarks in 
   446‚Üíthe color $\bar{3}$ (antisymmetric) channel:
   447‚Üí
   448‚Üí\begin{center}
   449‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lcl}
   450‚Üí\toprule
   451‚ÜíProperty & Symmetry & Wavefunction \\
   452‚Üí\midrule
   453‚ÜíColor $\bar{3}$ & Antisymmetric & $\epsilon_{abc} q^b q^c$ \\
   454‚ÜíSpin 0 & Antisymmetric & $(\uparrow\downarrow - \downarrow\uparrow)/\sqrt{2}$ \\
   455‚ÜíSpatial $L=0$ & Symmetric & $\psi(r)$ \\
   456‚Üí\midrule
   457‚ÜíOverall & Must be A & $A \times A \times S = S$ \quad$(\neq A!)$ \\
   458‚Üí\bottomrule
   459‚Üí\end{tabular}
   460‚Üí\end{center}
   461‚Üí
   462‚ÜíThe product is symmetric---\textbf{flavor must also be symmetric} 
   463‚Üíto make the overall wavefunction antisymmetric. But the sBootstrap 
   464‚Üírequires antisymmetric flavor.
   465‚Üí
   466‚ÜíThis is one of the deepest puzzles of the program. Possible 
   467‚Üíresolutions:
   468‚Üí\begin{itemize}
   469‚Üí\item \textbf{$L = 1$ diquarks:} Adding orbital angular momentum 
   470‚Üíintroduces spatial antisymmetry. Then color(A) $\times$ spin(A) 
   471‚Üí$\times$ space(A) $\times$ flavor = overall A requires 
   472‚Üíflavor(S)---still wrong. But $L=1$ with spin-1 gives spin(S), 
   473‚Üíand then color(A) $\times$ spin(S) $\times$ space(A) $\times$ 
   474‚Üíflavor(?) = A requires flavor(A). This works but requires both 
   475‚Üí$L=1$ and spin-1 diquarks, departing from the simplest scalar 
   476‚Üídiquark picture.
   477‚Üí\item \textbf{Dynamical projection:} A binding mechanism that 
   478‚Üíeffectively projects onto the flavor-antisymmetric channel, perhaps 
   479‚Üíthrough instanton effects or a condensate in the breaking sector.
   480‚Üí\item \textbf{The SO(32) route:} In the string-theoretic embedding 
   481‚Üí\cite{Rivero2005}, the $SO(32)$ spinor representation automatically 
   482‚Üíproduces the correct antisymmetric structure. The Pauli constraint 
   483‚Üíis an artifact of treating diquarks as simple two-quark states; in 
   484‚Üíthe string picture, they are fundamental objects of the open-string 
   485‚Üísector and the symmetry is determined by the gauge group, not by 
   486‚Üíquark statistics.
   487‚Üí\end{itemize}
   488‚Üí
   489‚Üí\subsection{The SO(32) connection}
   490‚Üí
   491‚ÜíThe sBootstrap counting matches a decomposition of the $SO(32)$ gauge 
   492‚Üígroup familiar from Type~I/heterotic string theory. Under 
   493‚Üí$SO(32) \supset SU(5) \times \ldots$, the adjoint or spinor 
   494‚Üírepresentations produce exactly the right combination of mesons 
   495‚Üí(in $\mathbf{5} \otimes \bar{\mathbf{5}}$) and antisymmetric 
   496‚Üídiquarks (in $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$). The 496-dimensional adjoint 
   497‚Üíof $SO(32)$ decomposes to include the 24 of $SU(5)$ (gauge bosons), 
   498‚Üíthe $\mathbf{5} \otimes \bar{\mathbf{5}} = \mathbf{1} + \mathbf{24}$ 
   499‚Üí(mesons/sleptons), and the $\overline{\mathbf{10}} + \mathbf{10}$ 
   500‚Üí(diquarks/squarks).
   501‚Üí
   502‚ÜíThis is suggestive: the same structure that gives anomaly-free string 
   503‚Üícompactifications also gives the correct sBootstrap particle count 
   504‚Üíwith the required antisymmetric diquarks. Whether this is a 
   505‚Üícoincidence or a hint of UV completion is an open question.
   506‚Üí
   507‚Üí%======================================================================
   508‚Üí\section{SUSY Breaking: From Seeds to Spectrum}
   509‚Üí\label{sec:breaking}
   510‚Üí%======================================================================
   511‚Üí
   512‚ÜíThe SUSY-breaking dynamics must accomplish three things simultaneously:
   513‚Üígenerate the seed-to-bloom transition for each tuple, preserve the 
   514‚Üíenergy-balance condition $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ through the 
   515‚Üíbreaking, and produce the correct splitting between meson and lepton 
   516‚Üímasses at scale $f_\pi$. In this section we develop the framework in 
   517‚Üídetail, connecting the three standard SUSY-breaking mechanisms to the 
   518‚Üíthree-tuple structure.
   519‚Üí
   520‚Üí\subsection{The Seiberg framework}
   521‚Üí
   522‚ÜíThe natural arena is $\mathcal{N}=1$ SQCD with gauge group $SU(3)_c$ 
   523‚Üíand $N_f$ flavors of chiral superfields $Q^i$, $\bar{Q}_j$ in the 
   524‚Üífundamental and antifundamental representations. For $N_f = N_c = 3$ 
   525‚Üí(the case relevant to light QCD), Seiberg's exact results 
   526‚Üí\cite{Seiberg1995} establish that the theory confines, with the 
   527‚Üílow-energy degrees of freedom being the composite meson superfield
   528‚Üí\begin{equation}
   529‚ÜíM^i{}_j = Q^i \cdot \bar{Q}_j\,,
   530‚Üí\end{equation}
   531‚Üía $3\times 3$ matrix of chiral superfields, together with baryons 
   532‚Üí$B = \epsilon_{ijk}Q^iQ^jQ^k$ and $\bar{B}$. These satisfy the 
   533‚Üíquantum-modified constraint
   534‚Üí\begin{equation}
   535‚Üí\label{eq:seiberg}
   536‚Üí\det M - B\bar{B} = \Lambda^{2N_c}\,,
   537‚Üí\end{equation}
   538‚Üíwhere $\Lambda$ is the dynamical scale.
   539‚Üí
   540‚ÜíEach entry $M^i{}_j$ is a chiral superfield containing a complex 
   541‚Üíscalar (the meson) and a Weyl fermion (the lepton, in the sBootstrap 
   542‚Üíidentification). The scalar and fermion are degenerate in the SUSY 
   543‚Üílimit; SUSY breaking splits them.
   544‚Üí
   545‚Üí\subsection{The three chiral superfields of each seed}
   546‚Üí
   547‚ÜíThe seed triple $(0, \zch_a, \zch_b)$ maps naturally onto three 
   548‚Üíchiral superfields with the following mass structure before breaking:
   549‚Üí
   550‚Üí\begin{center}
   551‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lcl}
   552‚Üí\toprule
   553‚ÜíSuperfield & Mass & Role \\
   554‚Üí\midrule
   555‚Üí$\Phi_0$ & $m_0 = 0$ & Massless: flat direction \\
   556‚Üí$\Phi_a$ & $m_a = \zch_a^2$ & Light massive member \\
   557‚Üí$\Phi_b$ & $m_b = \zch_b^2$ & Heavy massive member \\
   558‚Üí\bottomrule
   559‚Üí\end{tabular}
   560‚Üí\end{center}
   561‚Üí
   562‚ÜíThe ratio $\zch_a/\zch_b = 2 - \sqrt{3}$ is enforced by the 
   563‚ÜíKoide energy-balance condition applied to the seed.
   564‚Üí
   565‚ÜíIn the Seiberg description, these three superfields are entries of 
   566‚Üíthe meson matrix $M^i{}_j$. For the $(0, s, c)$ seed, the relevant 
   567‚Üíentries involve the strange and charm quarks; $\Phi_0$ is the entry 
   568‚Üíwhose quark-mass combination vanishes in the seed limit (i.e., the 
   569‚Üídirection in flavor space where $m_i + m_j \to 0$ through GOR).
   570‚Üí
   571‚Üí\subsection{F-term breaking: the O'Raifeartaigh mechanism}
   572‚Üí\label{subsec:fterm}
   573‚Üí
   574‚ÜíThe O'Raifeartaigh model \cite{ORaifeartaigh} is the simplest 
   575‚Üírealization of spontaneous $F$-term SUSY breaking. With three chiral 
   576‚Üísuperfields, the superpotential
   577‚Üí\begin{equation}
   578‚ÜíW = f\,\Phi_0 + m\,\Phi_1\Phi_2 + g\,\Phi_0\Phi_1^2
   579‚Üí\end{equation}
   580‚Üígives $F$-terms:
   581‚Üí\begin{align}
   582‚ÜíF_{\Phi_0}^* &= f + g\,\phi_1^2\,, \\
   583‚ÜíF_{\Phi_1}^* &= m\,\phi_2 + 2g\,\phi_0\phi_1\,, \\
   584‚ÜíF_{\Phi_2}^* &= m\,\phi_1\,.
   585‚Üí\end{align}
   586‚ÜíIf $f \neq 0$ and $m \neq 0$, then $F_{\Phi_2}^* = 0$ requires 
   587‚Üí$\phi_1 = 0$, but then $F_{\Phi_0}^* = f \neq 0$: SUSY is 
   588‚Üí\textbf{necessarily broken}. The key features are:
   589‚Üí\begin{enumerate}
   590‚Üí\item A massless field ($\Phi_0$) exists at tree level---it is the 
   591‚Üípseudo-modulus.
   592‚Üí\item The breaking is triggered because $F_{\Phi_0}$ cannot vanish.
   593‚Üí\item After including loop corrections, $\Phi_0$ acquires a mass 
   594‚Üí(the Coleman--Weinberg potential lifts the flat direction).
   595‚Üí\end{enumerate}
   596‚Üí
   597‚ÜíThe structural parallel with the seed triples is:
   598‚Üí
   599‚Üí\begin{center}
   600‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lll}
   601‚Üí\toprule
   602‚ÜíO'Raifeartaigh & Seed triple & After breaking \\
   603‚Üí\midrule
   604‚Üí$\Phi_0$ (massless) & 0 member & Lifted to $\zch < 0$ \\
   605‚Üí$\Phi_1$ (mass $m$) & $\zch_a$ (light) & Shifted \\
   606‚Üí$\Phi_2$ (mass $m$) & $\zch_b$ (heavy) & Shifted \\
   607‚Üí\midrule
   608‚Üí$f = \langle F\rangle$ & --- & $f_\pi$ \\
   609‚Üí\bottomrule
   610‚Üí\end{tabular}
   611‚Üí\end{center}
   612‚Üí
   613‚ÜíThis parallel is \emph{structural}, not quantitative: the minimal 
   614‚ÜíO'Raifeartaigh model gives $\Phi_1$ and $\Phi_2$ the same mass 
   615‚Üíparameter $m$, whereas the seed triple has a mass ratio 
   616‚Üí$m_a/m_b = (2 - \sqrt{3})^2 \approx 0.07$---far from degenerate. 
   617‚ÜíThe hierarchy between the two massive members must come from 
   618‚Üíadditional dynamics beyond the minimal O'Raifeartaigh superpotential, 
   619‚Üíeither through a generalized superpotential with two distinct mass 
   620‚Üíparameters $W = f\Phi_0 + m_1\Phi_1\Phi_2 + g\Phi_0\Phi_1^2$ 
   621‚Üí(where the Koide condition constrains $m_1/m_2$), or through loop 
   622‚Üícorrections that split the initially degenerate pair. What the 
   623‚ÜíO'Raifeartaigh structure \emph{does} provide is the essential 
   624‚Üífeature: a massless flat direction whose $F$-term is forced nonzero.
   625‚Üí
   626‚ÜíThe identification $f = \langle F_{\Phi_0}\rangle \sim f_\pi$ is the 
   627‚ÜísBootstrap's central claim: the SUSY-breaking $F$-term is the chiral 
   628‚Üícondensate parameter.
   629‚Üí
   630‚ÜíIn SQCD, this is realized dynamically rather than through a 
   631‚Üísuperpotential: the chiral condensate $\qqbar \neq 0$ acts as the 
   632‚Üínonzero $F$-term, the impossibility of restoring chiral symmetry 
   633‚Üíin the QCD vacuum is the dynamical analogue of $f \neq 0$, and the 
   634‚Üípion---the would-be Goldstone boson that acquires mass from explicit 
   635‚Üíchiral breaking---plays the role of $\Phi_0$ whose mass is lifted 
   636‚Üíby loop corrections (here, by quark masses).
   637‚Üí
   638‚Üí\subsection{The Goldstino direction and the Volkov--Akulov realization}
   639‚Üí\label{subsec:va}
   640‚Üí
   641‚ÜíWhen SUSY is spontaneously broken, a massless fermion appears: the 
   642‚ÜíGoldstino, the fermionic analogue of the Goldstone boson. Its decay 
   643‚Üíconstant $f_{\text{VA}}$ sets the SUSY-breaking scale through the 
   644‚ÜíVolkov--Akulov Lagrangian \cite{VolkovAkulov1973}:
   645‚Üí\begin{equation}
   646‚Üí\mathcal{L}_{\text{VA}} = -f_{\text{VA}}^2 \det\!\left(
   647‚Üí\delta_\mu^\nu + \frac{i}{f_{\text{VA}}^2}\,\bar{\lambda}\bar{\sigma}^\nu
   648‚Üí\partial_\mu\lambda\right),
   649‚Üí\end{equation}
   650‚Üíwhere $\lambda$ is the Goldstino field. At low energies, the Goldstino 
   651‚Üícouples derivatively with strength $\sim 1/f_{\text{VA}}^2$, exactly 
   652‚Üías the pion couples with strength $\sim 1/f_\pi^2$ in the chiral 
   653‚ÜíLagrangian.
   654‚Üí
   655‚ÜíThe sBootstrap identification is:
   656‚Üí\begin{equation}
   657‚Üí\label{eq:va_id}
   658‚Üíf_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi\,, \qquad 
   659‚Üí\text{Goldstino} = \text{muon (partner of }\pi\text{)}\,.
   660‚Üí\end{equation}
   661‚ÜíMore precisely, the muon is a \emph{pseudo-Goldstino}: in the 
   662‚Üílimit $m_q \to 0$ where the pion becomes exactly massless (a true 
   663‚ÜíGoldstone boson), its fermionic partner also becomes massless (a 
   664‚Üítrue Goldstino). The finite quark masses explicitly break both 
   665‚Üíchiral symmetry (giving $m_\pi \neq 0$) and the sBootstrap SUSY 
   666‚Üí(giving $m_\mu \neq m_\pi$), with the splitting controlled by 
   667‚Üí$f_\pi$:
   668‚Üí\begin{equation}
   669‚Üím_\pi^2 - m_\mu^2 = f_\pi^2\,.
   670‚Üí\end{equation}
   671‚Üí
   672‚ÜíThis is the Volkov--Akulov relation for the pseudo-Goldstino: the 
   673‚Üímass splitting between the scalar (pion) and its fermionic partner 
   674‚Üí(muon) is set by the SUSY-breaking scale squared.
   675‚Üí
   676‚ÜíThe VA mechanism is not a separate breaking from the F-term: it is 
   677‚Üíthe \emph{low-energy, composite-level description} of the same 
   678‚Üíbreaking. At the microscopic level, $\langle F\rangle \neq 0$ 
   679‚Üíin the meson superfield; at the composite level, this manifests 
   680‚Üías the Goldstino coupling with $f_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi$. These 
   681‚Üítwo quantities---the Goldstino decay constant and the pion decay 
   682‚Üíconstant---are \emph{identified} in the sBootstrap; this is the 
   683‚Üícentral claim, not a derivation.
   684‚Üí
   685‚ÜíThe identification should not be confused with the standard relation 
   686‚Üíbetween the $F$-term VEV and the chiral condensate. In standard 
   687‚ÜíSQCD, $\langle F\rangle \sim |\qqbar|/f_\pi \sim (250\MeV)^3/92\MeV 
   688‚Üí\approx 170{,}000\MeV^2$, while $f_\pi^2 \approx 8{,}500\MeV^2$---a 
   689‚Üífactor of $\sim 20$ discrepancy. The GOR relation 
   690‚Üí$m_\pi^2 f_\pi^2 = -(m_u + m_d)\qqbar$ connects these through the 
   691‚Üísmall quark masses, but does not equate them. The sBootstrap 
   692‚Üíidentification $f_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi$ is therefore a 
   693‚Üí\emph{low-energy effective} statement about the composite sector, 
   694‚Üínot a microscopic identity. It says: the scale at which SUSY 
   695‚Üíappears broken in the meson--lepton spectrum is $f_\pi$, regardless 
   696‚Üíof the microscopic $F$-term that produces this breaking.
   697‚Üí
   698‚ÜíA note on quantum numbers: the Goldstino of spontaneously broken 
   699‚Üíspacetime SUSY is a neutral fermion (the fermionic component of the 
   700‚Üísuperfield whose $F$-term breaks SUSY). The muon carries electric 
   701‚Üícharge and lepton number. The sBootstrap SUSY, however, is not a 
   702‚Üíspacetime symmetry but an emergent hadronic symmetry---a 
   703‚ÜíMiyazawa-type superalgebra \cite{Miyazawa} acting on composite 
   704‚Üístates. In this context, the ``Goldstino'' need not carry the 
   705‚Üíquantum numbers of a spacetime supercharge; it carries the quantum 
   706‚Üínumbers appropriate to the broken hadronic generator. The muon's 
   707‚Üícharge is inherited from the pion multiplet it partners.
   708‚Üí
   709‚Üí\subsection{D-term breaking: electromagnetic splittings}
   710‚Üí\label{subsec:dterm}
   711‚Üí
   712‚ÜíThe unbroken $U(1)_{\text{em}}$ gauge symmetry allows a 
   713‚ÜíFayet--Iliopoulos (FI) term \cite{FayetIliopoulos}:
   714‚Üí\begin{equation}
   715‚Üí\mathcal{L}_{\text{FI}} = \xi\,D_{\text{em}}\,,
   716‚Üí\end{equation}
   717‚Üíwhich shifts scalar masses by $\delta m^2 = Q_{\text{em}}\,\xi$, 
   718‚Üíwhere $Q_{\text{em}}$ is the electric charge. This is the 
   719‚Üísupersymmetric version of the electromagnetic mass difference.
   720‚Üí
   721‚ÜíIn QCD without SUSY, the electromagnetic mass splitting of the 
   722‚Üípion is given by the Das--Guralnik--Mathur--Low--Young (DGMLY) 
   723‚Üíformula \cite{DGMLY1967}:
   724‚Üí\begin{equation}
   725‚Üím_{\pi^\pm}^2 - m_{\pi^0}^2 = \frac{3\alpha_{\text{em}}}{4\pi}\,
   726‚Üí\frac{m_\rho^2}{f_\pi^2}\,f_\pi^2 
   727‚Üí\approx (35.5\MeV)^2\,,
   728‚Üí\end{equation}
   729‚Üíwhich in the SUSY framework becomes a D-term contribution:
   730‚Üí\begin{equation}
   731‚Üí\xi \sim \frac{3\alpha_{\text{em}}}{4\pi}\,m_\rho^2 
   732‚Üí\sim (35\MeV)^2\,.
   733‚Üí\end{equation}
   734‚Üí
   735‚ÜíIn charge space, the D-term shifts $\zch$ by an amount proportional 
   736‚Üíto $Q_{\text{em}}$:
   737‚Üí\begin{equation}
   738‚Üí\zch \to \zch + \delta\zch\,, \qquad 
   739‚Üí\delta\zch \approx \frac{Q_{\text{em}}\,\xi}{2\zch}\,.
   740‚Üí\end{equation}
   741‚ÜíFor the pion ($Q_{\text{em}} = \pm 1$, $\zch \approx 11.8$): 
   742‚Üí$\delta\zch \approx 0.2\MeV^{1/2}$, matching the observed 
   743‚Üí$\zch_{\pi^\pm} - \zch_{\pi^0} = 11.82 - 11.62 = 0.20\MeV^{1/2}$.
   744‚Üí
   745‚ÜíThe D-term effect is subleading to the F-term: 
   746‚Üí$\xi \sim (35\MeV)^2$ versus $f_\pi^2 \sim (92\MeV)^2$. It 
   747‚Üíoperates \emph{within} each supermultiplet entry, splitting charged 
   748‚Üífrom neutral mesons, while the F-term splits bosons from fermions 
   749‚Üíacross the supermultiplet.
   750‚Üí
   751‚ÜíWitten's inequality \cite{Witten1983} guarantees 
   752‚Üí$m_{\pi^\pm} > m_{\pi^0}$ in QCD with electromagnetism---the 
   753‚Üícharged pion is always heavier. In the SUSY framework this 
   754‚Üíbecomes the statement that the D-term contribution is positive 
   755‚Üífor $Q_{\text{em}} \neq 0$, consistent with the FI sign.
   756‚Üí
   757‚Üí\subsection{Mapping the three mechanisms to the three tuples}
   758‚Üí\label{subsec:mapping}
   759‚Üí
   760‚ÜíThe three-tuple structure suggests a layered breaking:
   761‚Üí
   762‚Üí\paragraph{Layer 1: F-term (chiral condensate, scale $\sim f_\pi$).}
   763‚ÜíThe chiral condensate $\qqbar \neq 0$ breaks both chiral symmetry 
   764‚Üíand the sBootstrap SUSY. This is the primary breaking, affecting 
   765‚Üíthe light sector. The seed triples $(0, s, c)$ and $(0, \pi, D_s)$ 
   766‚Üíare the pre-breaking configurations; the massless member is the flat 
   767‚Üídirection whose $F$-term is forced nonzero by confinement. After 
   768‚Üíbreaking:
   769‚Üí\begin{itemize}
   770‚Üí\item The zero member is lifted and acquires negative charge 
   771‚Üí($\zch < 0$), becoming the sign-flipped member of the full triple 
   772‚Üí($-s$, $-\pi$).
   773‚Üí\item The full triples $(-s,c,b)$ and $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ emerge, 
   774‚Üíincorporating the $b$-quark and $B$-meson sectors.
   775‚Üí\item The lepton triple $(e, \mu, \tau)$ is the fermionic shadow: 
   776‚Üíthe leptons acquire masses through the same condensate, with the 
   777‚Üímuon as pseudo-Goldstino.
   778‚Üí\end{itemize}
   779‚Üí
   780‚Üí\paragraph{Layer 2: D-term (electromagnetism, scale $\sim \alpha_{\text{em}} m_\rho$).}
   781‚ÜíThe $U(1)_{\text{em}}$ FI term generates electromagnetic mass 
   782‚Üísplittings within each multiplet. This is a perturbative correction 
   783‚Üíon top of the F-term breaking, affecting only charged particles. 
   784‚ÜíIt does not create new Koide triples but shifts existing ones by 
   785‚Üí$O(\alpha_{\text{em}})$.
   786‚Üí
   787‚Üí\paragraph{Layer 3: The heavy sector (scale $\sim v_0^{(cbt)}$).}
   788‚ÜíThe $(c,b,t)$ triple at $v_0^2 \approx 22.8\GeV$ operates far 
   789‚Üíabove the chiral scale. It has no seed (no massless member), no 
   790‚Üímeson shadow (top does not hadronize), and its breaking may be 
   791‚Üíconnected to a different sector of the theory---possibly the 
   792‚Üíelectroweak Higgs mechanism.
   793‚Üí
   794‚ÜíThis layered structure involves three distinct scales, each with 
   795‚Üídifferent physical meaning:
   796‚Üí\begin{align}
   797‚Üí\text{F-term (boson--fermion splitting):} &\quad 
   798‚Üíf_\pi \approx 92\MeV\,, \quad f_\pi^2 \approx 8{,}500\MeV^2\,; \\
   799‚Üí\text{D-term (EM fine structure):} &\quad 
   800‚Üí\xi^{1/2} \approx 35\MeV\,, \quad \xi \approx 1{,}200\MeV^2\,; \\
   801‚Üí\text{Heavy sector (Koide scale):} &\quad 
   802‚Üív_0^{(cbt)} \approx 151\MeV^{1/2}\,, \quad (v_0^{(cbt)})^2 \approx 22{,}800\MeV\,.
   803‚Üí\end{align}
   804‚ÜíComparing the squared scales: $f_\pi^2 \gg \xi$ (F-term dominates 
   805‚Üíover D-term by a factor $\sim 7$, consistent with the D-term being 
   806‚Üía perturbative $O(\alpha_{\text{em}})$ correction). The heavy-sector 
   807‚Üíscale $(v_0^{(cbt)})^2$ carries dimensions of $\MeV$ (not $\MeV^2$) 
   808‚Üíbecause $v_0$ is a charge ($\MeV^{1/2}$), not an energy; direct 
   809‚Üícomparison with $f_\pi$ requires specifying which physical quantity 
   810‚Üíis being compared.
   811‚Üí
   812‚Üí\subsection{The O'Raifeartaigh structure in SQCD}
   813‚Üí
   814‚ÜíIn Seiberg's SQCD with $N_f = N_c = 3$, the quantum constraint 
   815‚Üí\eqref{eq:seiberg} provides a natural setting for the 
   816‚ÜíO'Raifeartaigh mechanism. The constraint $\det M - B\bar{B} = \Lambda^6$ 
   817‚Üímeans the moduli space has a specific geometry: not all field directions 
   818‚Üíare flat. When quark masses are added via a tree-level superpotential 
   819‚Üí$W_{\text{tree}} = m_i M^i{}_i$ (sum over flavors), the $F$-terms become:
   820‚Üí\begin{equation}
   821‚ÜíF_{M^i{}_j} = m_i\,\delta^i_j + \Lambda^6\,(\text{constraint terms})\,.
   822‚Üí\end{equation}
   823‚Üí
   824‚ÜíFor the seed triple $(0, s, c)$, the ``$0$'' member corresponds to a 
   825‚Üídirection in the meson matrix where the tree-level mass vanishes---the 
   826‚Üíentry involving the lightest quark combination. In the exact chiral 
   827‚Üílimit ($m_u = m_d = 0$), this entry is truly massless, and the 
   828‚Üíconstraint \eqref{eq:seiberg} forces $\langle F\rangle \neq 0$ for 
   829‚Üíthat entry. This is the dynamical O'Raifeartaigh mechanism: 
   830‚Üíconfinement (encoded in $\Lambda^6$) plays the role of the parameter 
   831‚Üí$f$ in the superpotential, and the impossibility of setting all 
   832‚Üí$F$-terms to zero is a consequence of the strong dynamics rather 
   833‚Üíthan a choice of superpotential.
   834‚Üí
   835‚ÜíThe ISS mechanism (Intriligator--Seiberg--Shih \cite{ISS2006}) 
   836‚Üíestablishes metastable SUSY-breaking vacua for $N_c < N_f < 3N_c/2$, 
   837‚Üíwhere the rank condition in the magnetic dual prevents all $F$-terms 
   838‚Üífrom vanishing. The sBootstrap case $N_f = N_c = 3$ is strictly 
   839‚Üí\emph{below} the ISS window---at $N_f = N_c$ the low-energy 
   840‚Üídescription is qualitatively different, governed by the 
   841‚Üíquantum-modified constraint \eqref{eq:seiberg} on the moduli space 
   842‚Üírather than by a free magnetic dual. However, the physical QCD has 
   843‚Üí$N_f = 3$ light flavors and $N_f = 4$--$5$ if charm and bottom are 
   844‚Üíincluded at their respective thresholds, which does fall within the 
   845‚ÜíISS window for $N_c = 3$. The sBootstrap's multi-scale structure---with 
   846‚Üíthe seed triples involving $s$, $c$, and $b$ quarks at different 
   847‚Üíscales---may require treating the effective $N_f$ as 
   848‚Üíscale-dependent, entering the ISS regime above the strange threshold.
   849‚Üí
   850‚Üí\subsection{How the three supermultiplets join}
   851‚Üí
   852‚ÜíThe seed-to-bloom transition generates three separate Koide triples. 
   853‚ÜíBut the full sBootstrap requires these triples to be \emph{embedded 
   854‚Üíin a single meson matrix}. The $3\times 3$ light-flavor matrix is:
   855‚Üí
   856‚Üí\begin{equation}
   857‚ÜíM^{(3)} = \begin{pmatrix}
   858‚Üíu\bar{u} & u\bar{d} & u\bar{s} \\
   859‚Üíd\bar{u} & d\bar{d} & d\bar{s} \\
   860‚Üís\bar{u} & s\bar{d} & s\bar{s}
   861‚Üí\end{pmatrix}
   862‚Üí\;\longleftrightarrow\;
   863‚Üí\begin{pmatrix}
   864‚Üí\pi^0/\eta & \pi^+ & K^+ \\
   865‚Üí\pi^- & \pi^0/\eta & K^0 \\
   866‚ÜíK^- & \bar{K}^0 & \eta'
   867‚Üí\end{pmatrix}.
   868‚Üí\end{equation}
   869‚Üí
   870‚ÜíWhen charm and bottom are included, the matrix extends to $5\times 5$. 
   871‚ÜíWe display it schematically, with the members of the meson Koide 
   872‚Üítriple $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ marked by boxes:
   873‚Üí
   874‚Üí\begin{equation}
   875‚ÜíM^{(5)} = \begin{pmatrix}
   876‚Üí\pi^0/\eta & \boxed{\pi^+} & K^+ & D^0 & \boxed{B^+} \\
   877‚Üí\pi^- & \pi^0/\eta & K^0 & D^- & B^0 \\
   878‚ÜíK^- & \bar{K}^0 & \eta' & \boxed{D_s^-} & B_s \\
   879‚Üí\bar{D}^0 & D^+ & D_s^+ & \eta_c & B_c \\
   880‚ÜíB^- & \bar{B}^0 & \bar{B}_s & \bar{B}_c & \eta_b
   881‚Üí\end{pmatrix},
   882‚Üí\end{equation}
   883‚Üíwhere rows are indexed by $(u, d, s, c, b)$ and columns by 
   884‚Üí$(\bar{u}, \bar{d}, \bar{s}, \bar{c}, \bar{b})$.
   885‚Üí
   886‚ÜíThe three members of the meson Koide triple occupy different 
   887‚Üíoff-diagonal blocks of this matrix: $\pi^+ = u\bar{d}$ is in the 
   888‚Üílight $(u,d)$ block, $D_s^- = s\bar{c}$ bridges the light-charm 
   889‚Üísector, and $B^+ = u\bar{b}$ bridges the light-bottom sector. They 
   890‚Üído not form a ``slice'' in any simple linear-algebraic sense---they 
   891‚Üíare scattered entries connected by the Koide condition on their 
   892‚Üí\emph{masses}, not by their positions in the matrix. The meson seed 
   893‚Üí$(0, \pi, D_s)$ drops the $B^+$ and replaces it with zero (the 
   894‚Üíentry that would involve the top quark, absent from the matrix).
   895‚Üí
   896‚ÜíThis scattered structure is the central puzzle: the Koide triples 
   897‚Üíare \emph{mass relations} among matrix entries, not submatrices. 
   898‚ÜíThe question is whether the Seiberg constraint 
   899‚Üí$\det M - B\bar{B} = \Lambda^6$ (appropriately generalized to 
   900‚Üí$5 \times 5$), combined with the SUSY-breaking dynamics, produces 
   901‚Üícorrelations among these scattered entries that enforce the 
   902‚Üíenergy-balance condition $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$.
   903‚Üí
   904‚Üí\subsection{The energy balance as a fixed point}
   905‚Üí
   906‚ÜíOne possibility is that the energy-balance condition 
   907‚Üí$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ is a \emph{dynamical attractor}: 
   908‚Üíany set of three masses evolving under the SUSY-breaking dynamics 
   909‚Üíflows toward the Koide surface $Q = 2/3$, as a fixed point of the 
   910‚ÜíRG flow for the soft SUSY-breaking parameters. This is speculative, 
   911‚Üíbut the observation that the condition holds for fundamental fermions 
   912‚Üí(leptons, quarks) \emph{and} composite bosons (mesons) across very 
   913‚Üídifferent scales lends it plausibility. A concrete test would be to 
   914‚Üíshow that the one-loop soft-mass RG equations in $\mathcal{N}=1$ 
   915‚ÜíSQCD with appropriate boundary conditions flow toward 
   916‚Üí$\sigma(\zch)/\bar{\zch} = 1$; this calculation has not yet been 
   917‚Üíperformed.
   918‚Üí
   919‚Üí\subsection{What the $(c,b,t)$ triple tells us}
   920‚Üí
   921‚ÜíThe $(c,b,t)$ triple ($Q = 0.6695$, $v_0^2 = 22.8\GeV$) has 
   922‚Üíneither a seed nor a meson shadow. It operates at a scale far 
   923‚Üíabove $\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$, suggesting its origin is different:
   924‚Üí
   925‚Üí\begin{enumerate}
   926‚Üí\item It may be governed by the electroweak Higgs mechanism rather 
   927‚Üíthan the chiral condensate. The top Yukawa $y_t \approx 1$ is 
   928‚Üí``natural'' in the sense that $m_t \approx v_{\text{EW}}/\sqrt{2}$; 
   929‚Üíthe Koide condition on $(c,b,t)$ would then constrain the Yukawa 
   930‚Üícoupling matrix.
   931‚Üí\item It may represent a Volkov--Akulov realization at the 
   932‚Üíelectroweak scale: if there is a second SUSY breaking at 
   933‚Üí$f' \sim v_{\text{EW}}$, the $(c,b,t)$ triple would be its 
   934‚Üíanalog of the $(\pi, \mu)$ system, but with $f'$ replacing $f_\pi$.
   935‚Üí\item Or it may be a purely kinematic accident---the quark masses 
   936‚Üíhappen to satisfy the Koide condition without a dynamical reason. 
   937‚ÜíThe 0.42\% precision makes this unlikely but not impossible.
   938‚Üí\end{enumerate}
   939‚Üí
   940‚ÜíInterpretation (1) is most natural within the sBootstrap: the 
   941‚Üí$(c,b,t)$ triple involves quarks whose masses are dominated by 
   942‚ÜíYukawa couplings to the Higgs, not by the chiral condensate, and the 
   943‚ÜíKoide condition would then be a constraint on the Yukawa matrix 
   944‚Üírather than on the SQCD dynamics. The chiral sector and the 
   945‚Üíelectroweak sector would each independently produce a Koide-type 
   946‚Üíenergy balance, with the charm and bottom quarks serving as the 
   947‚Üíbridge between the two.
   948‚Üí
   949‚ÜíIf interpretation (2) is also invoked, the sBootstrap has two breaking 
   950‚Üíscales:
   951‚Üí\begin{equation}
   952‚Üíf_{\text{chiral}} = f_\pi \approx 92\MeV\,, \qquad
   953‚Üíf_{\text{EW}} \sim v_{\text{EW}} \approx 246\GeV\,,
   954‚Üí\end{equation}
   955‚Üíand the full spectrum requires both. The light sector (leptons, 
   956‚Üílight mesons) is governed by $f_\pi$; the heavy sector (top, and 
   957‚Üíby extension charm and bottom through the Koide network) is 
   958‚Üígoverned by $v_{\text{EW}}$. The charm and bottom quarks, 
   959‚Üíappearing in both the $(0,s,c)/(-s,c,b)$ light-sector triples 
   960‚Üíand the $(c,b,t)$ heavy-sector triple, are the ``bridges'' 
   961‚Üíbetween the two scales.

   964‚Üí\section{The Electron Mass and the Lepton Seed}
   965‚Üí\label{sec:electron}
   966‚Üí%======================================================================
   967‚Üí
   968‚ÜíThe charged lepton triple $(e, \mu, \tau)$ is the ``almost-seed'': 
   969‚Üí$\zch_e = 0.715\MeV^{1/2}$ is small but nonzero. The electron's 
   970‚Üícharge is near zero because of cancellation in $\zch_e = z_0 + z_e$:
   971‚Üí\begin{equation}
   972‚Üív_0 = 17.72, \quad z_e = -17.00, \quad 
   973‚Üí\zch_e = 17.72 - 17.00 = 0.72\MeV^{1/2}\,.
   974‚Üí\end{equation}
   975‚ÜíA 96\% cancellation. The mass $m_e = 0.72^2 = 0.52\MeV$ is the 
   976‚Üísquare of something already small.
   977‚Üí
   978‚ÜíThe near-vanishing of $m_e$ is a structural property of the Koide 
   979‚Üíembedding, not fine-tuning: the energy balance allows perturbations 
   980‚Üías large as $z_0$. Every Koide triple has one member with 
   981‚Üí$|\zch|/v_0$ small:
   982‚Üí
   983‚Üí\begin{center}
   984‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lccl}
   985‚Üí\toprule
   986‚ÜíTriple & Lightest & $|\zch|/v_0$ & Character \\
   987‚Üí\midrule
   988‚Üí$(e, \mu, \tau)$ & $e$ & 0.040 & Almost-seed \\
   989‚Üí$(-\pi, D_s, B)$ & $\pi$ (neg.) & 0.337 & Full triple \\
   990‚Üí$(c, b, t)$ & $c$ & 0.236 & Full triple \\
   991‚Üí$(-s, c, b)$ & $s$ (neg.) & 0.320 & Full triple \\
   992‚Üí\bottomrule
   993‚Üí\end{tabular}
   994‚Üí\end{center}
   995‚Üí
   996‚ÜíThe electron is by far the most extreme case, consistent with the 
   997‚Üílepton triple being nearest to its seed.

  1000‚Üí\section{Symmetry Restoration}
  1001‚Üí\label{sec:restoration}
  1002‚Üí%======================================================================
  1003‚Üí
  1004‚ÜíThe broken spectrum reassembles as symmetry-breaking effects are 
  1005‚Üíremoved:
  1006‚Üí
  1007‚Üí\begin{enumerate}
  1008‚Üí\item \textbf{Turn off QED:} Electromagnetic splittings vanish 
  1009‚Üí($\pi^\pm = \pi^0$, $K^\pm = K^0$). D-term effects disappear.
  1010‚Üí\item \textbf{Set $m_u = m_d$:} Exact isospin.
  1011‚Üí\item \textbf{Set $m_s = m_d$:} $SU(3)_V$ restored, 
  1012‚Üí$\pi = K = \eta$. Koide splitting $v_1 \to 0$; lepton partners 
  1013‚Üídegenerate.
  1014‚Üí\item \textbf{Send $m_q \to 0$:} Chiral limit, all meson charges 
  1015‚Üí$\to 0$. If sBootstrap SUSY restores, all light lepton masses 
  1016‚Üívanish too. This is the seed configuration: $(0, 0, 0)$.
  1017‚Üí\end{enumerate}
  1018‚Üí
  1019‚ÜíThe seed triples are the ``penultimate'' stage: one member has 
  1020‚Üíreached zero, but the other two retain the $2 - \sqrt{3}$ ratio. 
  1021‚ÜíFull triples are the final broken state. The lepton triple, 
  1022‚Üítrapped between seed and bloom, reveals the hierarchy: 
  1023‚Üíchiral breaking $\to$ seed $\to$ bloom $\to$ full spectrum.
  1024‚Üí
  1025‚ÜíThe direction of the flow also matters: approaching the chiral 
  1026‚Üílimit, the pion charge rises from $\zch = -11.81$ toward zero 
  1027‚Üífrom below. It crosses zero exactly in the chiral limit. This 
  1028‚Üímeans the pion was ``born negative''---its charge sign is set by 
  1029‚Üíthe direction of chiral symmetry breaking, and the seed 
  1030‚Üí$(0, \pi, D_s)$ is the configuration at the moment of crossing.
  1031‚Üí

  1033‚Üí\section{Nature Abhors Chirality}
  1034‚Üí\label{sec:chirality}
  1035‚Üí%======================================================================
  1036‚Üí
  1037‚ÜíA pattern across the Standard Model: every chiral structure is 
  1038‚Üíeliminated in the infrared.
  1039‚Üí
  1040‚Üí\begin{center}
  1041‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lcl}
  1042‚Üí\toprule
  1043‚ÜíUV structure & IR survivor & Mechanism \\
  1044‚Üí\midrule
  1045‚Üí$SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ & $U(1)_{\text{em}}$ & Higgs \\
  1046‚Üí$SU(N_f)_L \times SU(N_f)_R$ & $SU(N_f)_V$ & $\qqbar$ condensate \\
  1047‚Üí$U(1)_A$ & nothing & Anomaly \\
  1048‚Üí$\bar{\theta}$-vacuum & $|\bar{\theta}| < 10^{-10}$ & Unknown \\

  1051‚Üí\end{tabular}
  1052‚Üí\end{center}
  1053‚Üí
  1054‚ÜíThe sBootstrap adds: superspace chirality (boson vs.\ fermion 
  1055‚Üídistinction within a multiplet) is broken by the same condensate 
  1056‚Üíthat breaks flavor chirality, with $f_{\text{SUSY}} = f_\pi$. The 
  1057‚Üíenergy-balance condition $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ may be 
  1058‚Üírelated: the breaking is ``as large as the background 
  1059‚Üíallows''---maximal, not perturbative.

  1062‚Üí\section{Open Problems}
  1063‚Üí\label{sec:open}
  1064‚Üí%======================================================================
  1065‚Üí
  1066‚Üí\subsection{The diquark statistics problem}
  1067‚ÜíThe sBootstrap requires antisymmetric $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$ 
  1068‚Üídiquarks; Pauli statistics for spin-0, $L=0$, color $\bar{3}$ 
  1069‚Üídiquarks requires symmetric flavor. Extra dynamical input---$L=1$ 
  1070‚Üíplus spin-1, instantons, or the $SO(32)$ embedding---is needed.
  1071‚Üí
  1072‚Üí\subsection{What determines the Koide phase?}
  1073‚ÜíThe energy balance constrains but does not uniquely fix $\delta$. 
  1074‚ÜíThe value $\delta_\ell \approx 2/9$ for leptons must come from 
  1075‚Üídynamics. Is it a fixed point?
  1076‚Üí
  1077‚Üí\subsection{Connecting the three tuples}
  1078‚ÜíThe light sector (seed $\to$ bloom, scale $\sim \Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$) 
  1079‚Üíand heavy sector ($c,b,t$, scale $\sim v_{\text{EW}}$) must connect. 
  1080‚ÜíThe charm and bottom quarks appear in both, providing the links.
  1081‚Üí
  1082‚Üí\subsection{The baryon sector}
  1083‚ÜíIn Seiberg's SQCD with $N_f = N_c = 3$, baryons are part of the 
  1084‚Üílow-energy spectrum. Their SUSY partners are ``baryonic fermions'' 
  1085‚Üíwith no known candidates among observed particles.
  1086‚Üí
  1087‚Üí\subsection{The strong CP problem}
  1088‚ÜíThe principle ``Nature abhors chirality'' predicts $\bar{\theta} = 0$ 
  1089‚Üíbut provides no mechanism.
  1090‚Üí
  1091‚Üí\subsection{The kaon--muon puzzle}
  1092‚ÜíThe SUSY splitting $m_K^2 - m_\mu^2 \approx (482\MeV)^2$ is much 
  1093‚Üílarger than $f_K^2 \approx (110\MeV)^2$, showing that the breaking 
  1094‚Üíis strongly flavor-dependent.

  1096‚Üí%======================================================================
  1097‚Üí\section{Conclusions}
  1098‚Üí\label{sec:conclusions}
  1099‚Üí%======================================================================
  1100‚Üí
  1101‚ÜíThree principal results:
  1102‚Üí
  1103‚Üí\paragraph{1.\ The Koide formula is energy equipartition.}
  1104‚Üí$Q = 2/3$ is $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$: perturbation energies 
  1105‚Üímatch the vacuum energy. The signed-charge framework resolves the 
  1106‚Üíelectron mass puzzle (near-cancellation, not fine-tuning) and reveals 
  1107‚Üíthat mesons satisfy Koide with $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ to $0.1\%$.
  1108‚Üí
  1109‚Üí\paragraph{2.\ Seed triples set the stage for SUSY breaking.}
  1110‚ÜíThe quark seed $(0,s,c)$ and meson seed $(0,\pi,D_s)$ share the 
  1111‚Üíuniversal ratio $2 - \sqrt{3}$ and operate at $v_0^2 \sim 
  1112‚Üí230$--$350\MeV$, close to the lepton triple's $v_0^2 = 314\MeV$. 
  1113‚ÜíThe massless member is the necessary condition for F-term 
  1114‚Üí(O'Raifeartaigh) SUSY breaking. Upon breaking, seeds bloom into 
  1115‚Üífull triples $(-s,c,b)$ and $(-\pi,D_s,B)$. The lepton triple 
  1116‚Üíis the ``almost-seed'' that has barely bloomed.
  1117‚Üí
  1118‚Üí\paragraph{3.\ Three tuples require three breaking structures.}
  1119‚ÜíF-term breaking (via the massless seed member) generates the light 
  1120‚Üísector. D-term breaking ($U(1)_{\text{em}}$ FI parameter) provides 
  1121‚Üíelectromagnetic splittings. Volkov--Akulov nonlinear realization 
  1122‚Üísets the overall scale $f_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi$. The heavy triple 
  1123‚Üí$(c,b,t)$ stands apart: no seed, no meson shadow, scale far above 
  1124‚Üí$\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$, possibly connected to electroweak breaking.
  1125‚Üí
  1126‚ÜíThe diquark sector, required to complete the SUSY multiplet count, 
  1127‚Üímust fill the antisymmetric $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$ of $SU(5)_f$ 
  1128‚Üídespite the apparent conflict with Fermi statistics---a problem 
  1129‚Üíthat may ultimately require the $SO(32)$ embedding for its 
  1130‚Üíresolution.
  1131‚Üí
  1132‚ÜíThe organizing principle is that each sector of the spectrum begins 
  1133‚Üíwith a massless member (a seed), which is the necessary condition 
  1134‚Üífor spontaneous SUSY breaking. The breaking lifts the zero, 
  1135‚Üígenerates the full triple, and the energy-balance condition 
  1136‚Üí$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ is preserved as a fixed point 
  1137‚Üíof the dynamics. The entire charged fermion and pseudoscalar meson 
  1138‚Üíspectrum is then determined by a small number of parameters: the 
  1139‚Üívacuum charges $v_0$ of the three tuples, the Koide phases
