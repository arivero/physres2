    29‚Üí\begin{abstract}
    30‚ÜíWe reformulate the sBootstrap in terms of signed mass charges 
    31‚Üí$\zch = z_0 + z_i$ with $m = \zch^2$, and show that the Koide ratio 
    32‚Üí$Q = 2/3$ is the algebraic consequence of two conditions: 
    33‚Üí$\sum z_i = 0$ and $\langle z_i^2\rangle = z_0^2$ (perturbation 
    34‚Üíenergies average to the vacuum energy). Allowing negative charges, 
    35‚Üíwe find that pseudoscalar mesons satisfy a Koide triple 
    36‚Üí$(-\pi, D_s, B)$ to 0.1\%, and identify \emph{seed triples} with 
    37‚Üíone massless member---$(0, s, c)$ for quarks, $(0, \pi, D_s)$ for 
    38‚Üímesons---as the pre-breaking configurations from which the full 
    39‚Üíspectrum emerges. These seed triples share the same analytical 
    40‚Üístructure (charge ratio $2-\sqrt{3}$) and, strikingly, the meson 
    41‚Üíseed $(0,\pi,D_s)$ has vacuum energy $v_0^2 \approx 351\MeV$, 
    42‚Üíclose to the lepton triple's $v_0^2 \approx 314\MeV$. A third 
    43‚Üítuple $(c,b,t)$ stands apart, having no meson counterpart because 
    44‚Üítop diquarks do not hadronize. We discuss the diquark sector---which 
    45‚Üímust fill the antisymmetric representation of $SU(5)$ flavor rather 
    46‚Üíthan the symmetric, requiring additional dynamical input---and outline 
    47‚Üíhow the three types of SUSY breaking (F-term, D-term, nonlinear 
    48‚ÜíVolkov--Akulov) may map onto the three-tuple structure.
    49‚Üí\end{abstract}

    53‚Üí%======================================================================
    54‚Üí\section{Introduction}
    55‚Üí\label{sec:intro}
    56‚Üí%======================================================================
    57‚Üí
    58‚ÜíThe sBootstrap \cite{Rivero2005,Rivero2012} proposes that the Standard 
    59‚ÜíModel's pseudoscalar mesons and leptons fill common $\mathcal{N}=1$ 
    60‚Üísupermultiplets. Its foundational relation is
    61‚Üí\begin{equation}
    62‚Üí\label{eq:pimu}
    63‚Üím_\pi^2 - m_\mu^2 \approx f_\pi^2\,,
    64‚Üí\end{equation}
    65‚Üíidentifying the pion decay constant $f_\pi \approx 92.2\MeV$ as the 
    66‚ÜíSUSY-breaking order parameter. In this paper we develop two themes 
    67‚Üíthat change the structure of the program.
    68‚Üí
    69‚ÜíFirst, \textbf{mass is the square of a signed charge}: $m = \zch^2$ 
    70‚Üíwhere $\zch = z_0 + z_i$ can be negative. The Koide ratio $Q = 2/3$ 
    71‚Üíis then not numerology but the energy-balance condition 
    72‚Üí$\langle z_i^2\rangle = z_0^2$. With signed charges, pseudoscalar 
    73‚Üímesons satisfy Koide, and the spectrum organizes into \emph{seed 
    74‚Üítriples} with a massless member that grow into full triples upon 
    75‚Üísymmetry breaking.
    76‚Üí
    77‚ÜíSecond, \textbf{the full sBootstrap has three Koide tuples, not one}, 
    78‚Üíand the breaking structure must generate all three: a light sector 
    79‚Üí$(0,s,c) \to (-s,c,b)$ seen in both quarks and mesons, a lepton 
    80‚Üísector $(e\!\approx\!0, \mu, \tau)$, and a heavy sector $(c,b,t)$ 
    81‚Üíthat decouples from meson physics because the top quark does not 
    82‚Üíhadronize. The diquark partners of quarks, needed to complete the 
    83‚ÜíSUSY multiplets, must fill the antisymmetric $\overline{10}$ of 
    84‚Üí$SU(5)$ flavor rather than the symmetric 15, requiring extra 
    85‚Üídynamical input from the breaking sector.
    86‚Üí
    87‚Üí%======================================================================
    88‚Üí\section{The Koide Formula as Energy Balance}
    89‚Üí\label{sec:koide_algebra}
    90‚Üí%======================================================================
    91‚Üí
    92‚Üí\subsection{Charges, not masses}
    93‚Üí
    94‚ÜíThe fundamental variable is the signed mass charge $\zch = z_0 + z_i 
    95‚Üí\in \mathbb{R}$, with the physical mass $m = \zch^2 \geq 0$.
    96‚Üí
    97‚Üí\subsection{Derivation of $Q = 2/3$}
    98‚Üí
    99‚ÜíFor three charges $\zch_k = z_0 + z_k$ with $z_0$ the mean 
   100‚Üí($\sum z_k = 0$):
   101‚Üí\begin{equation}
   102‚ÜíQ = \frac{\sum \zch_k^2}{(\sum \zch_k)^2} 
   103‚Üí= \frac{3z_0^2 + \sum z_k^2}{9z_0^2}
   104‚Üí= \frac{1}{3} + \frac{\langle z_k^2\rangle}{3z_0^2}\,.
   105‚Üí\end{equation}
   106‚ÜíTherefore:
   107‚Üí\begin{equation}
   108‚Üí\boxed{Q = \frac{2}{3} \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad 
   109‚Üí\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2\,.}
   110‚Üí\end{equation}
   111‚Üí
   112‚ÜíThe two conditions are:
   113‚Üí\begin{enumerate}
   114‚Üí\item \textbf{Zero-sum perturbations:} $z_1 + z_2 + z_3 = 0$ 
   115‚Üí(definition of $z_0$ as the mean charge).
   116‚Üí\item \textbf{Energy balance:} The mean energy of the perturbations 
   117‚Üíequals the energy of the unperturbed charge: 
   118‚Üí$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$.
   119‚Üí\end{enumerate}
   120‚Üí
   121‚ÜíCondition~2 is the physics. It says the splitting scale matches the 
   122‚Üíbase scale---the coefficient of variation equals unity: 
   123‚Üí$\sigma(\zch)/\bar{\zch} = 1$. This is neither perturbative 
   124‚Üí($\sigma \ll \bar{\zch}$, giving $Q \to 1/3$, all equal) nor 
   125‚Üídominant ($\sigma \gg \bar{\zch}$, giving $Q \to 1$, one mass 
   126‚Üídominates). The Koide value $2/3$ is the equipartition point.
   127‚Üí
   128‚Üí\subsection{Why one mass can vanish}
   129‚Üí
   130‚ÜíIf $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$, a perturbation $z_k$ can reach 
   131‚Üí$|z_k| \sim z_0$, making $\zch_k = z_0 + z_k \approx 0$. A vanishing 
   132‚Üímass is the \emph{natural extreme} of energy balance, not fine-tuning.
   133‚Üí
   134‚Üí%======================================================================
   135‚Üí\section{Seed Triples: The $(0, a, b)$ Structure}
   136‚Üí\label{sec:seeds}
   137‚Üí%======================================================================
   138‚Üí
   139‚Üí\subsection{Analytical condition}
   140‚Üí
   141‚ÜíFor a triple $(0, \zch_a, \zch_b)$ with one massless member, the 
   142‚ÜíKoide ratio becomes:
   143‚Üí\begin{equation}
   144‚ÜíQ = \frac{\zch_a^2 + \zch_b^2}{(\zch_a + \zch_b)^2}\,.
   145‚Üí\end{equation}
   146‚ÜíSetting $Q = 2/3$ and writing $r = \zch_a/\zch_b$ (with $r < 1$):
   147‚Üí\begin{equation}
   148‚Üí3(r^2 + 1) = 2(r + 1)^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad r^2 - 4r + 1 = 0\,,
   149‚Üí\end{equation}
   150‚Üígiving:
   151‚Üí\begin{equation}
   152‚Üí\boxed{r = 2 - \sqrt{3} \approx 0.26795\,.}
   153‚Üí\end{equation}
   154‚Üí
   155‚ÜíEquivalently, in mass space: 
   156‚Üí$m_{\text{light}}/m_{\text{heavy}} = (2 - \sqrt{3})^2 = 7 - 4\sqrt{3} 
   157‚Üí\approx 0.07180$.
   158‚Üí
   159‚ÜíThis is a \emph{universal} prediction: any Koide triple with one 
   160‚Üímassless member has the same charge ratio between the two massive 
   161‚Üímembers, regardless of their absolute scale.
   162‚Üí
   163‚Üí\subsection{The quark seed: $(0, s, c)$}
   164‚Üí
   165‚ÜíThe strange and charm quark charges give:
   166‚Üí\begin{equation}
   167‚Üí\frac{\sqrt{m_s}}{\sqrt{m_c}} = \frac{9.67}{35.64} = 0.2712\,, 
   168‚Üí\qquad r_{\text{Koide}} = 0.2679\,, \qquad \text{deviation: } 1.2\%\,.
   169‚Üí\end{equation}
   170‚Üí
   171‚ÜíThis triple has $Q = 0.6644$, deviating from $2/3$ by only $0.35\%$. 
   172‚ÜíIts vacuum energy is $v_0^2 = (\zch_s + \zch_c)^2/9 \approx 228\MeV$.
   173‚Üí
   174‚ÜíA historical note: Harari and a collaborator observed that $(0, d, s)$ 
   175‚Üíwas approximately a Koide triple, at a time when the up quark mass was 
   176‚Üístill thought to be possibly zero \cite{Harari}. With current masses, 
   177‚Üí$\sqrt{m_d}/\sqrt{m_s} = 0.224$, deviating from $2 - \sqrt{3}$ by 
   178‚Üí$16\%$---too large for a good Koide triple. But if $m_s$ were 
   179‚Üí$\sim 150\MeV$ and $m_d \sim 7\MeV$ (values common in that era), the 
   180‚Üíratio improves considerably. The seed idea was already present, ahead 
   181‚Üíof its time.
   182‚Üí
   183‚Üí\subsection{The meson seed: $(0, \pi, D_s)$}
   184‚Üí
   185‚ÜíThe pion and $D_s$ meson charges give:
   186‚Üí\begin{equation}
   187‚Üí\frac{\sqrt{m_\pi}}{\sqrt{m_{D_s}}} = \frac{11.81}{44.37} = 0.2663\,, 
   188‚Üí\qquad r_{\text{Koide}} = 0.2679\,, \qquad \text{deviation: } 0.6\%\,.
   189‚Üí\end{equation}
   190‚Üí
   191‚ÜíThis gives $Q = 0.6679$, deviating by $0.18\%$. It is the 
   192‚Üí\textbf{meson counterpart} of the quark seed $(0, s, c)$---both 
   193‚Üíinvolve the strange-charm sector. Indeed, $D_s = c\bar{s}$ is the 
   194‚Üímeson that directly contains the $s$ and $c$ quarks.
   195‚Üí
   196‚Üí\subsection{The lepton triple as an ``almost-seed''}
   197‚Üí
   198‚ÜíThe charged lepton triple $(e, \mu, \tau)$ has $\zch_e = 0.715\MeV^{1/2}$, 
   199‚Üíwhich is small but nonzero. If $m_e$ were zero, we would need 
   200‚Üí$\sqrt{m_\mu}/\sqrt{m_\tau} = 2 - \sqrt{3} = 0.2679$; the actual value 
   201‚Üíis $0.2439$, deviating by $9\%$. So the leptons are \emph{not} an 
   202‚Üíexact seed---the electron, while very light, is not massless. But the 
   203‚Üítriple is clearly the same family: the near-critical Koide phase puts 
   204‚Üíone member close to zero.
   205‚Üí
   206‚Üí\subsection{Vacuum energy coincidence}
   207‚Üí
   208‚ÜíThe $v_0^2$ values of the seed triples cluster remarkably:
   209‚Üí
   210‚Üí\begin{center}
   211‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lcc}
   212‚Üí\toprule
   213‚ÜíTriple & $v_0^2$ (MeV) & $v_0$ ($\MeV^{1/2}$) \\
   214‚Üí\midrule
   215‚Üí$(e, \mu, \tau)$ & 314 & 17.72 \\
   216‚Üí$(0, \pi, D_s)$ & 351 & 18.73 \\
   217‚Üí$(0, s, c)$ & 228 & 15.10 \\
   218‚Üí\bottomrule
   219‚Üí\end{tabular}
   220‚Üí\end{center}
   221‚Üí
   222‚ÜíThe lepton and meson seed triples share a vacuum energy around 
   223‚Üí310--350~MeV, squarely in the QCD scale range. This is the 
   224‚ÜísBootstrap connection made quantitative: \textbf{the lepton triple 
   225‚Üíand the meson seed triple operate at the same scale.}
   226‚Üí
   227‚Üí%======================================================================
   228‚Üí\section{A Tale of Two Tuples}
   229‚Üí\label{sec:two_tuples}
   230‚Üí%======================================================================
   231‚Üí
   232‚Üí\subsection{From seed to full triple}
   233‚Üí
   234‚ÜíThe seed triples grow into full triples upon symmetry breaking:
   235‚Üí
   236‚Üí\begin{center}
   237‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lccc}
   238‚Üí\toprule
   239‚ÜíSector & Seed triple & Full triple & $v_0^2$ (full) \\
   240‚Üí\midrule
   241‚ÜíQuarks & $(0, s, c)$ & $(-s, c, b)$ & 913 MeV \\
   242‚ÜíMesons & $(0, \pi, D_s)$ & $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ & 1230 MeV \\
   243‚ÜíLeptons & $(0 \approx e, \mu, \tau)$ & $(e, \mu, \tau)$ & 314 MeV \\
   244‚Üí\bottomrule
   245‚Üí\end{tabular}
   246‚Üí\end{center}
   247‚Üí
   248‚ÜíIn each case:
   249‚Üí\begin{itemize}
   250‚Üí\item The seed has a massless (or near-massless) member, two massive 
   251‚Üímembers in the ratio $2 - \sqrt{3}$, and $Q \approx 2/3$.
   252‚Üí\item The full triple has \textbf{no} massless member: the zero has 
   253‚Üíbeen lifted (to $m_e$, or the sign-flipped strange/pion mass), and a 
   254‚Üínew heavy member ($b$ quark, $B$ meson) has appeared. The full triple 
   255‚Üíalso satisfies $Q \approx 2/3$ but at a larger scale $v_0$.
   256‚Üí\end{itemize}
   257‚Üí
   258‚ÜíThe ``tale of two tuples'' is this: \textbf{each sector presents 
   259‚Üífirst as a seed with a massless member, then ``blooms'' into a 
   260‚Üífull triple when SUSY breaks.} The massless member is the necessary 
   261‚Üíinitial condition; the breaking lifts it and generates the full spectrum.
   262‚Üí
   263‚Üí\subsection{Precision summary}
   264‚Üí
   265‚Üí\begin{center}
   266‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lccc}
   267‚Üí\toprule
   268‚ÜíTriple & $Q$ & Dev.\ from $2/3$ & $v_0^2$ (MeV) \\
   269‚Üí\midrule
   270‚Üí\multicolumn{4}{c}{\textit{Seed triples}} \\
   271‚Üí$(0, \pi, D_s)$ & 0.6679 & 0.18\% & 351 \\
   272‚Üí$(0, s, c)$ & 0.6644 & 0.35\% & 228 \\
   273‚Üí$(0 \approx e, \mu, \tau)$ & 0.6667 & 0.001\% & 314 \\
   274‚Üí\midrule
   275‚Üí\multicolumn{4}{c}{\textit{Full triples}} \\
   276‚Üí$(e, \mu, \tau)$ & 0.66666 & 0.001\% & 314 \\
   277‚Üí$(-\pi, D_s, B)$ & 0.6674 & 0.10\% & 1230 \\
   278‚Üí$(-s, c, b)$ & 0.6750 & 1.24\% & 913 \\
   279‚Üí$(c, b, t)$ & 0.6695 & 0.42\% & 22\,770 \\
   280‚Üí\bottomrule
   281‚Üí\end{tabular}
   282‚Üí\end{center}
   283‚Üí
   284‚ÜíThe lepton triple is unique: seed and full triple are nearly 
   285‚Üíidentical, because the electron mass is small enough to serve as 
   286‚Üíthe ``zero'' while being nonzero enough to make $Q$ extremely 
   287‚Üíprecise.
   288‚Üí
   289‚Üí\subsection{Orthogonality of seed and full triples}
   290‚Üí
   291‚ÜíEach Koide triple defines a perturbation vector 
   292‚Üí$\vec{z} = (z_1, z_2, z_3)$ constrained to the plane $\sum z_k = 0$ 
   293‚Üíwith length $|\vec{z}|^2 = 3z_0^2$. The angle between seed and 
   294‚Üífull triples measures how much the breaking rotates the spectrum 
   295‚Üíin charge space:
   296‚Üí
   297‚Üí\begin{center}
   298‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lc}
   299‚Üí\toprule
   300‚ÜíPair of triples & Angle \\
   301‚Üí\midrule
   302‚Üí$(0,s,c)$ vs.\ $(-s,c,b)$ & $22^\circ$ \\
   303‚Üí$(0,\pi,D_s)$ vs.\ $(-\pi,D_s,B)$ & $26^\circ$ \\
   304‚Üí$(e,\mu,\tau)$ vs.\ $(0,\mu,\tau)$ & $0.8^\circ$ \\
   305‚Üí$(0,s,c)$ vs.\ $(0,\pi,D_s)$ & $0.3^\circ$ \\
   306‚Üí\bottomrule
   307‚Üí\end{tabular}
   308‚Üí\end{center}
   309‚Üí
   310‚ÜíTwo results are notable. First, the quark seed $(0,s,c)$ and meson 
   311‚Üíseed $(0,\pi,D_s)$ are \textbf{nearly parallel} ($0.3^\circ$): the 
   312‚Üímeson seed is the composite image of the quark seed, with the GOR 
   313‚Üímap preserving direction while changing scale. Second, the lepton 
   314‚Üítriple is essentially its own seed ($0.8^\circ$)---the bloom has 
   315‚Üíbarely begun.
   316‚Üí
   317‚Üí%======================================================================
   318‚Üí\section{The Third Tuple: $(c, b, t)$}
   319‚Üí\label{sec:cbt}
   320‚Üí%======================================================================
   321‚Üí
   322‚Üí\subsection{A tuple without mesons}
   323‚Üí
   324‚ÜíThe triple $(c, b, t)$ with all positive signs gives $Q = 0.6695$, 
   325‚Üídeviating by $0.42\%$. Its vacuum energy $v_0^2 \approx 22.8\GeV$ 
   326‚Üíis far above the QCD scale.
   327‚Üí
   328‚ÜíThis triple has \textbf{no meson counterpart}: the top quark decays 
   329‚Üíweakly ($t \to Wb$, lifetime $\sim 5 \times 10^{-25}$~s) before the 
   330‚Üístrong interaction can bind it ($\tau_{\text{QCD}} \sim 1/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}} 
   331‚Üí\sim 10^{-24}$~s). No $T$-meson ($t\bar{q}$) exists. The meson matrix 
   332‚Üí$M^i{}_j$ extends only to $b$-flavored states; the top sector lives 
   333‚Üípurely at the quark level.
   334‚Üí
   335‚Üí\subsection{Diquarks and the top}
   336‚Üí
   337‚ÜíIn the sBootstrap, diquarks $[q_i q_j]$ are the scalar partners of 
   338‚Üíantiquarks $\bar{q}_k$, with the baryon acting as the SUSY generator 
   339‚Üí\cite{CattoGursey, Miyazawa}. For five active flavors 
   340‚Üí$(u, d, s, c, b)$, the diquarks form a representation of $SU(5)_f$.
   341‚Üí
   342‚ÜíThe top diquarks $[tq]$ are kinematic objects only---they do not 
   343‚Üíhadronize. Yet their masses should follow the same pattern as other 
   344‚Üídiquarks: \textbf{there is no dynamical reason for top diquark masses 
   345‚Üíto differ from the others}. The $(c,b,t)$ Koide triple governs the 
   346‚Üíquark masses that enter the diquark sector, and its energy balance 
   347‚Üí$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ constrains the possible diquark 
   348‚Üíspectrum even though the top diquarks are never observed as bound 
   349‚Üístates.
   350‚Üí
   351‚Üí\subsection{The $(c,b,t)$ triple has no seed}
   352‚Üí
   353‚ÜíUnlike the $(0,s,c)$ and $(0,\pi,D_s)$ seeds, there is no obvious 
   354‚Üí$(0, x, y)$ seed for the $(c,b,t)$ triple. No pair of quarks in this 
   355‚Üísector satisfies $\sqrt{m_{\text{light}}}/\sqrt{m_{\text{heavy}}} 
   356‚Üí\approx 2 - \sqrt{3}$: one has $\sqrt{m_c}/\sqrt{m_t} = 0.086$ 
   357‚Üí(too small) and $\sqrt{m_c}/\sqrt{m_b} = 0.551$ (too large).
   358‚Üí
   359‚ÜíThis means $(c,b,t)$ is \emph{not} a ``bloomed seed'' in the same 
   360‚Üísense as $(-s,c,b)$. It may represent a fundamentally different 
   361‚Üístructure---a triple that was never seeded by a massless member but 
   362‚Üíinstead emerged fully formed at a higher breaking scale, perhaps 
   363‚Üíconnected to electroweak symmetry breaking rather than to chiral 
   364‚Üísymmetry breaking.
   365‚Üí
   366‚Üí%======================================================================
   367‚Üí\section{The Diquark Sector and SU(5) Flavor}
   368‚Üí\label{sec:diquarks}
   369‚Üí%======================================================================
   370‚Üí
   371‚Üí\subsection{Counting partners}
   372‚Üí
   373‚ÜíIn the full sBootstrap \cite{Rivero2005}, the scalar partners of 
   374‚Üíthe Standard Model fermions include both mesons ($q\bar{q}$) and 
   375‚Üídiquarks ($qq$). For five active flavors, the meson matrix gives 
   376‚Üí$5 \times 5 = 25$ scalars. The diquarks $[q_i q_j]$ form a 
   377‚Üírepresentation of $SU(5)_f$:
   378‚Üí\begin{equation}
   379‚Üí\mathbf{5} \otimes \mathbf{5} = \overline{\mathbf{10}}_A 
   380‚Üí\oplus \mathbf{15}_S\,.
   381‚Üí\end{equation}
   382‚Üí
   383‚ÜíThe symmetric $\mathbf{15}_S$ gives 15 diquarks including 
   384‚Üísame-flavor states $[uu]$, $[dd]$, \ldots, while the antisymmetric 
   385‚Üí$\overline{\mathbf{10}}_A$ gives 10 diquarks with $i \neq j$ only.
   386‚Üí
   387‚Üí\subsection{Why antisymmetric?}
   388‚Üí
   389‚ÜíFor the sBootstrap to produce the exact count of scalar partners 
   390‚Üímatching the Standard Model fermion content, the diquarks must 
   391‚Üítransform in the \textbf{antisymmetric} $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$ 
   392‚Üí\cite{Rivero2005}. The argument:
   393‚Üí
   394‚ÜíThe 25 mesons account for the slepton and ``internal'' squark 
   395‚Üísectors. The remaining squarks must come from diquarks. The 
   396‚Üí$\overline{\mathbf{10}}$ provides exactly 10 diquarks: $[ud]$, 
   397‚Üí$[us]$, $[uc]$, $[ub]$, $[ds]$, $[dc]$, $[db]$, $[sc]$, $[sb]$, 
   398‚Üí$[cb]$. With their antiparticles, this gives 20 scalar states. The 
   399‚Üítotal $25 + 10 = 35$ (or $25 + 20 = 45$ counting antiparticles 
   400‚Üíseparately) matches the scalar content of the Supersymmetric 
   401‚ÜíStandard Model.
   402‚Üí
   403‚ÜíThe symmetric $\mathbf{15}$ would give 15 diquarks, overcounting 
   404‚Üíby 5 (the same-flavor states). Moreover, the exotic $+4/3$ charged 
   405‚Üídiquarks $[uu]$, $[uc]$, $[cc]$ have no lepton partner among known 
   406‚Üíparticles---a fatal obstacle.
   407‚Üí
   408‚Üí\subsection{The Pauli problem}
   409‚Üí
   410‚ÜíThe antisymmetric flavor requirement conflicts with a naive 
   411‚Üíapplication of Fermi statistics. For spin-0, $L=0$ diquarks in 
   412‚Üíthe color $\bar{3}$ (antisymmetric) channel:
   413‚Üí
   414‚Üí\begin{center}
   415‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lcl}
   416‚Üí\toprule
   417‚ÜíProperty & Symmetry & Wavefunction \\
   418‚Üí\midrule
   419‚ÜíColor $\bar{3}$ & Antisymmetric & $\epsilon_{abc} q^b q^c$ \\
   420‚ÜíSpin 0 & Antisymmetric & $(\uparrow\downarrow - \downarrow\uparrow)/\sqrt{2}$ \\
   421‚ÜíSpatial $L=0$ & Symmetric & $\psi(r)$ \\
   422‚Üí\midrule
   423‚ÜíOverall & Must be A & $A \times A \times S = S$ \quad$(\neq A!)$ \\
   424‚Üí\bottomrule
   425‚Üí\end{tabular}
   426‚Üí\end{center}
   427‚Üí
   428‚ÜíThe product is symmetric---\textbf{flavor must also be symmetric} 
   429‚Üíto make the overall wavefunction antisymmetric. But the sBootstrap 
   430‚Üírequires antisymmetric flavor.
   431‚Üí
   432‚ÜíThis is one of the deepest puzzles of the program. Possible 
   433‚Üíresolutions:
   434‚Üí\begin{itemize}
   435‚Üí\item \textbf{$L = 1$ diquarks:} Adding orbital angular momentum 
   436‚Üíintroduces spatial antisymmetry. Then color(A) $\times$ spin(A) 
   437‚Üí$\times$ space(A) $\times$ flavor = overall A requires 
   438‚Üíflavor(S)---still wrong. But $L=1$ with spin-1 gives spin(S), 
   439‚Üíand then color(A) $\times$ spin(S) $\times$ space(A) $\times$ 
   440‚Üíflavor(?) = A requires flavor(A). This works but requires both 
   441‚Üí$L=1$ and spin-1 diquarks, departing from the simplest scalar 
   442‚Üídiquark picture.
   443‚Üí\item \textbf{Dynamical projection:} A binding mechanism that 
   444‚Üíeffectively projects onto the flavor-antisymmetric channel, perhaps 
   445‚Üíthrough instanton effects or a condensate in the breaking sector.
   446‚Üí\item \textbf{The SO(32) route:} In the string-theoretic embedding 
   447‚Üí\cite{Rivero2005}, the $SO(32)$ spinor representation automatically 
   448‚Üíproduces the correct antisymmetric structure. The Pauli constraint 
   449‚Üíis an artifact of treating diquarks as simple two-quark states; in 
   450‚Üíthe string picture, they are fundamental objects of the open-string 
   451‚Üísector and the symmetry is determined by the gauge group, not by 
   452‚Üíquark statistics.
   453‚Üí\end{itemize}
   454‚Üí
   455‚Üí\subsection{The SO(32) connection}
   456‚Üí
   457‚ÜíThe sBootstrap counting matches a decomposition of the $SO(32)$ gauge 
   458‚Üígroup familiar from Type~I/heterotic string theory. Under 
   459‚Üí$SO(32) \supset SU(5) \times \ldots$, the adjoint or spinor 
   460‚Üírepresentations produce exactly the right combination of mesons 
   461‚Üí(in $\mathbf{5} \otimes \bar{\mathbf{5}}$) and antisymmetric 
   462‚Üídiquarks (in $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$). The 496-dimensional adjoint 
   463‚Üíof $SO(32)$ decomposes to include the 24 of $SU(5)$ (gauge bosons), 
   464‚Üíthe $\mathbf{5} \otimes \bar{\mathbf{5}} = \mathbf{1} + \mathbf{24}$ 
   465‚Üí(mesons/sleptons), and the $\overline{\mathbf{10}} + \mathbf{10}$ 
   466‚Üí(diquarks/squarks).
   467‚Üí
   468‚ÜíThis is suggestive: the same structure that gives anomaly-free string 
   469‚Üícompactifications also gives the correct sBootstrap particle count 
   470‚Üíwith the required antisymmetric diquarks. Whether this is a 
   471‚Üícoincidence or a hint of UV completion is an open question.

   473‚Üí%======================================================================
   474‚Üí\section{The Meson Koide Triple: $(-\pi, D_s, B)$}
   475‚Üí\label{sec:mesonkoide}
   476‚Üí%======================================================================
   477‚Üí
   478‚ÜíWith the pion charge negative, the triple $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ satisfies:
   479‚Üí\begin{equation}
   480‚ÜíQ(-\pi, D_s, B) = 
   481‚Üí\frac{m_\pi + m_{D_s} + m_B}{(-\sqrt{m_\pi} + \sqrt{m_{D_s}} + \sqrt{m_B})^2}
   482‚Üí= 0.6674\,,
   483‚Üí\end{equation}
   484‚Üídeviating from $2/3$ by $0.10\%$---the second most precise Koide 
   485‚Üítriple known. The energy balance:
   486‚Üí\begin{equation}
   487‚Üí\frac{\langle z_k^2\rangle}{z_0^2} = 1.002\,.
   488‚Üí\end{equation}
   489‚Üí
   490‚ÜíFor comparison, the ``standard'' all-positive quark triples $(d,s,b)$ 
   491‚Üíand $(u,c,t)$ deviate from $Q = 2/3$ by 9.7\% and 27\% respectively 
   492‚Üíat $\overline{\text{MS}}$ masses. \textbf{The meson triple is more 
   493‚Üíprecise than any quark triple} except $(c,b,t)$.
   494‚Üí
   495‚ÜíThe pion's negative charge has physical meaning: as a pseudo-Goldstone 
   496‚Üíboson, the pion would be massless ($\zch = 0$) if chiral symmetry were 
   497‚Üíexact. It sits just below zero ($\zch_\pi = -11.81$), approaching zero 
   498‚Üífrom the negative side as $m_q \to 0$. The sign of $\zch$ encodes 
   499‚ÜíGoldstone nature: pseudo-Goldstone bosons sit on the negative side 
   500‚Üíof the critical point, having crossed through zero from positive 
   501‚Üíterritory during chiral symmetry breaking.

   503‚Üí%======================================================================
   504‚Üí\section{SUSY Breaking: From Seeds to Spectrum}
   505‚Üí\label{sec:breaking}
   506‚Üí%======================================================================
   507‚Üí
   508‚ÜíThe SUSY-breaking dynamics must accomplish three things simultaneously:
   509‚Üígenerate the seed-to-bloom transition for each tuple, preserve the 
   510‚Üíenergy-balance condition $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ through the 
   511‚Üíbreaking, and produce the correct splitting between meson and lepton 
   512‚Üímasses at scale $f_\pi$. In this section we develop the framework in 
   513‚Üídetail, connecting the three standard SUSY-breaking mechanisms to the 
   514‚Üíthree-tuple structure.
   515‚Üí
   516‚Üí\subsection{The Seiberg framework}
   517‚Üí
   518‚ÜíThe natural arena is $\mathcal{N}=1$ SQCD with gauge group $SU(3)_c$ 
   519‚Üíand $N_f$ flavors of chiral superfields $Q^i$, $\bar{Q}_j$ in the 
   520‚Üífundamental and antifundamental representations. For $N_f = N_c = 3$ 
   521‚Üí(the case relevant to light QCD), Seiberg's exact results 
   522‚Üí\cite{Seiberg1995} establish that the theory confines, with the 
   523‚Üílow-energy degrees of freedom being the composite meson superfield
   524‚Üí\begin{equation}
   525‚ÜíM^i{}_j = Q^i \cdot \bar{Q}_j\,,
   526‚Üí\end{equation}
   527‚Üía $3\times 3$ matrix of chiral superfields, together with baryons 
   528‚Üí$B = \epsilon_{ijk}Q^iQ^jQ^k$ and $\bar{B}$. These satisfy the 
   529‚Üíquantum-modified constraint
   530‚Üí\begin{equation}
   531‚Üí\label{eq:seiberg}
   532‚Üí\det M - B\bar{B} = \Lambda^{2N_c}\,,
   533‚Üí\end{equation}
   534‚Üíwhere $\Lambda$ is the dynamical scale.
   535‚Üí
   536‚ÜíEach entry $M^i{}_j$ is a chiral superfield containing a complex 
   537‚Üíscalar (the meson) and a Weyl fermion (the lepton, in the sBootstrap 
   538‚Üíidentification). The scalar and fermion are degenerate in the SUSY 
   539‚Üílimit; SUSY breaking splits them.
   540‚Üí
   541‚Üí\subsection{The three chiral superfields of each seed}
   542‚Üí
   543‚ÜíThe seed triple $(0, \zch_a, \zch_b)$ maps naturally onto three 
   544‚Üíchiral superfields with the following mass structure before breaking:
   545‚Üí
   546‚Üí\begin{center}
   547‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lcl}
   548‚Üí\toprule
   549‚ÜíSuperfield & Mass & Role \\
   550‚Üí\midrule
   551‚Üí$\Phi_0$ & $m_0 = 0$ & Massless: flat direction \\
   552‚Üí$\Phi_a$ & $m_a = \zch_a^2$ & Light massive member \\
   553‚Üí$\Phi_b$ & $m_b = \zch_b^2$ & Heavy massive member \\
   554‚Üí\bottomrule
   555‚Üí\end{tabular}
   556‚Üí\end{center}
   557‚Üí
   558‚ÜíThe ratio $\zch_a/\zch_b = 2 - \sqrt{3}$ is enforced by the 
   559‚ÜíKoide energy-balance condition applied to the seed.
   560‚Üí
   561‚ÜíIn the Seiberg description, these three superfields are entries of 
   562‚Üíthe meson matrix $M^i{}_j$. For the $(0, s, c)$ seed, the relevant 
   563‚Üíentries involve the strange and charm quarks; $\Phi_0$ is the entry 
   564‚Üíwhose quark-mass combination vanishes in the seed limit (i.e., the 
   565‚Üídirection in flavor space where $m_i + m_j \to 0$ through GOR).
   566‚Üí
   567‚Üí\subsection{F-term breaking: the O'Raifeartaigh mechanism}
   568‚Üí\label{subsec:fterm}
   569‚Üí
   570‚ÜíThe O'Raifeartaigh model \cite{ORaifeartaigh} is the simplest 
   571‚Üírealization of spontaneous $F$-term SUSY breaking. With three chiral 
   572‚Üísuperfields, the superpotential
   573‚Üí\begin{equation}
   574‚ÜíW = f\,\Phi_0 + m\,\Phi_1\Phi_2 + g\,\Phi_0\Phi_1^2
   575‚Üí\end{equation}
   576‚Üígives $F$-terms:
   577‚Üí\begin{align}
   578‚ÜíF_{\Phi_0}^* &= f + g\,\phi_1^2\,, \\
   579‚ÜíF_{\Phi_1}^* &= m\,\phi_2 + 2g\,\phi_0\phi_1\,, \\
   580‚ÜíF_{\Phi_2}^* &= m\,\phi_1\,.
   581‚Üí\end{align}
   582‚ÜíIf $f \neq 0$ and $m \neq 0$, then $F_{\Phi_2}^* = 0$ requires 
   583‚Üí$\phi_1 = 0$, but then $F_{\Phi_0}^* = f \neq 0$: SUSY is 
   584‚Üí\textbf{necessarily broken}. The key features are:
   585‚Üí\begin{enumerate}
   586‚Üí\item A massless field ($\Phi_0$) exists at tree level---it is the 
   587‚Üípseudo-modulus.
   588‚Üí\item The breaking is triggered because $F_{\Phi_0}$ cannot vanish.
   589‚Üí\item After including loop corrections, $\Phi_0$ acquires a mass 
   590‚Üí(the Coleman--Weinberg potential lifts the flat direction).
   591‚Üí\end{enumerate}
   592‚Üí
   593‚ÜíThe structural parallel with the seed triples is precise:
   594‚Üí
   595‚Üí\begin{center}
   596‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lll}
   597‚Üí\toprule
   598‚ÜíO'Raifeartaigh & Seed triple & After breaking \\
   599‚Üí\midrule
   600‚Üí$\Phi_0$ (massless) & 0 member & Lifted to $\zch < 0$ \\
   601‚Üí$\Phi_1$ (mass $m$) & $\zch_a$ (light) & Shifted \\
   602‚Üí$\Phi_2$ (mass $m$) & $\zch_b$ (heavy) & Shifted \\
   603‚Üí\midrule
   604‚Üí$f = \langle F\rangle$ & --- & $f_\pi$ \\
   605‚Üí\bottomrule
   606‚Üí\end{tabular}
   607‚Üí\end{center}
   608‚Üí
   609‚ÜíThe identification $f = \langle F_{\Phi_0}\rangle \sim f_\pi$ is the 
   610‚ÜísBootstrap's central claim: the SUSY-breaking $F$-term is the chiral 
   611‚Üícondensate parameter.
   612‚Üí
   613‚ÜíIn SQCD, this is realized dynamically rather than through a 
   614‚Üísuperpotential: the chiral condensate $\qqbar \neq 0$ acts as the 
   615‚Üínonzero $F$-term, the impossibility of restoring chiral symmetry 
   616‚Üíin the QCD vacuum is the dynamical analogue of $f \neq 0$, and the 
   617‚Üípion---the would-be Goldstone boson that acquires mass from explicit 
   618‚Üíchiral breaking---plays the role of $\Phi_0$ whose mass is lifted 
   619‚Üíby loop corrections (here, by quark masses).
   620‚Üí
   621‚Üí\subsection{The Goldstino direction and the Volkov--Akulov realization}
   622‚Üí\label{subsec:va}
   623‚Üí
   624‚ÜíWhen SUSY is spontaneously broken, a massless fermion appears: the 
   625‚ÜíGoldstino, the fermionic analogue of the Goldstone boson. Its decay 
   626‚Üíconstant $f_{\text{VA}}$ sets the SUSY-breaking scale through the 
   627‚ÜíVolkov--Akulov Lagrangian \cite{VolkovAkulov1973}:
   628‚Üí\begin{equation}
   629‚Üí\mathcal{L}_{\text{VA}} = -f_{\text{VA}}^2 \det\!\left(
   630‚Üí\delta_\mu^\nu + \frac{i}{f_{\text{VA}}^2}\,\bar{\lambda}\bar{\sigma}^\nu
   631‚Üí\partial_\mu\lambda\right),
   632‚Üí\end{equation}
   633‚Üíwhere $\lambda$ is the Goldstino field. At low energies, the Goldstino 
   634‚Üícouples derivatively with strength $\sim 1/f_{\text{VA}}^2$, exactly 
   635‚Üías the pion couples with strength $\sim 1/f_\pi^2$ in the chiral 
   636‚ÜíLagrangian.
   637‚Üí
   638‚ÜíThe sBootstrap identification is:
   639‚Üí\begin{equation}
   640‚Üí\label{eq:va_id}
   641‚Üíf_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi\,, \qquad 
   642‚Üí\text{Goldstino} = \text{muon (partner of }\pi\text{)}\,.
   643‚Üí\end{equation}
   644‚ÜíMore precisely, the muon is a \emph{pseudo-Goldstino}: in the 
   645‚Üílimit $m_q \to 0$ where the pion becomes exactly massless (a true 
   646‚ÜíGoldstone boson), its fermionic partner also becomes massless (a 
   647‚Üítrue Goldstino). The finite quark masses explicitly break both 
   648‚Üíchiral symmetry (giving $m_\pi \neq 0$) and the sBootstrap SUSY 
   649‚Üí(giving $m_\mu \neq m_\pi$), with the splitting controlled by 
   650‚Üí$f_\pi$:
   651‚Üí\begin{equation}
   652‚Üím_\pi^2 - m_\mu^2 = f_\pi^2\,.
   653‚Üí\end{equation}
   654‚Üí
   655‚ÜíThis is the Volkov--Akulov relation for the pseudo-Goldstino: the 
   656‚Üímass splitting between the scalar (pion) and its fermionic partner 
   657‚Üí(muon) is set by the SUSY-breaking scale squared.
   658‚Üí
   659‚ÜíThe VA mechanism is not a separate breaking from the F-term: it is 
   660‚Üíthe \emph{low-energy, composite-level description} of the same 
   661‚Üíbreaking. At the microscopic level, $\langle F\rangle \neq 0$ 
   662‚Üíin the meson superfield; at the composite level, this manifests 
   663‚Üías the Goldstino coupling with $f_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi$. The two 
   664‚Üídescriptions are related by:
   665‚Üí\begin{equation}
   666‚Üíf_{\text{VA}}^2 = \langle F\rangle = \frac{|\qqbar|}{f_\pi}\,,
   667‚Üí\end{equation}
   668‚Üíconsistent with the GOR relation $m_\pi^2 f_\pi^2 = 
   669‚Üí-(m_u + m_d)\qqbar$.
   670‚Üí
   671‚Üí\subsection{D-term breaking: electromagnetic splittings}
   672‚Üí\label{subsec:dterm}
   673‚Üí
   674‚ÜíThe unbroken $U(1)_{\text{em}}$ gauge symmetry allows a 
   675‚ÜíFayet--Iliopoulos (FI) term \cite{FayetIliopoulos}:
   676‚Üí\begin{equation}
   677‚Üí\mathcal{L}_{\text{FI}} = \xi\,D_{\text{em}}\,,
   678‚Üí\end{equation}
   679‚Üíwhich shifts scalar masses by $\delta m^2 = Q_{\text{em}}\,\xi$, 
   680‚Üíwhere $Q_{\text{em}}$ is the electric charge. This is the 
   681‚Üísupersymmetric version of the electromagnetic mass difference.
   682‚Üí
   683‚ÜíIn QCD without SUSY, the electromagnetic mass splitting of the 
   684‚Üípion is given by the Das--Guralnik--Mathur--Low--Young (DGMLY) 
   685‚Üíformula \cite{DGMLY1967}:
   686‚Üí\begin{equation}
   687‚Üím_{\pi^\pm}^2 - m_{\pi^0}^2 = \frac{3\alpha_{\text{em}}}{4\pi}\,
   688‚Üí\frac{m_\rho^2}{f_\pi^2}\,f_\pi^2 
   689‚Üí\approx (35.5\MeV)^2\,,
   690‚Üí\end{equation}
   691‚Üíwhich in the SUSY framework becomes a D-term contribution:
   692‚Üí\begin{equation}
   693‚Üí\xi \sim \frac{3\alpha_{\text{em}}}{4\pi}\,m_\rho^2 
   694‚Üí\sim (35\MeV)^2\,.
   695‚Üí\end{equation}
   696‚Üí
   697‚ÜíIn charge space, the D-term shifts $\zch$ by an amount proportional 
   698‚Üíto $Q_{\text{em}}$:
   699‚Üí\begin{equation}
   700‚Üí\zch \to \zch + \delta\zch\,, \qquad 
   701‚Üí\delta\zch \approx \frac{Q_{\text{em}}\,\xi}{2\zch}\,.
   702‚Üí\end{equation}
   703‚ÜíFor the pion ($Q_{\text{em}} = \pm 1$, $\zch \approx 11.8$): 
   704‚Üí$\delta\zch \approx 0.2\MeV^{1/2}$, matching the observed 
   705‚Üí$\zch_{\pi^\pm} - \zch_{\pi^0} = 11.82 - 11.62 = 0.20\MeV^{1/2}$.
   706‚Üí
   707‚ÜíThe D-term effect is subleading to the F-term: 
   708‚Üí$\xi \sim (35\MeV)^2$ versus $f_\pi^2 \sim (92\MeV)^2$. It 
   709‚Üíoperates \emph{within} each supermultiplet entry, splitting charged 
   710‚Üífrom neutral mesons, while the F-term splits bosons from fermions 
   711‚Üíacross the supermultiplet.
   712‚Üí
   713‚ÜíWitten's inequality \cite{Witten1983} guarantees 
   714‚Üí$m_{\pi^\pm} > m_{\pi^0}$ in QCD with electromagnetism---the 
   715‚Üícharged pion is always heavier. In the SUSY framework this 
   716‚Üíbecomes the statement that the D-term contribution is positive 
   717‚Üífor $Q_{\text{em}} \neq 0$, consistent with the FI sign.
   718‚Üí
   719‚Üí\subsection{Mapping the three mechanisms to the three tuples}
   720‚Üí\label{subsec:mapping}
   721‚Üí
   722‚ÜíThe three-tuple structure suggests a layered breaking:
   723‚Üí
   724‚Üí\paragraph{Layer 1: F-term (chiral condensate, scale $\sim f_\pi$).}
   725‚ÜíThe chiral condensate $\qqbar \neq 0$ breaks both chiral symmetry 
   726‚Üíand the sBootstrap SUSY. This is the primary breaking, affecting 
   727‚Üíthe light sector. The seed triples $(0, s, c)$ and $(0, \pi, D_s)$ 
   728‚Üíare the pre-breaking configurations; the massless member is the flat 
   729‚Üídirection whose $F$-term is forced nonzero by confinement. After 
   730‚Üíbreaking:
   731‚Üí\begin{itemize}
   732‚Üí\item The zero member is lifted and acquires negative charge 
   733‚Üí($\zch < 0$), becoming the sign-flipped member of the full triple 
   734‚Üí($-s$, $-\pi$).
   735‚Üí\item The full triples $(-s,c,b)$ and $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ emerge, 
   736‚Üíincorporating the $b$-quark and $B$-meson sectors.
   737‚Üí\item The lepton triple $(e, \mu, \tau)$ is the fermionic shadow: 
   738‚Üíthe leptons acquire masses through the same condensate, with the 
   739‚Üímuon as pseudo-Goldstino.
   740‚Üí\end{itemize}
   741‚Üí
   742‚Üí\paragraph{Layer 2: D-term (electromagnetism, scale $\sim \alpha_{\text{em}} m_\rho$).}
   743‚ÜíThe $U(1)_{\text{em}}$ FI term generates electromagnetic mass 
   744‚Üísplittings within each multiplet. This is a perturbative correction 
   745‚Üíon top of the F-term breaking, affecting only charged particles. 
   746‚ÜíIt does not create new Koide triples but shifts existing ones by 
   747‚Üí$O(\alpha_{\text{em}})$.
   748‚Üí
   749‚Üí\paragraph{Layer 3: The heavy sector (scale $\sim v_0^{(cbt)}$).}
   750‚ÜíThe $(c,b,t)$ triple at $v_0^2 \approx 22.8\GeV$ operates far 
   751‚Üíabove the chiral scale. It has no seed (no massless member), no 
   752‚Üímeson shadow (top does not hadronize), and its breaking may be 
   753‚Üíconnected to a different sector of the theory---possibly the 
   754‚Üíelectroweak Higgs mechanism.
   755‚Üí
   756‚ÜíThis layered structure has a natural hierarchy:
   757‚Üí\begin{equation}
   758‚Üí\underbrace{f_\pi \sim 92\MeV}_{\text{F-term, chiral}} 
   759‚Üí\;\ll\; 
   760‚Üí\underbrace{\xi^{1/2} \sim 35\MeV}_{\text{D-term, EM}} 
   761‚Üí\;\ll\; 
   762‚Üí\underbrace{v_0^{(cbt)} \sim 151\MeV^{1/2}}_{\text{heavy sector}}\,,
   763‚Üí\end{equation}
   764‚Üíwhere the scales refer to different physical quantities (the 
   765‚Üícomparison is schematic). The F-term dominates the boson--fermion 
   766‚Üísplitting; the D-term provides electromagnetic fine structure; the 
   767‚Üíheavy sector is governed by its own dynamics.
   768‚Üí
   769‚Üí\subsection{The O'Raifeartaigh structure in SQCD}
   770‚Üí
   771‚ÜíIn Seiberg's SQCD with $N_f = N_c = 3$, the quantum constraint 
   772‚Üí\eqref{eq:seiberg} provides a natural setting for the 
   773‚ÜíO'Raifeartaigh mechanism. The constraint $\det M - B\bar{B} = \Lambda^6$ 
   774‚Üímeans the moduli space has a specific geometry: not all field directions 
   775‚Üíare flat. When quark masses are added via a tree-level superpotential 
   776‚Üí$W_{\text{tree}} = m_i M^i{}_i$ (sum over flavors), the $F$-terms become:
   777‚Üí\begin{equation}
   778‚ÜíF_{M^i{}_j} = m_i\,\delta^i_j + \Lambda^6\,(\text{constraint terms})\,.
   779‚Üí\end{equation}
   780‚Üí
   781‚ÜíFor the seed triple $(0, s, c)$, the ``$0$'' member corresponds to a 
   782‚Üídirection in the meson matrix where the tree-level mass vanishes---the 
   783‚Üíentry involving the lightest quark combination. In the exact chiral 
   784‚Üílimit ($m_u = m_d = 0$), this entry is truly massless, and the 
   785‚Üíconstraint \eqref{eq:seiberg} forces $\langle F\rangle \neq 0$ for 
   786‚Üíthat entry. This is the dynamical O'Raifeartaigh mechanism: 
   787‚Üíconfinement (encoded in $\Lambda^6$) plays the role of the parameter 
   788‚Üí$f$ in the superpotential, and the impossibility of setting all 
   789‚Üí$F$-terms to zero is a consequence of the strong dynamics rather 
   790‚Üíthan a choice of superpotential.
   791‚Üí
   792‚ÜíThe ISS mechanism (Intriligator--Seiberg--Shih \cite{ISS2006}) makes 
   793‚Üíthis precise: for $N_f$ slightly above $N_c$, SQCD has metastable 
   794‚ÜíSUSY-breaking vacua where the rank condition prevents all $F$-terms 
   795‚Üífrom vanishing. The sBootstrap case $N_f = N_c = 3$ is at the 
   796‚Üíboundary of the ISS window, suggesting that the SUSY breaking may 
   797‚Üíbe long-lived but not absolutely stable---consistent with the 
   798‚Üíapproximate nature of the Koide relations.
   799‚Üí
   800‚Üí\subsection{How the three supermultiplets join}
   801‚Üí
   802‚ÜíThe seed-to-bloom transition generates three separate Koide triples. 
   803‚ÜíBut the full sBootstrap requires these triples to be \emph{embedded 
   804‚Üíin a single $3\times 3$ meson matrix}. The entries of $M^i{}_j$ are:
   805‚Üí
   806‚Üí\begin{equation}
   807‚ÜíM = \begin{pmatrix}
   808‚Üíu\bar{u} & u\bar{d} & u\bar{s} \\
   809‚Üíd\bar{u} & d\bar{d} & d\bar{s} \\
   810‚Üís\bar{u} & s\bar{d} & s\bar{s}
   811‚Üí\end{pmatrix}
   812‚Üí\;\longleftrightarrow\;
   813‚Üí\begin{pmatrix}
   814‚Üí\pi^0/\eta & \pi^+ & K^+ \\
   815‚Üí\pi^- & \pi^0/\eta & K^0 \\
   816‚ÜíK^- & \bar{K}^0 & \eta'
   817‚Üí\end{pmatrix}.
   818‚Üí\end{equation}
   819‚Üí
   820‚ÜíWhen heavier quarks ($c$, $b$) are included, the matrix extends to 
   821‚Üí$5\times 5$ (or effectively $5\times 5$ minus the top row/column). 
   822‚ÜíThe Koide triples are specific \emph{slices} through this matrix:
   823‚Üí
   824‚Üí\begin{itemize}
   825‚Üí\item The lepton triple $(e, \mu, \tau)$ corresponds to the 
   826‚Üífermionic components of specific matrix entries.
   827‚Üí\item The meson seed $(0, \pi, D_s)$ involves the $(u\bar{d})$, 
   828‚Üí$(c\bar{s})$ entries and a zero.
   829‚Üí\item The full meson triple $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ involves 
   830‚Üí$(u\bar{d})$, $(c\bar{s})$, $(u\bar{b})$.
   831‚Üí\end{itemize}
   832‚Üí
   833‚ÜíThe joining of the three supermultiplets is the central unsolved 
   834‚Üíproblem: how does a single $5\times 5$ (or $3\times 3$) meson 
   835‚Üímatrix, subject to the Seiberg constraint, produce \emph{three} 
   836‚ÜíKoide triples with different $v_0$ values, each satisfying 
   837‚Üí$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$?
   838‚Üí
   839‚Üí\subsection{The energy balance as a fixed point}
   840‚Üí
   841‚ÜíOne possibility is that the energy-balance condition 
   842‚Üí$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ is a \emph{dynamical attractor}: 
   843‚Üíany set of three masses evolving under the SUSY-breaking dynamics 
   844‚Üíflows toward the Koide surface $Q = 2/3$. In the renormalization 
   845‚Üígroup language, the Koide condition would be a fixed point of the 
   846‚ÜíRG flow for the soft SUSY-breaking parameters.
   847‚Üí
   848‚ÜíThis is plausible if the breaking dynamics has an extremum (minimum 
   849‚Üíof effective potential, or maximum of entropy) at the equipartition 
   850‚Üípoint $\sigma = \mu$. The condition $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ 
   851‚Üísays the variance of the charges equals their squared mean---this 
   852‚Üíis the maximum-entropy distribution for three positive-definite 
   853‚Üíquantities with a given mean, subject to a log-normal prior. If 
   854‚Üíthe SUSY-breaking effective potential favors ``democratic'' 
   855‚Üídistributions of mass among the multiplet members, the Koide 
   856‚Üícondition would emerge as a thermodynamic consequence.
   857‚Üí
   858‚ÜíThe fact that the condition holds both for fundamental fermions 
   859‚Üí(leptons, quarks) and composite bosons (mesons) supports the 
   860‚Üíattractor interpretation: it is not specific to one sector but 
   861‚Üíoperates universally wherever three related masses coexist.
   862‚Üí
   863‚Üí\subsection{What the $(c,b,t)$ triple tells us}
   864‚Üí
   865‚ÜíThe $(c,b,t)$ triple ($Q = 0.6695$, $v_0^2 = 22.8\GeV$) has 
   866‚Üíneither a seed nor a meson shadow. It operates at a scale far 
   867‚Üíabove $\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$, suggesting its origin is different:
   868‚Üí
   869‚Üí\begin{enumerate}
   870‚Üí\item It may be governed by the electroweak Higgs mechanism rather 
   871‚Üíthan the chiral condensate. The top Yukawa $y_t \approx 1$ is 
   872‚Üí``natural'' in the sense that $m_t \approx v_{\text{EW}}/\sqrt{2}$; 
   873‚Üíthe Koide condition on $(c,b,t)$ would then constrain the Yukawa 
   874‚Üícoupling matrix.
   875‚Üí\item It may represent a Volkov--Akulov realization at the 
   876‚Üíelectroweak scale: if there is a second SUSY breaking at 
   877‚Üí$f' \sim v_{\text{EW}}$, the $(c,b,t)$ triple would be its 
   878‚Üíanalog of the $(\pi, \mu)$ system, but with $f'$ replacing $f_\pi$.
   879‚Üí\item Or it may be a purely kinematic accident---the quark masses 
   880‚Üíhappen to satisfy the Koide condition without a dynamical reason. 
   881‚ÜíThe 0.42\% precision makes this unlikely but not impossible.
   882‚Üí\end{enumerate}
   883‚Üí
   884‚ÜíIf interpretation (2) is correct, the sBootstrap has two breaking 
   885‚Üíscales:
   886‚Üí\begin{equation}
   887‚Üíf_{\text{chiral}} = f_\pi \approx 92\MeV\,, \qquad
   888‚Üíf_{\text{EW}} \sim v_{\text{EW}} \approx 246\GeV\,,
   889‚Üí\end{equation}
   890‚Üíand the full spectrum requires both. The light sector (leptons, 
   891‚Üílight mesons) is governed by $f_\pi$; the heavy sector (top, and 
   892‚Üíby extension charm and bottom through the Koide network) is 
   893‚Üígoverned by $v_{\text{EW}}$. The charm and bottom quarks, 
   894‚Üíappearing in both the $(0,s,c)/(-s,c,b)$ light-sector triples 
   895‚Üíand the $(c,b,t)$ heavy-sector triple, are the ``bridges'' 
   896‚Üíbetween the two scales.
   897‚Üí
   898‚Üí%======================================================================
   899‚Üí\section{The Electron Mass and the Lepton Seed}
   900‚Üí\label{sec:electron}
   901‚Üí%======================================================================
   902‚Üí
   903‚ÜíThe charged lepton triple $(e, \mu, \tau)$ is the ``almost-seed'': 
   904‚Üí$\zch_e = 0.715\MeV^{1/2}$ is small but nonzero. The electron's 
   905‚Üícharge is near zero because of cancellation in $\zch_e = z_0 + z_e$:
   906‚Üí\begin{equation}
   907‚Üív_0 = 17.72, \quad z_e = -17.00, \quad 
   908‚Üí\zch_e = 17.72 - 17.00 = 0.72\MeV^{1/2}\,.
   909‚Üí\end{equation}
   910‚ÜíA 96\% cancellation. The mass $m_e = 0.72^2 = 0.52\MeV$ is the 
   911‚Üísquare of something already small.
   912‚Üí
   913‚ÜíThe near-vanishing of $m_e$ is a structural property of the Koide 
   914‚Üíembedding, not fine-tuning: the energy balance allows perturbations 
   915‚Üías large as $z_0$. Every Koide triple has one member with 
   916‚Üí$|\zch|/v_0$ small:
   917‚Üí
   918‚Üí\begin{center}
   919‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lccl}
   920‚Üí\toprule
   921‚ÜíTriple & Lightest & $|\zch|/v_0$ & Character \\
   922‚Üí\midrule
   923‚Üí$(e, \mu, \tau)$ & $e$ & 0.040 & Almost-seed \\
   924‚Üí$(-\pi, D_s, B)$ & $\pi$ (neg.) & 0.337 & Full triple \\
   925‚Üí$(c, b, t)$ & $c$ & 0.236 & Full triple \\
   926‚Üí$(-s, c, b)$ & $s$ (neg.) & 0.320 & Full triple \\
   927‚Üí\bottomrule
   928‚Üí\end{tabular}
   929‚Üí\end{center}
   930‚Üí
   931‚ÜíThe electron is by far the most extreme case, consistent with the 
   932‚Üílepton triple being nearest to its seed.
   933‚Üí
   934‚Üí%======================================================================
   935‚Üí\section{Symmetry Restoration}
   936‚Üí\label{sec:restoration}
   937‚Üí%======================================================================
   938‚Üí
   939‚ÜíThe broken spectrum reassembles as symmetry-breaking effects are 
   940‚Üíremoved:
   941‚Üí
   942‚Üí\begin{enumerate}
   943‚Üí\item \textbf{Turn off QED:} Electromagnetic splittings vanish 
   944‚Üí($\pi^\pm = \pi^0$, $K^\pm = K^0$). D-term effects disappear.
   945‚Üí\item \textbf{Set $m_u = m_d$:} Exact isospin.
   946‚Üí\item \textbf{Set $m_s = m_d$:} $SU(3)_V$ restored, 
   947‚Üí$\pi = K = \eta$. Koide splitting $v_1 \to 0$; lepton partners 
   948‚Üídegenerate.
   949‚Üí\item \textbf{Send $m_q \to 0$:} Chiral limit, all meson charges 
   950‚Üí$\to 0$. If sBootstrap SUSY restores, all light lepton masses 
   951‚Üívanish too. This is the seed configuration: $(0, 0, 0)$.
   952‚Üí\end{enumerate}
   953‚Üí
   954‚ÜíThe seed triples are the ``penultimate'' stage: one member has 
   955‚Üíreached zero, but the other two retain the $2 - \sqrt{3}$ ratio. 
   956‚ÜíFull triples are the final broken state. The lepton triple, 
   957‚Üítrapped between seed and bloom, reveals the hierarchy: 
   958‚Üíchiral breaking $\to$ seed $\to$ bloom $\to$ full spectrum.
   959‚Üí
   960‚ÜíThe direction of the flow also matters: approaching the chiral 
   961‚Üílimit, the pion charge rises from $\zch = -11.81$ toward zero 
   962‚Üífrom below. It crosses zero exactly in the chiral limit. This 
   963‚Üímeans the pion was ``born negative''---its charge sign is set by 
   964‚Üíthe direction of chiral symmetry breaking, and the seed 
   965‚Üí$(0, \pi, D_s)$ is the configuration at the moment of crossing.
   966‚Üí
   967‚Üí%======================================================================
   968‚Üí\section{Nature Abhors Chirality}
   969‚Üí\label{sec:chirality}
   970‚Üí%======================================================================
   971‚Üí
   972‚ÜíA pattern across the Standard Model: every chiral structure is 
   973‚Üíeliminated in the infrared.
   974‚Üí
   975‚Üí\begin{center}
   976‚Üí\begin{tabular}{lcl}
   977‚Üí\toprule
   978‚ÜíUV structure & IR survivor & Mechanism \\
   979‚Üí\midrule
   980‚Üí$SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ & $U(1)_{\text{em}}$ & Higgs \\
   981‚Üí$SU(N_f)_L \times SU(N_f)_R$ & $SU(N_f)_V$ & $\qqbar$ condensate \\
   982‚Üí$U(1)_A$ & nothing & Anomaly \\
   983‚Üí$\bar{\theta}$-vacuum & $|\bar{\theta}| < 10^{-10}$ & Unknown \\
   984‚ÜíChiral color? & $SU(3)_C$ & Axigluon mass \\
   985‚Üí\bottomrule
   986‚Üí\end{tabular}
   987‚Üí\end{center}
   988‚Üí
   989‚ÜíThe sBootstrap adds: superspace chirality (boson vs.\ fermion 
   990‚Üídistinction within a multiplet) is broken by the same condensate 
   991‚Üíthat breaks flavor chirality, with $f_{\text{SUSY}} = f_\pi$. The 
   992‚Üíenergy-balance condition $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ may be 
   993‚Üírelated: the breaking is ``as large as the background 
   994‚Üíallows''---maximal, not perturbative.
   995‚Üí
   996‚Üí%======================================================================
   997‚Üí\section{Open Problems}
   998‚Üí\label{sec:open}
   999‚Üí%======================================================================
  1000‚Üí
  1001‚Üí\subsection{The diquark statistics problem}
  1002‚ÜíThe sBootstrap requires antisymmetric $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$ 
  1003‚Üídiquarks; Pauli statistics for spin-0, $L=0$, color $\bar{3}$ 
  1004‚Üídiquarks requires symmetric flavor. Extra dynamical input---$L=1$ 
  1005‚Üíplus spin-1, instantons, or the $SO(32)$ embedding---is needed.
  1006‚Üí
  1007‚Üí\subsection{What determines the Koide phase?}
  1008‚ÜíThe energy balance constrains but does not uniquely fix $\delta$. 
  1009‚ÜíThe value $\delta_\ell \approx 2/9$ for leptons must come from 
  1010‚Üídynamics. Is it a fixed point?
  1011‚Üí
  1012‚Üí\subsection{Connecting the three tuples}
  1013‚ÜíThe light sector (seed $\to$ bloom, scale $\sim \Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$) 
  1014‚Üíand heavy sector ($c,b,t$, scale $\sim v_{\text{EW}}$) must connect. 
  1015‚ÜíThe charm and bottom quarks appear in both, providing the links.
  1016‚Üí
  1017‚Üí\subsection{The baryon sector}
  1018‚ÜíIn Seiberg's SQCD with $N_f = N_c = 3$, baryons are part of the 
  1019‚Üílow-energy spectrum. Their SUSY partners are ``baryonic fermions'' 
  1020‚Üíwith no known candidates among observed particles.
  1021‚Üí
  1022‚Üí\subsection{The strong CP problem}
  1023‚ÜíThe principle ``Nature abhors chirality'' predicts $\bar{\theta} = 0$ 
  1024‚Üíbut provides no mechanism.
  1025‚Üí
  1026‚Üí\subsection{The kaon--muon puzzle}
  1027‚ÜíThe SUSY splitting $m_K^2 - m_\mu^2 \approx (482\MeV)^2$ is much 
  1028‚Üílarger than $f_K^2 \approx (110\MeV)^2$, showing that the breaking 
  1029‚Üíis strongly flavor-dependent.
  1030‚Üí
  1031‚Üí%======================================================================
  1032‚Üí\section{Conclusions}
  1033‚Üí\label{sec:conclusions}
  1034‚Üí%======================================================================
  1035‚Üí
  1036‚ÜíThree principal results:
  1037‚Üí
  1038‚Üí\paragraph{1.\ The Koide formula is energy equipartition.}
  1039‚Üí$Q = 2/3$ is $\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$: perturbation energies 
  1040‚Üímatch the vacuum energy. The signed-charge framework resolves the 
  1041‚Üíelectron mass puzzle (near-cancellation, not fine-tuning) and reveals 
  1042‚Üíthat mesons satisfy Koide with $(-\pi, D_s, B)$ to $0.1\%$.
  1043‚Üí
  1044‚Üí\paragraph{2.\ Seed triples set the stage for SUSY breaking.}
  1045‚ÜíThe quark seed $(0,s,c)$ and meson seed $(0,\pi,D_s)$ share the 
  1046‚Üíuniversal ratio $2 - \sqrt{3}$ and operate at $v_0^2 \sim 
  1047‚Üí230$--$350\MeV$, close to the lepton triple's $v_0^2 = 314\MeV$. 
  1048‚ÜíThe massless member is the necessary condition for F-term 
  1049‚Üí(O'Raifeartaigh) SUSY breaking. Upon breaking, seeds bloom into 
  1050‚Üífull triples $(-s,c,b)$ and $(-\pi,D_s,B)$. The lepton triple 
  1051‚Üíis the ``almost-seed'' that has barely bloomed.
  1052‚Üí
  1053‚Üí\paragraph{3.\ Three tuples require three breaking structures.}
  1054‚ÜíF-term breaking (via the massless seed member) generates the light 
  1055‚Üísector. D-term breaking ($U(1)_{\text{em}}$ FI parameter) provides 
  1056‚Üíelectromagnetic splittings. Volkov--Akulov nonlinear realization 
  1057‚Üísets the overall scale $f_{\text{VA}} = f_\pi$. The heavy triple 
  1058‚Üí$(c,b,t)$ stands apart: no seed, no meson shadow, scale far above 
  1059‚Üí$\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$, possibly connected to electroweak breaking.
  1060‚Üí
  1061‚ÜíThe diquark sector, required to complete the SUSY multiplet count, 
  1062‚Üímust fill the antisymmetric $\overline{\mathbf{10}}$ of $SU(5)_f$ 
  1063‚Üídespite the apparent conflict with Fermi statistics---a problem 
  1064‚Üíthat may ultimately require the $SO(32)$ embedding for its 
  1065‚Üíresolution.
  1066‚Üí
  1067‚ÜíThe organizing principle is that each sector of the spectrum begins 
  1068‚Üíwith a massless member (a seed), which is the necessary condition 
  1069‚Üífor spontaneous SUSY breaking. The breaking lifts the zero, 
  1070‚Üígenerates the full triple, and the energy-balance condition 
  1071‚Üí$\langle z_k^2\rangle = z_0^2$ is preserved as a fixed point 
  1072‚Üíof the dynamics. The entire charged fermion and pseudoscalar meson 
  1073‚Üíspectrum is then determined by a small number of parameters: the 
  1074‚Üívacuum charges $v_0$ of the three tuples, the Koide phases 
  1075‚Üí$\delta$ that control the bloom, and the SUSY-breaking scale 
  1076‚Üí$f_\pi$ that separates bosons from fermions.
